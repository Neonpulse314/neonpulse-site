On October 16, 1846, a dentist named William Morton put a man to sleep with ether vapor in a lecture hall at Massachusetts General Hospital. A surgeon removed a tumor from the man's neck. The man felt nothing.

The audience, expecting screams, heard silence.

Within months, ether was being used in surgeries across Europe. The age of anesthesia had begun. In the 175 years since, we've administered general anesthesia billions of times — for heart surgeries, brain surgeries, appendectomies, hip replacements, C-sections. It's one of the foundational technologies of modern medicine.

And we still don't have a complete explanation for why it works.

---

## What It Actually Does

General anesthesia isn't just unconsciousness. It does three distinct things simultaneously:

1. **Amnesia** — you don't form memories of the procedure
2. **Unconsciousness** — you're not aware of what's happening
3. **Immobility / analgesia** — you don't move or feel pain

These turn out to be separate phenomena. Researchers discovered this when they started looking at where different effects originate. Immobility during surgery is controlled largely by the spinal cord. Unconsciousness is controlled by the brain. Amnesia involves different circuits again. That means "general anesthesia" isn't one thing — it's at least three things happening simultaneously, possibly through different mechanisms, achieved by the same drug.

This should make you immediately suspicious of any simple explanation.

---

## The First Theory: It's the Fat

In 1899 and 1901, two scientists independently noticed something strange: the more soluble a chemical was in fat (lipids), the more potent it was as an anesthetic. A compound that dissolved easily in fat would knock you out at a lower dose than one that didn't. This correlation — now called the Meyer-Overton rule — held across a stunning range of molecules, from noble gases like xenon to complex organic compounds.

The interpretation seemed obvious: anesthetics work by dissolving into the fatty membranes of nerve cells and disrupting how they function. Mess with the membrane, disrupt the neuron, no more consciousness.

This explanation dominated for most of the 20th century.

It was wrong — or at least incomplete.

The problem: the Meyer-Overton correlation tells you *who* might work as an anesthetic but says almost nothing about *how* they work. Fat solubility is a correlation, not a mechanism. And when researchers looked closely at what anesthetics actually did to cell membranes at clinical doses, the disruption was barely measurable. A degree of temperature change causes more membrane disruption than a surgical dose of anesthetic.

---

## The Second Theory: It's the Proteins

In the 1980s and 90s, a biochemist named Nick Franks and a biophysicist named William Lieb at Imperial College London started pulling apart the lipid theory. Their experiments showed that anesthetics didn't need lipids at all — they could directly inhibit certain enzymes in solution. The target wasn't the membrane. The target was specific proteins.

This shifted the field. Researchers started identifying which proteins: certain ion channels, particularly GABA-A receptors (which quiet neural activity), NMDA receptors (which excite it), glycine receptors, and two-pore-domain potassium channels. Anesthetics seem to enhance the inhibitory ones and block the excitatory ones, producing the net effect of dramatically reduced neural activity.

This is the current mainstream explanation. It's more accurate than the lipid theory. It has led to better drugs and more targeted approaches.

But it still doesn't answer the question.

Which proteins, exactly? There are hundreds of ion channels in the human brain. Different anesthetics bind to different ones. Why do they all produce the same unconsciousness? What is the common downstream effect? How does messing with ion channels remove *awareness* rather than just quieting neurons? We don't know. We don't have a clear mechanistic path from "GABA-A receptor activation" to "this person is no longer conscious."

---

## The Consciousness Problem

This is where anesthesia collides with something much deeper.

To explain *why* anesthesia removes consciousness, you need to know what consciousness is. You need to know what neural activity corresponds to "being aware" versus "processing without awareness." You need to understand the difference between a brain that's running but experiencing nothing, and a brain that's running and experiencing everything.

We don't have that understanding. The hard problem of consciousness — why there is subjective experience at all — remains unsolved. And anesthesia sits directly on top of it.

EEG studies of anesthetized patients show that some neural activity continues. Certain regions of the brain remain active. Sensory processing doesn't fully stop. But something is gone. The lights go out in some functional sense, and no one has a precise description of which lights those are or why turning them off matters.

There's a phenomenon called "anesthesia awareness" — roughly 1 in 1,000 patients regains some degree of consciousness during surgery. They may be paralyzed, may feel no pain, but are in some sense *present*. Studying these cases has taught us that anesthetic depth and awareness are poorly correlated with any single measurable parameter. You can have a patient whose EEG looks deep under anesthesia who is awake, and a patient whose EEG looks light who is not.

---

## The Quantum Theory (and Why It's Still Out There)

In the 1990s, mathematician Roger Penrose and anesthesiologist Stuart Hameroff proposed something radical: consciousness arises from quantum processes in structures inside neurons called microtubules. And anesthetics work by disrupting those quantum processes.

Most neuroscientists are skeptical. The brain is warm, wet, and noisy — a terrible environment for quantum coherence. The Penrose-Hameroff hypothesis is speculative and remains outside the mainstream.

But here's why it hasn't been fully dismissed: anesthetics do interact with microtubules. In cell biology experiments, they disrupt microtubule dynamics at clinically relevant concentrations. That's a real effect. Whether it has anything to do with consciousness, or is just a side effect of lipid-adjacent chemistry, is unknown.

The hypothesis lives in the uncertainty. We don't know enough about how consciousness works to rule it out.

---

## What We're Left With

General anesthesia works. Billions of data points confirm it works. We can adjust doses, combine agents, reverse effects, monitor depth. The clinical practice has become sophisticated.

But our mechanistic understanding is, in the honest assessment of researchers in the field, incomplete. We have strong correlations. We have plausible targets. We don't have a complete causal chain from molecule to missing consciousness.

In 2019, a major review in *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* put it plainly: the precise molecular, cellular, and circuit mechanisms by which anesthetics produce unconsciousness remain poorly understood, and will likely remain so until we have a much better theory of what consciousness is.

You've probably been under. Millions of people are put under every year. The person who did it knew how much to give you, how to keep you safe, how to bring you back.

They didn't know exactly where you went.
