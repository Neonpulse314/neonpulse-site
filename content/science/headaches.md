The brain has no pain receptors.

This is one of those facts that sounds made up until you look it up. Surgeons can cut into brain tissue while the patient is awake, and the patient feels nothing from the brain itself. The brain cannot hurt. It has no nociceptors — no pain-sensing nerve endings of its own.

And yet headaches are one of the most common medical complaints on earth. Nearly half of the world's adult population has had at least one in the past year. Migraines alone disable more people globally than epilepsy, multiple sclerosis, and Parkinson's combined.

So if the brain can't feel pain, where is the pain coming from?

## The Pain Is in the Packaging

The brain is surrounded by layers of tissue — membranes called the **meninges**, which wrap the brain and spinal cord. These membranes are richly supplied with pain-sensitive nerve fibers. So are the blood vessels running through and around the brain, and the muscles of the scalp and neck.

The dominant player in most headaches is the **trigeminal nerve** — the main sensory nerve of the head and face. When something irritates or inflames the pain-sensitive structures around the brain, the trigeminal nerve is often the one carrying the signal.

Different things irritate those structures. That's why different headaches feel so different.

## Tension Headaches

About 75 percent of all headaches are tension headaches — the dull, pressing, "band around the head" kind.

For decades, they were assumed to be caused by muscle tension: tighten the muscles in your neck and scalp enough, and you'd feel it as pain. Simple mechanics. The name stuck.

The mechanism, it turns out, is more complicated.

What research now suggests is that **central sensitization** plays a major role. The trigeminal nerve and the brain's pain-processing centers can become sensitized — their threshold for firing drops, and signals that would normally be background noise get amplified into pain. Stress, sleep deprivation, eye strain, and dehydration all appear to lower that threshold. But the precise chain of events from "I've been staring at this screen for six hours" to "there is a band of pressure around my head" is still being mapped.

What we do know: muscle tension alone doesn't explain it. Many people with severe muscle tension get no headache. Many people with minimal muscle tension get significant ones. The sensitization of the pain network seems to matter more than the muscles themselves.

## Migraines

Migraines are a different animal. One-sided, throbbing, moderate to severe, often accompanied by nausea and an intense need to be somewhere dark and quiet. About 12 to 15 percent of the population gets them. For some people, they arrive with an **aura** — visual disturbances, tingling, or difficulty speaking in the minutes before the pain hits.

The mechanism here is better understood, and it involves one of the more remarkable phenomena in neuroscience.

In the 1940s, a Brazilian physiologist named Aristides Leão discovered that he could trigger a slow wave of electrical silence that moved across the cortex — neurons firing in a spreading cascade, then going quiet. He called it **cortical spreading depression** (CSD). The wave moves at about three to five millimeters per minute.

In people with migraines, CSD appears to be the trigger for the pain cascade. As the wave moves across the cortex, it causes the release of a molecule called **CGRP** (calcitonin gene-related peptide) from nerve endings in the meninges. CGRP activates trigeminal nerve fibers, which start sending pain signals. Blood vessels in the meninges dilate and become inflamed. The pain arrives.

The aura is the CSD made visible. When the wave moves across the visual cortex, it disrupts visual processing — which is why many people see a moving arc of shimmering light before the headache begins. As the wave passes, vision normalizes. The headache follows.

Why only some people experience migraines has a significant genetic component. Several genes have been identified that lower the cortical threshold for CSD — meaning the wave fires more easily. But genetics isn't destiny: the same person can go months without a migraine and then have several in a week. Triggers — stress, hormonal shifts, sleep disruption, certain foods, changes in barometric pressure — appear to lower the threshold further. The migraine happens when accumulated triggers push a sensitized brain past its threshold.

Why three times as many women get migraines as men is almost certainly related to estrogen, which affects trigeminal sensitivity. Migraines often cluster around hormonal fluctuations — the week before menstruation is a particularly common window. The mechanism linking estrogen to trigeminal sensitization is an active research area.

## Cluster Headaches

Cluster headaches are rare — about one in a thousand people — and widely described as the most severe pain a human being can experience. They've been called "suicide headaches." The pain is centered around one eye, comes on abruptly, and can last fifteen minutes to three hours. During a cluster period, attacks can happen at the same time every day for weeks.

That clockwork timing is a clue. Research has consistently pointed to the **hypothalamus** — the brain's master clock — as a key player. During a cluster headache, the posterior hypothalamus activates. Why it activates the trigeminal pathway in this precise, periodic way isn't understood. Treatment is difficult, and the underlying biology remains murky enough that cluster headache research is sometimes called one of the great neglected frontiers in neuroscience.

## The Caffeine Paradox

One thing about headaches that confuses most people: caffeine both causes and cures them.

A cup of coffee can relieve a headache — this is real enough that caffeine is added to many over-the-counter pain medications. But regular caffeine users who miss their morning dose often develop a headache by midday.

The mechanism runs through **adenosine**, a molecule that accumulates in the brain during wakefulness and promotes sleep. Adenosine also dilates blood vessels in the brain. Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors — which means it constricts blood vessels, reduces adenosine signaling, and when you're already in pain from dilated, inflamed meningeal vessels, this helps.

But consistent caffeine use causes the brain to upregulate adenosine receptors — to build more of them to compensate for the blockade. When caffeine is withdrawn, all those receptors are suddenly unblocked, adenosine floods them, blood vessels dilate hard, and you get a headache. The drug that relieves the headache also causes dependence on itself for headache prevention.

## What We Don't Know

The biggest open question in headache research is probably this: what actually determines who has a sensitive enough pain threshold to develop chronic or severe headaches, and why does that threshold change over time?

Migraines often begin in adolescence or early adulthood and diminish after menopause. Some people have one migraine in their life; others have them weekly for decades. The transformation from episodic to chronic migraine — where attacks go from occasional to more than fifteen days per month — appears to involve structural and functional changes in the brain, but it's not clear whether those changes cause the chronicity or result from it.

There's also the **medication overuse headache**, one of the stranger phenomena in medicine: if you take pain medication (including ibuprofen, aspirin, and triptans) for headaches too frequently, you can cause headaches. The pain-relieving medication, used too often, sensitizes the pain system until it starts firing on its own. Overuse headache accounts for a significant fraction of chronic daily headache cases worldwide, but the exact mechanism — why the brain responds to analgesic overuse by generating pain — remains poorly understood.

The brain that cannot feel pain turns out to have an extraordinarily sophisticated system for deciding what does.
