You look up at a bright sky and notice them: translucent threads, dots, or rings drifting slowly across your field of view. Try to look directly at one and it slides away. Look back to center and it drifts back. You've seen them your whole life and probably assumed everyone had them and no one talked about them.

They're called **floaters**, and you're literally seeing inside your own eye.

## What's Actually in There

Your eyeball is filled with a gel called the **vitreous humor** — roughly 99% water, the rest a matrix of collagen fibers held in shape by hyaluronic acid molecules. This gel gives the eye its round structure and helps maintain optical clarity.

The floaters you see aren't on your retina. They're in this gel. When collagen fibers clump together, or when cells migrate into the vitreous, they cast small shadows on the retina behind them. What you perceive as a floating squiggle or blob is actually the shadow of something suspended in the jelly inside your eye.

This is an **entoptic phenomenon** — you're perceiving something that exists inside your own visual system, not out in the world.

## Why They Move the Way They Do

The gel isn't solid. When you move your eye quickly, the vitreous lags slightly behind, like water sloshing in a bowl. The clumps move with the gel, then slowly drift due to gravity when your eye stops. That's why you can never quite catch a floater by looking directly at it: your eye moves, the vitreous lags, and the shadow shifts away from where you looked.

The brain usually suppresses awareness of floaters through **neural adaptation** — the same mechanism that makes you stop noticing background hum or the weight of your clothes. This is why you may go months without noticing them, then suddenly see them on a bright day against a plain background, and can't unsee them for a while.

## The Age Factor: What Happens as the Eye Changes

Young vitreous is a firm, clear gel. As you age, it slowly **liquefies** — the collagen fibers aggregate and the hyaluronic acid loses its ability to keep them dispersed. By age 70, more than half the vitreous has turned liquid. This is normal.

The more dramatic event is **posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)**. The vitreous is attached to the retina at several points, most firmly at the optic disc at the back of the eye. As the gel liquefies and shrinks, it eventually peels away from the retina. This usually happens between ages 50 and 70, though it can happen earlier.

When the vitreous peels away from the optic disc, the ring of attachment tissue sometimes remains as a clump in the gel — a large circular floater called a **Weiss ring**. This is one of the most common causes of a new, sudden floater in middle-aged adults. It's also often accompanied by flashes of light: the detaching vitreous tugs on the retina, mechanically stimulating photoreceptors, which fire and register as light even though there's no light.

About 75% of people experience PVD by their late 70s. For most, the floaters from PVD become less noticeable over time as the brain adapts — and as the clumps slowly settle below the optical axis.

## The Warning Signs

Most floaters are benign. But certain presentations require urgent attention:

- A **sudden shower of new floaters** (dozens at once)
- Floaters accompanied by **flashes of light** (especially in the peripheral field)
- A **dark curtain or shadow** creeping across part of your visual field

These can indicate a **retinal tear or detachment** — the vitreous has pulled too hard on a weak spot in the retina and ripped it. If the retina detaches from its blood supply, the photoreceptors begin to die within hours. This is a medical emergency: same-day treatment preserves vision; delayed treatment can mean permanent blindness in that eye.

About 10–15% of eyes that develop acute PVD also develop a retinal tear. Why some eyes tear and others don't isn't fully understood — it appears to depend on how strongly the vitreous was adhered to the retina at specific points, which varies significantly between individuals.

## Why Your Brain Can't Just Ignore Them

You might wonder: if the brain filters out stable background signals, why can't it just filter out floaters permanently?

The problem is that floaters **move**. The visual system is exquisitely tuned to motion — movement detection was one of the earliest evolutionary pressures on the eye, because predators move. A slowly drifting object in the visual field triggers motion detectors, and the brain treats motion as potentially meaningful. This makes floaters harder to suppress than truly static signals.

Some people develop **myodesopsia** — clinically significant floater distress. They describe floaters as constantly distracting, affecting reading and driving. For them, the neural adaptation never fully kicks in. Research into why some people habituate and others don't is thin; it may relate to anxiety sensitivity, visual processing differences, or simply the number and opacity of floaters.

## The Treatment Problem

There's no universally satisfactory treatment for bothersome floaters.

**Vitrectomy** — surgically removing and replacing the vitreous — eliminates floaters almost completely, but it's a serious surgery with real risks: cataracts (near-universal within years), infection, and an increased risk of retinal detachment. For most people, the risk-benefit calculation doesn't favor it.

**Laser vitreolysis** (also called YAG laser treatment) uses short pulses of laser energy to vaporize floater clumps into smaller, less noticeable particles. Its success rate is inconsistent — it works well for large, discrete floaters like Weiss rings, but poorly for diffuse or peripheral floaters. Studies show modest improvements in symptoms, with low complication rates. Many ophthalmologists remain cautious.

The honest answer for most people with floaters is: you learn to live with them.

## The Open Question

Floaters are one of the most common visual complaints in ophthalmology, and yet the field doesn't have a clear answer for why some people find them devastating and others never think about them. Neural adaptation rates, the specific geometry of floater placement, individual differences in vitreous composition — these are poorly mapped.

More fundamentally: why do only some PVD events cause retinal tears? The adhesion pattern between vitreous and retina varies in ways that are difficult to image before the fact. Better predictive tools for who's at high risk of tearing — before the tear happens — remain an open problem.

For now, those drifting shapes are a reminder that the eye is a living, changing structure. What you see through it depends not just on what's in front of you, but on the slow biological shifts happening inside.
