In 1998, two teams of astronomers set out to measure how fast the universe was slowing down.

That wasn't a typo. They expected it to be slowing down. Gravity pulls everything toward everything else. The Big Bang sent matter flying outward. Gravity should be putting on the brakes. The question was just how hard.

Both teams were measuring distant supernovae — exploding stars that always burn at the same brightness, which makes them useful as cosmic rulers. If you know how bright something is, and you can see how dim it appears from here, you can calculate exactly how far away it is. Measure enough of them, plot how the universe has expanded over time, and you get the deceleration rate.

What they found instead broke physics.

The universe isn't slowing down. It's speeding up.

---

## What Acceleration Means

This requires a moment to sit with.

Galaxies are already moving away from each other at enormous speeds. The ones closest to us are receding at thousands of kilometers per second. Galaxies halfway across the observable universe are receding faster than light — not because anything is moving through space faster than light, but because space itself is stretching.

In a universe governed by gravity alone, that expansion should be slowing. Every galaxy's gravity is pulling on every other galaxy. Friction between matter. Eventually, expansion decelerates.

But in 1998, Saul Perlmutter and Brian Schmidt's teams found the opposite: the further away a supernova was — meaning the older it was — the slower the universe was expanding at that point in time. The universe was *accelerating*. It's moving apart faster now than it was a billion years ago, and faster still than it was two billion years ago.

They won the Nobel Prize in 2011. The discovery didn't solve anything. It opened a hole.

Something is pushing the universe apart. Something that doesn't cluster into galaxies, doesn't emit light, doesn't interact with matter — something that acts as a kind of anti-gravity, a repulsion that fills empty space itself. We named it dark energy. Giving it a name was the only thing we were able to do.

---

## How Much of Everything Is It

Here's the accounting problem.

Normal matter — stars, planets, gas, dust, you, everything you can see — makes up about 5% of the universe's total energy content. Dark matter, the invisible stuff holding galaxies together, makes up about 27%. Dark energy makes up the remaining 68%.

We understand 5% of the universe.

The other 95% is mystery, split between two completely different kinds of unknown. Dark matter is a missing particle — something that has mass but doesn't interact with light. Dark energy is something stranger: it fills empty space, it doesn't dilute as the universe expands, and it pushes outward with increasing strength over time.

These aren't small gaps in our knowledge. They are the knowledge problem. The observable universe contains roughly two trillion galaxies. Across all of them, 95% of what makes them up is unknown.

## The Cosmological Constant

Einstein got here first, in the wrong direction.

In 1917, before anyone knew galaxies existed, Einstein applied general relativity to the universe as a whole. He got a universe that had to be either expanding or contracting — it couldn't hold still. That seemed wrong; the universe looked static. So he added a term to his equations: a cosmological constant, a kind of built-in repulsion, just strong enough to balance gravity and keep the universe from collapsing.

Then Hubble discovered other galaxies. Then found they were moving away from us. The universe was expanding. Einstein pulled the cosmological constant out of his equations and called it his greatest mistake.

In 1998, it came back.

The cosmological constant is the leading candidate for dark energy. The idea: empty space has a baseline energy. It's not nothing — the quantum vacuum seethes with particles flickering in and out of existence. That energy has a repulsive gravitational effect. Einstein's constant describes how strong it is.

The problem: when you use quantum field theory to calculate what the energy of empty space should be, you get a number. And when you compare that number to the energy of empty space we've actually observed — the one consistent with the acceleration — the theoretical prediction is wrong by a factor of 10 to the power of 120.

That's not a small error. That's not a rounding mistake. 10^120 is a number with 120 zeros. The ratio of the age of the universe in seconds to the Planck time is only 10^61. The number of atoms in the observable universe is around 10^80. The discrepancy between what quantum field theory predicts and what we observe is a factor larger than any of those.

Physicists call this the cosmological constant problem. It may be the worst mismatch between theory and observation in the history of science.

## What We've Tried

**Quintessence** — maybe dark energy isn't a constant. Maybe it's a dynamic field, something that changes over time and across space, like a fluid that fills the universe and slowly evolves. Different versions of quintessence predict different expansion histories. So far, every observation is consistent with a simple constant — which doesn't rule quintessence out, but doesn't require it either.

**Modified gravity** — maybe we've got gravity wrong at cosmic scales, the same way Newtonian gravity needed correcting for strong fields. Some modifications to general relativity produce accelerating expansion without needing dark energy at all. None have managed to explain everything else gravity explains without breaking something.

**Anthropic selection** — maybe the cosmological constant isn't calculated, it's selected. If there are vast numbers of universes with different constants, we'd only exist in ones where the constant is small enough to allow matter to form. We're here, so we observe a small constant. This is logically coherent and scientifically useless — it explains why we observe what we observe by pointing out that we had to observe something. It doesn't predict anything.

**We've got the wrong picture entirely** — the standard cosmological model, called Lambda-CDM, assumes the universe is smooth and homogeneous at large scales. Maybe it isn't. Maybe we're measuring the expansion rate in a region that's locally unusual. Maybe the problem isn't dark energy — it's our assumptions about what's average.

None of these work cleanly. None are ruled out completely.

---

## The Tension That Keeps Physicists Up at Night

There's a second problem layered inside the first one.

The Hubble constant — the number describing how fast the universe is expanding — can be measured two ways. You can measure it directly using nearby objects like Cepheid variable stars and supernovae. Or you can infer it from the cosmic microwave background, the afterglow of the Big Bang, and calculate what the expansion rate should be today.

These two methods disagree. By about 9%. In cosmology terms, that's enormous.

Both measurements keep getting more precise. Both keep disagreeing. Either one method is systematically wrong — which would require errors that nobody has found — or the standard model of cosmology is missing something. Something changed in the universe's expansion history that we haven't accounted for.

Maybe dark energy isn't constant. Maybe there's a new particle. Maybe gravity works differently at some scale we haven't tested. Nobody knows. This tension has a name — the Hubble tension — and it's been sharpening for a decade.

---

## The Scale of What We Don't Know

If dark energy behaves like Einstein's cosmological constant — if it's truly constant — then the universe has a fate: eternal acceleration. Galaxies currently in our observable universe will eventually recede faster than light, crossing an event horizon we can't see past. The local group — our galaxy, Andromeda, a few dozen neighbors — will eventually be all that's gravitationally bound. Everything else vanishes beyond the horizon.

In a hundred billion years or so, the night sky will be dark. No other galaxies visible. No way to even know the Big Bang happened. Future civilizations, if they exist, will have no evidence for an expanding universe, no microwave background, no distant galaxies. They'll think they live in a static island in an infinite void.

We're lucky to be here now, when the universe is still legible.

But that's only if the cosmological constant stays constant. If dark energy is dynamic — if quintessence is right, if the equation of state evolves — the fate could be different. More expansion. Or less. Or eventually collapse.

We're 95% ignorant about the thing that determines how the universe ends.

---

The two competing teams in 1998 expected a boring measurement — confirming the expected slowdown, fitting the data into existing models. What they found instead was that 68% of everything is something physics can't explain, doing something nobody predicted, at a strength that is off from our best theoretical calculation by the largest factor ever recorded in science.

They called it dark energy because they needed to call it something. The name hasn't gotten more informative since.
