In 1957, a PhD student named Hugh Everett III submitted a thesis to Princeton that his advisor initially called "not acceptable in its present form." Everett argued that quantum mechanics, taken seriously, implied something almost unthinkable: every time a quantum event occurs — every time a particle could go left or right, every time a photon hits a detector — the universe splits. Both outcomes happen. They just happen in different branches of reality that can never interact.

His thesis was eventually accepted. His advisor, John Wheeler, quietly edited out the most radical implications. Everett left physics, went to work for the Defense Department, and died at 51. His ideas were largely forgotten for decades.

Today, his Many-Worlds interpretation is one of the most widely accepted in quantum mechanics. A 2011 survey found it was the preferred interpretation among a significant portion of physicists. The universe, according to a serious fraction of the field, is constantly branching into copies of itself.

And that's just one route to the multiverse.

## Three Independent Paths to the Same Conclusion

The strange thing about the multiverse is that it keeps showing up uninvited. You don't need to start with "what if there are other universes?" — you start with completely unrelated problems in physics, work through the math, and find yourself arriving at the same destination.

**Route 1: Inflation**

In 1981, MIT physicist Alan Guth proposed a solution to several puzzling problems about the early universe — why the cosmic microwave background is so uniform, why the universe looks the same in all directions, why it has almost exactly the right density to avoid collapsing or expanding too fast.

His solution: in the first fraction of a second after the Big Bang, the universe underwent a period of exponential expansion, doubling in size perhaps 100 times in roughly 10^-32 seconds. This would smooth everything out and explain the uniformity we observe.

Inflation almost immediately became the standard model of cosmology. It explains the data.

The problem came next. In 1983, Andrei Linde showed that in most versions of inflation, it doesn't stop cleanly. Once inflation starts, it keeps going — not everywhere, but in patches. Our universe is one bubble that stopped inflating. But the broader process of inflation is ongoing, eternal, constantly spawning new bubble universes, each potentially with different physical constants, different particle masses, different laws of physics. This is "eternal inflation."

If eternal inflation is correct, our universe is one bubble in an infinite sea of bubbles that can never communicate with each other.

**Route 2: String Theory**

String theory was supposed to be the final theory of everything — the framework that would unify quantum mechanics and general relativity. It started promising. By the late 1990s, something unexpected emerged.

String theory requires extra spatial dimensions beyond the three we experience. The geometry of those extra dimensions determines the physical constants in our universe — the strength of gravity, the mass of the electron, everything. And the number of possible configurations for those extra dimensions is staggering. In 2003, Stanford physicist Leonard Susskind calculated it: roughly 10^500 different configurations. Each produces a different universe with different physics.

There is no equation that tells you which configuration you're in. There's no derivation that predicts our specific constants. Instead, physicists increasingly suggest that all 10^500 configurations exist — the "string theory landscape" — and we live in one that supports life because only life-supporting universes can be observed.

Susskind wrote a book about it. He called it "The Cosmic Landscape." Many of his colleagues were furious.

**Route 3: Quantum Mechanics (Everett Again)**

The measurement problem — why quantum systems behave as waves of probability until observed, then suddenly "choose" a definite outcome — has never been resolved. (See our article on the measurement problem.)

Many-Worlds sidesteps the problem by rejecting the "choice." There is no collapse. All outcomes occur. The universe splits. What you experience as a definite outcome is just the branch you happen to be in.

The math works. The interpretation doesn't require any new physics beyond standard quantum mechanics. It just requires accepting that every quantum event creates new branches of reality, multiplying universes constantly.

Three completely different branches of physics. The same answer.

## Why This Is a Scientific Crisis

In 1934, philosopher of science Karl Popper published a criterion that became the defining standard for what counts as science: a theory must make predictions that could, in principle, be shown to be false. If no observation could ever contradict it, it's not science — it's metaphysics.

The multiverse fails this test. Not because the physics is bad, but because the geometry of the situation makes it unfalsifiable by design. Other bubble universes are behind our cosmic horizon — regions of space expanding away from us faster than light. We cannot observe them. Everett branches split off and become causally disconnected. We cannot observe them. String theory configurations generate different universes. We cannot observe them.

This produced a genuine confrontation in physics. In 2014, cosmologists George Ellis and Joe Silk published a commentary in *Nature* titled "Scientific Method: Defend the Integrity of Physics." They argued that multiverse theories, and string theory more broadly, were leading physicists to abandon falsifiability as a standard — to accept theories on the grounds that they're mathematically beautiful or explanatorily powerful, even when untestable.

Their essay was signed by dozens of physicists and scientists. Susskind responded. Sean Carroll, a Caltech physicist and prominent multiverse advocate, argued that falsifiability as a strict criterion was outdated — that explanatory power and theoretical coherence were legitimate scientific virtues even without direct testability.

The debate is unresolved. It's not just about the multiverse. It's about what science is allowed to claim.

## The Strongest Argument for the Multiverse

The multiverse's most compelling justification is the fine-tuning problem. (See our article on fine-tuning.)

The physical constants of our universe appear to be calibrated with extraordinary precision for the existence of complex structures. The cosmological constant — the energy density of empty space — is set to roughly 1 in 10^120 of what it "should" be according to quantum field theory. If it were even slightly larger, the universe would have expanded too fast for galaxies to form. Slightly smaller, it would have collapsed.

There are two ways to respond to this:
1. **Accept it as unexplained coincidence**, or hope that a future theory will show the constants had to be what they are.
2. **Invoke the anthropic principle**: if there are many universes with different constants, of course we find ourselves in one that supports life — only life-supporting universes can have observers. We're not special; we're just in a lucky bubble.

The multiverse turns the fine-tuning problem from a mystery into a selection effect. Many physicists find this deeply satisfying. Others find it deeply troubling, because it explains everything by assuming the existence of entities we can never observe.

Lee Smolin, a physicist at the Perimeter Institute, proposed a different solution: cosmological natural selection. Black holes spawn new universes, he argued, and universes with parameters that produce more black holes produce more offspring. Our universe's constants might be the result of a selection process analogous to evolution, not random chance. His proposal is also not easily testable, but it makes specific predictions about the relationship between black hole production and universal constants — which is more than pure multiverse theory can claim.

## What We Might Actually Be Able to Test

The word "multiverse" covers different kinds of claims that have very different levels of testability.

**Inflation and bubble collisions**: If our universe underwent inflation and other bubble universes exist nearby, they might have collided with ours early in cosmic history. Such collisions would leave detectable imprints in the cosmic microwave background — subtle temperature patterns with specific properties. In 2011, a team including cosmologist Hiranya Peiris analyzed CMB data from the WMAP satellite looking for these signatures. They found no convincing evidence, but ruled out only certain types of collisions.

The CMB "cold spot" — an anomalously cool region in the sky first identified in 2004 — was briefly proposed as a possible bubble collision signature. Subsequent analysis has generally attributed it to other causes, though the debate isn't completely closed.

**Quantum gravity signatures**: Some models predict that if extra dimensions exist at the scales string theory requires, the Large Hadron Collider might produce gravitons that escape into those dimensions, showing up as missing energy. So far, no such signatures have been detected. The LHC has placed strong limits on many versions of extra dimensions.

**The Everett prediction problem**: Many-Worlds doesn't make different empirical predictions than other interpretations of quantum mechanics — that's precisely the problem. Some physicists argue this means it's untestable. Others argue that interpretations of quantum mechanics aren't meant to make new predictions — they're trying to explain what an existing set of correct equations means.

## The People Who Think It's All Wrong

Not everyone has accepted the multiverse turn.

**Roger Penrose**, Nobel laureate, argues that Everett's Many-Worlds is not just untestable but logically incoherent — that the probability question (why do we experience outcomes with specific probabilities if all outcomes occur?) has no satisfactory answer. He also believes that quantum gravity will eventually eliminate the need for multiple worlds.

**Lee Smolin** argues that physics has gone badly wrong by accepting unfalsifiable theories, and that the real crisis is the failure to solve quantum gravity — not an invitation to multiply untestable universes.

**Sabine Hossenfelder**, a theoretical physicist and popular science communicator, has written extensively that multiverse theories are symptoms of physics losing its way — too much reliance on mathematical beauty and not enough on experimental guidance. Her book *Lost in Math* argues that the field has been led astray by aesthetic preferences that don't reliably track truth.

These are not fringe views. They come from working physicists with serious credentials. The field is genuinely divided.

---

## The Deepest Problem

Behind all of this is a question that physics has not yet answered: what are the laws of physics?

We've discovered equations that describe how things behave. But we don't know why those equations apply. We don't know why the constants have the values they do. We don't know whether those constants could have been different — whether they had to be what they are, or whether they're contingent, a cosmic roll of dice.

If they're contingent — if other values were possible — then either we're extraordinarily lucky, or there are other universes where different values apply, or there's something we don't yet understand that determines the values from the inside.

The multiverse is one response to that ignorance. It's not obviously wrong. It might be the most honest reckoning with what the equations tell us. But it comes at a cost: we've entered territory where our standard tools for evaluating truth — prediction, experiment, falsification — either don't apply or apply only weakly.

That's not a reason to stop. It might be a reason to develop better tools.

Physics has done it before.
