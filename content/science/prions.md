In the late 1970s, a man in Venice began having trouble sleeping.

It started with mild insomnia. Then the insomnia became total. Then came hallucinations, dementia, coma. He died within a year. Before he died, the same thing had happened to his sister. His grandfather, too.

His family — the Venetian family affected by what researchers would later call Fatal Familial Insomnia — carried a mutation in a single gene. The mutation produced a protein that, under certain conditions, refolded into a shape that destroyed the part of the brain that regulates sleep. It was not a virus. Not a bacterium. Not a parasite. Just a protein in a different shape.

There are roughly 40 families in the world with this mutation. Some of them know what's coming. Some don't.

This is a prion disease. And despite a Nobel Prize, decades of research, and a global crisis involving contaminated beef that killed hundreds of people, we still cannot fully explain how a protein — alone, with no genetic material — can cause disease and spread it.

---

## Every Known Infectious Agent Has DNA or RNA. Except This.

The central dogma of biology: DNA makes RNA makes protein. Genetic information flows in one direction. A virus hijacks this system by introducing its own DNA or RNA, forcing your cells to replicate the virus. Bacteria carry their own DNA and metabolic machinery. Fungi, parasites — same principle. They all have nucleic acids encoding the instructions for their own reproduction.

A prion has none of this.

The word was coined by Stanley Prusiner in 1982, from "proteinaceous infectious particle." The idea: a single misfolded protein, with no nucleic acid, could be infectious. It could enter tissue, convert normally shaped proteins into copies of itself, spread through the brain, and kill.

This was considered nonsense.

The established science of the time — accumulated over a century of microbiology — held that this was impossible. Infection required nucleic acids. That was the definition. The protein-only hypothesis wasn't a fringe idea; it was a category error.

The pushback against Prusiner was fierce. His grant funding was cut. Papers were rejected. Colleagues questioned his integrity. The Nobel Committee, when it eventually awarded him the 1997 prize in Physiology or Medicine, was doing something unusual: honoring a hypothesis that was still contested.

But the experimental evidence had accumulated. And it was pointing in one direction.

## The Shape That Kills

Your brain contains a protein called PrPC — cellular prion protein. It's present in most tissues but densely concentrated in neurons. It's been conserved across evolution; mice, cattle, and humans all make versions of it. We've had it, in roughly its current form, for at least 500 million years.

We don't know what it does normally.

This is one of the strangest facts about prions. The protein is ancient, ubiquitous, and expressed at high levels in the brain — all signs of something important. Mice engineered to lack the gene entirely are mostly normal. Some studies suggest roles in protecting neurons from stress, regulating copper metabolism, or modulating sleep. None of it has been established clearly. The function of the normal form of the most dangerous protein in neurology is still an open question.

What we do know is what happens when it misfolds.

The misfolded version, called PrPSc (Sc for scrapie, the sheep prion disease), has the same amino acid sequence as PrPC. Same letters, different fold. The 3D structure changes dramatically — sheets of beta protein replace the normal coiled structure — and with it, everything about the protein's behavior changes.

PrPSc is insoluble. It aggregates. It resists the proteases cells normally use to clear damaged proteins. And crucially: when PrPSc encounters PrPC, it catalyzes its conversion. One misfolded protein touches a normal one and changes its shape. That new misfolded protein can convert another. The process is autocatalytic.

The disease is not caused by the prion killing cells directly. It's caused by the accumulation of aggregated PrPSc, which disrupts cellular function and eventually destroys brain tissue, leaving it riddled with microscopic holes — spongiform degeneration. The brain looks like a sponge under the microscope.

## Mad Cow and the Public Crisis

The protein-only hypothesis stopped being theoretical in the 1980s when British cattle began dying of bovine spongiform encephalopathy — BSE, or mad cow disease.

The proximate cause: cattle feed that contained rendered animal products, including cattle brain and spinal cord tissue. An infected animal's prions entered the food supply, infected other cattle, and spread. By the peak of the epidemic in the early 1990s, hundreds of thousands of cattle were infected. 4.4 million animals were slaughtered as a precaution.

In 1996, the UK government confirmed what researchers had feared: people who had eaten BSE-contaminated beef were dying of a new variant of Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease — vCJD. The prion had crossed the species barrier.

The number of human deaths was, ultimately, smaller than the worst-case projections: 232 people died from vCJD worldwide, the majority in the UK. But the crisis transformed the prion hypothesis from laboratory curiosity to public health reality. Prions were real. They were infectious. They spread through food. They killed people.

Prusiner's Nobel came the following year.

## What the Nobel Prize Didn't Answer

The protein-only hypothesis is now accepted. But accepted doesn't mean explained.

**The mechanism is still incomplete.** We know that PrPSc converts PrPC. What we don't fully understand is the physical chemistry of how this happens — what intermediate forms the protein passes through, which cellular environments promote or suppress the conversion, why the process sometimes takes decades to produce symptoms while other times progresses in months. Kuru, the prion disease spread by cannibalism in Papua New Guinea (studied by Gajdusek, who won a Nobel in 1976), had incubation periods of up to 50 years. Variant CJD can kill within a year. The same basic mechanism, wildly different timescales. Why?

**Prion strains exist without nucleic acid.** Different prion diseases produce different patterns of brain damage, different rates of progression, different clinical symptoms. In conventional biology, distinct strains of an infectious agent differ in their DNA or RNA sequences. Prion strains have no nucleic acid — but they behave as distinct entities. The current hypothesis is that different strains correspond to different stable conformations of PrPSc. But how different folded shapes of the same protein reliably produce different disease phenotypes across animals and generations of propagation is not mechanistically solved.

**The function of normal PrPC remains unknown.** Prusiner spent years arguing that PrPC must be doing something essential — the alternative seemed absurd, given how conserved it is. But knockout mice lacking the protein live relatively normal lives. One current hypothesis is that PrPC acts as a signal transduction receptor, that its function emerges only under stress or at specific developmental windows. There is no consensus.

**Prion-like spreading in other diseases.** This is perhaps the most consequential open question. In Alzheimer's disease, tau protein and amyloid-beta aggregates spread through the brain in a spatially predictable pattern — adjacent regions accumulate deposits in progression. In Parkinson's, alpha-synuclein. In ALS, TDP-43.

All of these proteins, when misfolded, appear to behave like prions: they seed the misfolding of the same protein in neighboring cells, spreading through neural circuits in a way that mirrors the progression of symptoms. Studies have shown that injecting misfolded alpha-synuclein into mice induces Parkinson's-like pathology. Tau extracts from Alzheimer's brains inoculated into mice produce tau aggregation that spreads.

Whether this means Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and ALS are "prion diseases" in any meaningful sense is contested. The molecules involved are different. The transmissibility between individuals is absent or extremely rare. But the biological mechanism — one misfolded protein templating the misfolding of others — may be the same.

If it is, then prion biology isn't a rare curiosity affecting a few hundred people per year. It may be the core mechanism of the most common causes of dementia and neurodegeneration.

## The Family in Venice

The Venetian family with Fatal Familial Insomnia has lived with the knowledge that some of them carry the mutation for decades. Genetic testing can identify who has it — but there is no treatment. No way to stop the sleep from stopping.

The maximum reported age of onset is around 60. For a carrier in their 30s, this is information they must decide what to do with. Have children who might inherit the mutation? Get tested and find out? Not find out?

One family member, D.T.M. — who asked researchers not to use his full name — chose to be tested, found he was a carrier, and began donating blood and brain tissue samples to research. He participated in clinical trials of drugs that didn't work. The last reported stage of his life was documented in a book by D.T. Max, who followed the family over years.

There is one funded research project working on Fatal Familial Insomnia. It's trying to develop an RNA interference approach to silence the mutant gene. Progress is slow.

The sleep still stops.
