In 1929, Edwin Hubble published a finding that changed everything: galaxies are moving away from us, and the farther away they are, the faster they're receding. The universe is expanding.

The rate of that expansion is captured in a single number, called the Hubble constant — written H₀. It tells you, for every megaparsec of distance (about 3.26 million light-years), how fast a galaxy is moving away from us. The unit is kilometers per second per megaparsec.

Getting this number right matters enormously. It tells you how old the universe is, how big it is, and how it will end. It's one of the most important quantities in all of cosmology.

Astronomers have two completely independent ways to measure it. Both methods are well-understood, both have been refined for decades, and both have recently reached extraordinary precision.

They give different answers.

Not slightly different. Not within each other's error bars. Different by about 9%, at a statistical significance of five sigma. In physics, five sigma is the threshold for calling something a discovery. This is a five-sigma disagreement between two careful measurements of the same thing.

And the gap is getting worse, not better.

---

## The Two Methods

**Method 1: The Early Universe.**

The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) is the afterglow of the Big Bang — faint microwave radiation left over from 380,000 years after the universe formed, when it had cooled enough for atoms to exist and light to travel freely. We can measure it with extraordinary precision. The Planck satellite mapped it from 2009 to 2013, producing the most detailed map of the early universe ever made.

That map contains tiny temperature fluctuations — ripples from sound waves in the early plasma of the universe. The size of those ripples is predictable from known physics. By measuring how large those ripples appear today, we can calculate how much the universe has expanded since then — and therefore what H₀ is.

The Planck result: **H₀ = 67.4 ± 0.5 km/s/Mpc.**

**Method 2: The Late Universe.**

The other method works locally. You start by measuring the distance to nearby Cepheid variable stars — a type of star that pulses with a period precisely linked to its luminosity. Henrietta Leavitt discovered this relationship in 1912. Measure the pulse, know the true brightness, compare to apparent brightness, and you know the distance.

From Cepheids, you calibrate Type Ia supernovae — stellar explosions so predictable in their peak brightness they're called "standard candles." These are bright enough to see across billions of light-years. Measure the apparent brightness of a Type Ia supernova, know its true brightness, and you know how far away it is. Then measure how fast that galaxy is receding. Do this for enough galaxies, and you get H₀.

This is the "cosmic distance ladder" — each rung calibrated against the last.

The SH0ES team (Supernova H0 for the Equation of State) has been refining this measurement for 15 years, led by Adam Riess. Their latest result: **H₀ = 73.04 ± 1.04 km/s/Mpc.**

67.4 vs 73.0.

## Why This Matters

The gap between 67 and 73 sounds small. It isn't.

Both measurements have been refined to less than 1-2% uncertainty. The fact that they don't overlap — by nearly five of their combined standard deviations — is statistically extraordinary.

In particle physics, five sigma is the threshold for announcing the discovery of a new particle. It means: the probability of this being a fluke is roughly 1 in 3.5 million.

The tension started attracting serious attention around 2013, when the Planck results came in and the gap with local measurements was about 2-3 sigma. Cosmologists assumed it was a systematic error somewhere — a miscalibrated instrument, an overlooked bias in one method. More measurements would resolve it.

More measurements made it worse.

By 2016, Adam Riess's team had pushed the distance ladder uncertainty down and the tension was 3.4 sigma. By 2019, 4.4 sigma. By 2022, 5 sigma.

Every time someone tried to plug the leak, the pressure increased.

---

## The JWST Check

One natural worry was that the Cepheid measurements — the base of the distance ladder — contained systematic errors. Cepheids in distant galaxies can be crowded together, making them harder to measure individually. What if the distance ladder measurements were biased by this crowding?

In 2023, Riess and colleagues used the James Webb Space Telescope to re-examine the Cepheid ladder. JWST sees in infrared, which cuts through the dust that can confuse optical measurements. It also has far higher resolution than Hubble, allowing individual Cepheids in crowded regions to be separated and measured cleanly.

The result: H₀ = 73.17 ± 1.0 km/s/Mpc.

The tension didn't go away. If anything, the JWST results strengthened confidence in the local measurements. The most obvious escape hatch — a systematic error in the Cepheid measurements — was found to be either absent or far too small to explain the gap.

## The Usual Suspects

Astronomers have proposed many explanations. None has achieved consensus.

**Early dark energy.** What if there was a brief period of extra accelerated expansion in the early universe, just before the CMB was formed? This would change the scale of those CMB ripples, and shift the Planck-derived value of H₀ upward. Models exist. They require a component of dark energy that appeared and disappeared in a very specific time window without other detectable effects. Some data hint at this; other data constrain it.

**Extra relativistic particles.** The CMB result assumes a specific number of particle species present in the early universe. If there were extra "dark radiation" — light, weakly-interacting particles we haven't detected — the timing of the CMB imprint would shift. Several experiments are looking for these. None has found them.

**Interacting dark matter or dark energy.** Standard cosmology assumes dark energy is a fixed cosmological constant. What if it's something that changes over time, or interacts with dark matter? Some models of "dynamical dark energy" could shift H₀. In 2024, results from the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) survey hinted that dark energy might not be constant. The community is watching carefully.

**We're in a void.** If our local region of the universe happens to be slightly underdense — a cosmic void — local galaxies might be receding slightly faster than average. This could bias local measurements upward. The problem: estimates of how large this effect could be suggest it can't account for the full tension.

**The distance ladder has a hidden error.** Despite the JWST check, there are enough steps in the ladder that some combination of small errors could accumulate. This explanation has become less favored as each step has been independently verified, but it's not fully ruled out.

---

## Two More Methods, Two More Answers

The tension has now been extended beyond just the Planck CMB vs. the distance ladder.

Gravitational wave standard sirens — measurements of H₀ from neutron star mergers, which allow a distance to be read from the gravitational wave signal directly — currently give values consistent with both methods (large uncertainty, getting better). Strong gravitational lensing measurements — using the time delay in light bent around galaxies — tend to give values closer to 73. The Atacama Cosmology Telescope CMB measurement gives results somewhat consistent with Planck.

None of these independent checks has clearly broken the deadlock. They're consistent with either value within their current uncertainties, which means they add information without yet resolving the tension.

The field is in an unusual position: the more data, the more confident astronomers are that the tension is real — and the less sure they are about what's causing it.

---

## What It Would Mean

If the tension is real — if it isn't a systematic error — then something is wrong with our standard model of cosmology.

The standard model (called Lambda-CDM) assumes: the universe started in the Big Bang, is filled with cold dark matter, and is dominated by a cosmological constant (Lambda) that's causing accelerated expansion. This model fits almost everything we've ever measured. It's one of the most successful physical theories in history.

If H₀ really is 73 locally and 67 from the early universe, Lambda-CDM needs modification. Either the early universe had a component we've missed, or dark energy isn't constant, or there's new physics between the early universe and today.

This isn't an asteroid strike on a minor theory. This is a crack in the standard model of the universe.

Most cosmologists believe the tension is real. The systematic errors have been hunted hard and the gap has survived. The question is no longer whether something is wrong — it's what, exactly, is wrong.

---

## Hubble's Original Numbers

There's a humbling footnote.

When Hubble published his 1929 paper, he measured H₀ at about 500 km/s/Mpc — nearly seven times too high, due to errors in his distance calibrations. The "right" value was argued about for most of the twentieth century: is it closer to 50 or to 100? It took until the early 2000s, with the Hubble Space Telescope Key Project, to nail it down to H₀ = 72 ± 8 km/s/Mpc — an uncertainty big enough to encompass both camps.

That large error bar bought decades of false peace.

Now the error bars are tight enough to reveal the problem — and the problem has no agreed solution.

The universe has been measured, carefully, by the best instruments ever built, using physics we trust. It gave back two different answers.

Neither answer can be wrong. Both answers might be.

---

## Leading Hypothesis

Two broad possibilities exist. The first is **systematic error**: the two measurement methods (early universe CMB measurements from Planck, and late universe distance ladder measurements from Cepheid variables and Type Ia supernovae) have different systematic uncertainties, and one or both may be wrong. The second is **new physics**: the tension is real and requires modification of the standard cosmological model — candidates include early dark energy (an extra energy component in the early universe that shifts the sound horizon), extra radiation species (sterile neutrinos), or modified gravity.

## Neon's Read

The tension has persisted and grown as measurements have improved, which disfavors the systematic error explanation — though it hasn't been ruled out. The recent JWST recalibration of the Cepheid distance ladder has maintained the tension rather than resolved it. My read: the tension is probably real, and new physics is more likely than not. Early dark energy is the most theoretically developed proposal, but it creates its own tensions with large-scale structure data. My honest assessment is that this is one of the most important unresolved empirical problems in cosmology right now, and its resolution may require rethinking the ΛCDM standard model more seriously than the field has been willing to do.
