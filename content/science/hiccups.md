Charles Osborne started hiccupping in 1922. He was 28 years old, slaughtering a hog in Anthon, Iowa, when the hiccups began. They didn't stop.

He hiccupped for 68 years — through two marriages, eight children, and most of the twentieth century. Doctors couldn't find a cause or a cure. In 1990, the hiccups stopped on their own. Osborne died eleven months later. His record, certified by Guinness, stands at approximately 430 million hiccups.

The Osborne case is extreme, but it illustrates something genuinely strange about hiccups: a reflex this universal, this persistent, and this precisely controlled by the nervous system apparently does nothing useful.

## What a Hiccup Actually Is

A hiccup is a two-part event.

First, the diaphragm — the sheet of muscle under your lungs that drives breathing — contracts suddenly and involuntarily. This pulls air inward sharply.

Then, about 35 milliseconds later, the glottis (the opening between your vocal cords) snaps shut. This abrupt closure stops the inrushing air and produces the characteristic sound. The "hic" is the glottis slamming.

The whole thing is controlled by a neural circuit running through the brainstem, coordinating the phrenic nerve (which controls the diaphragm) and the vagus nerve (which supplies the throat and lungs). This circuit is sometimes called the **hiccup arc**. It operates essentially like a spinal reflex — the brainstem executes it without waiting for input from higher brain regions, which is why you can't simply decide to stop.

Common triggers — eating too fast, carbonated drinks, sudden temperature change, alcohol, stress — all seem to irritate the vagus nerve or the nerve endings near the stomach and esophagus. But the exact chain of events between "ate too fast" and "hiccup reflex fires" is not fully characterized.

## The Uselessness Problem

What makes hiccups scientifically interesting is their apparent purposelessness.

Most reflexes have a clear function. The blink reflex protects the eye. The gag reflex prevents aspiration. The sneeze clears the nasal passages. Pain withdrawal reflexes prevent tissue damage. These all make obvious evolutionary sense.

Hiccups fit none of this logic. The glottis-closure that defines the hiccup blocks airflow. If the reflex served any kind of respiratory purpose, that closure is precisely what you'd want to avoid. The hiccup doesn't clear anything, protect anything, or advance any process. In adult humans, it is simply disruptive — sometimes mildly, sometimes (as Osborne demonstrated) devastatingly.

A reflex controlled by a dedicated neural arc, conserved across hundreds of millions of years of vertebrate evolution, that does nothing. That's the paradox.

## The Aquatic Ancestor Hypothesis

The most compelling explanation is that hiccups aren't for us. They're inherited from ancestors that needed them.

In 2003, a research team led by neuroscientist Christian Straus at the University of Paris proposed a detailed hypothesis: the hiccup reflex is evolutionarily ancient, and its original function was to help aquatic ancestors — fish and amphibians — manage water in their gills.

Fish with gills use a similar motor pattern. To push water across the gills for gas exchange, they contract muscles that draw water in, then close the glottis-equivalent to prevent water from entering the lungs. The neural circuit is strikingly similar to the human hiccup arc. Tadpoles, which still have functioning gills, use the same mechanism.

When vertebrates transitioned to land and lungs replaced gills, the circuit came along. It no longer serves its original function — there are no gills to clear — but the neural machinery persists.

Supporting evidence: **fetuses hiccup extensively in the third trimester**, before the lungs are even functional for breathing. Some researchers suggest this might train the respiratory muscles, but a more likely explanation is that the reflex is simply running — activated by the same triggers that activated it in gill-breathing ancestors — because there's been no strong evolutionary pressure to suppress it. A vestigial reflex that only mildly inconveniences adults doesn't get eliminated quickly.

The gill-clearing hypothesis isn't proven — it's a phylogenetic argument by analogy rather than direct evidence — but no competing hypothesis explains the anatomy and timing as well.

## Why Can't You Stop It?

The hiccup reflex bypasses voluntary control in the same way that the knee-jerk reflex does. The arc runs through the brainstem and doesn't require cortical approval.

This is why every folk remedy works approximately never. Holding your breath, breathing into a bag, drinking water upside down, being frightened — none of these have been shown to reliably terminate hiccups in controlled testing. They may occasionally help by altering CO2 levels, stimulating the vagus nerve, or disrupting the reflex arc through sensory input, but the effect is inconsistent and almost certainly placebo-mediated in most cases.

The one intervention that reliably terminates hiccups is direct stimulation or interruption of the phrenic nerve — the nerve that drives the diaphragm. This can be done by pressure on certain points in the neck, or in severe cases, surgically. It works, but it's not an approach appropriate for hiccups that have been going on for twenty minutes.

For chronic hiccups (lasting more than 48 hours) and intractable hiccups (lasting more than a month), there are pharmacological options. Chlorpromazine, an antipsychotic, is the only FDA-approved drug for hiccups. Baclofen (a muscle relaxant that acts on GABA receptors) and gabapentin have shown efficacy in case reports and small trials. These drugs work by modulating the neural arc, not by addressing any underlying cause.

## Chronic Hiccups and What They Reveal

Chronic and intractable hiccups are rare but genuinely debilitating. They disrupt sleep, eating, and speech. They cause exhaustion and can lead to malnutrition in severe cases.

Most chronic hiccup cases are secondary to something else — a lesion or irritation affecting the vagus nerve or phrenic nerve, or conditions including gastroesophageal reflux, central nervous system disorders, metabolic problems (uremia, hyponatremia), or as a side effect of certain drugs including dexamethasone and some chemotherapy agents.

This reveals something about the reflex's architecture: because the vagus nerve is one of the most wide-ranging in the body — connecting the brainstem to the heart, lungs, stomach, and intestines — nearly any irritation in that extended territory can set off the hiccup arc. The circuit is exquisitely sensitive.

Osborne's case was unusual in that no underlying cause was ever found despite extensive examination. The hiccups appeared to be idiopathic — self-sustaining, with no treatable root. The arc was firing, and nothing stopped it.

## The Open Questions

Why the reflex persists across all air-breathing vertebrates without any clear modern function is the central puzzle. The aquatic ancestor hypothesis explains the origin but not the persistence. Most vestigial structures either degrade over evolutionary time (if they carry a cost) or remain stable (if they carry no cost). Hiccups are in the latter category — annoying but rarely fatal, not subject to strong selection pressure for elimination.

What controls the duration of a normal hiccup episode is also not understood. Most episodes stop within minutes, apparently spontaneously, but the mechanism that terminates the reflex arc isn't characterized. Something resets the system, but researchers don't know what.

And the broader question — whether the fetal hiccuping serves any actual developmental function or is purely vestigial activity of an ancient circuit — remains open. One hypothesis is that it helps the fetus practice the coordination between breathing muscles and glottis that will be needed after birth. Another is that it does nothing, and evolution just hasn't gotten around to turning it off.

The hog-slaughtering incident in Anthon, Iowa was probably a vagal event — the shock or exertion triggered the nerve, and in Osborne's case, whatever mechanism normally terminates the arc after a few minutes never engaged. The circuit ran for 68 years.

We still don't know how to stop it.
