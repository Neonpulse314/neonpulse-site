When matter and antimatter collide, they annihilate.

Not "destroy each other" in a dramatic sense. Literally annihilate — two particles become nothing but pure energy. An electron and a positron meet, and both cease to exist. What's left is two photons of light, carrying exactly the energy that the two particles had as mass. Nothing else. No residue. No matter.

This is the cleanest conversion in physics. Einstein's E=mc² describes it: mass becomes energy with 100% efficiency. Antimatter is the only thing in the universe that fulfills that equation completely.

The problem is what it means for the Big Bang.

---

## The Universe Should Not Exist

Physics says antimatter is matter's mirror image. Every particle has an antiparticle: the electron has the positron, the proton has the antiproton, the neutron has the antineutron. They have identical masses. They behave identically in most interactions. The laws of physics, as far as we can tell, should treat them symmetrically.

Which means the Big Bang — the event that created all the energy and matter in the universe — should have created equal amounts of both.

If it did, here's what happens next: matter and antimatter meet. They annihilate. All of it. Every particle finds its antiparticle partner, and both cease to exist. What's left is a universe of pure photons, expanding outward forever. No atoms. No molecules. No chemistry. No planets. No stars. No anything.

You would not exist. Neither would the galaxy you're in, or the 400 billion other galaxies in the observable universe. There would be nothing with mass anywhere. Just light.

And yet: here we are.

Something went wrong. Or, more precisely, something went exactly right in a way we cannot explain.

---

## The One in a Billion

Here's what actually happened, as best as we can reconstruct it.

In the first second after the Big Bang, the universe was hot enough to constantly create matter-antimatter pairs from pure energy, and those pairs would annihilate back into energy, over and over in a frenzy of creation and destruction. As the universe cooled, this stopped — there was no longer enough energy to create new pairs, and the annihilation swept through what remained.

But the annihilation wasn't perfect. For every billion matter-antimatter pairs that annihilated, one extra matter particle was left over.

One in a billion. A 0.0000001% imbalance. That tiny asymmetry is everything you have ever seen or touched or known. Every atom in your body is made from one of those survivors. Every star, every planet, every galaxy is built from the residue of that imperfect cancellation. The photons left over from all that annihilation are still around — they're the cosmic microwave background radiation, the faint glow that fills the entire sky. You're living in the light from the annihilation.

The question is: where did that one-in-a-billion excess come from?

## What We Know (Not Much)

In 1964, James Cronin and Val Fitch were studying the decay of particles called kaons. What they found was unexpected: matter and antimatter kaons didn't behave identically. The laws of physics treated them differently. Subtly, but measurably.

This was the first experimental proof that matter and antimatter are not perfect mirrors of each other. The effect is called CP violation — the violation of charge-parity symmetry, the rule that swapping matter for antimatter and flipping all directions should leave physics unchanged. Cronin and Fitch got the Nobel Prize in 1980 for breaking that rule experimentally.

Since then, we've found CP violation in several types of particles: B mesons, D mesons, and hints in neutrinos. In 2019, the LHCb experiment at CERN confirmed CP violation in charm quarks for the first time. In 2020, the T2K experiment in Japan found tantalizing evidence that neutrinos and antineutrinos behave differently — possibly the strongest hint yet of the source of the imbalance.

But here's the problem.

The amount of CP violation we've found — in every particle, in every experiment — is far too small to explain the observed imbalance. Too small by a factor of roughly 10 billion. The math says the excess we see in the universe would require ten billion times more asymmetry than the Standard Model of particle physics can produce.

We are, in some sense, made from a gap in the accounting. And we don't know what fills it.

## The Sakharov Conditions

In 1967, the Soviet physicist Andrei Sakharov — who also designed the Soviet hydrogen bomb and later won the Nobel Peace Prize for opposing it — wrote a short paper laying out the three conditions necessary for the observed matter-antimatter imbalance to have arisen naturally.

The three conditions are:

**1. Baryon number violation.** Baryons are particles like protons and neutrons — the stuff normal matter is made of. For more matter than antimatter to exist, some process must have created or destroyed baryons without creating their antimatter counterparts. This has never been directly observed. The proton appears to be essentially stable — experiments have watched enormous tanks of water for decades without seeing a single proton decay.

**2. C and CP violation.** Matter and antimatter must behave differently in some interaction. This we have seen. But not enough of it.

**3. Departure from thermal equilibrium.** If everything was in perfect thermal equilibrium, any process creating excess baryons would be perfectly balanced by the reverse process destroying them. Something must have disrupted the balance — a phase transition, a rapid expansion, some asymmetric decay.

Sakharov's conditions are a framework, not an answer. They tell us what kind of physics must have happened. They don't tell us which physics, or where, or how.

## Leptogenesis: The Leading Theory

The most popular current framework is called leptogenesis. It goes like this.

In the very early universe, before most of what we think of as matter existed, there may have been extremely heavy particles called sterile neutrinos. They have no charge, interact only through gravity (and possibly the weak force), and they're hypothetical — we've never detected them directly. But they're predicted by extensions of the Standard Model.

If sterile neutrinos existed, they would have decayed asymmetrically — producing slightly more matter-type particles (leptons, like electrons and neutrinos) than their antimatter counterparts. This created an imbalance in the lepton sector first.

Then, through a process called sphaleron transitions — a complex electroweak mechanism that was active only in the extremely hot early universe — that lepton asymmetry was converted into a baryon asymmetry. The matter excess we see today is the echo of an imbalance that started in particles that no longer exist, converted by a process that only operated for a fraction of a second after the Big Bang.

This is elegant. It's also almost entirely untestable. We can't recreate the conditions of the early universe. We can't detect sterile neutrinos directly (yet). We can look for CP violation in normal neutrinos — the T2K result is promising — but connecting that to the baryon asymmetry requires assumptions we can't yet confirm.

---

## What CERN Is Looking For

Right now, experiments at CERN and elsewhere are hunting for the physics that explains the imbalance.

The LHCb experiment is specifically designed to compare matter and antimatter behavior in B mesons — particles that contain heavy quarks and decay in many different ways, each of which might show slightly different behavior between matter and antimatter versions. The more asymmetry they find, and the more precisely they can measure it, the better the constraint on whatever new physics might be responsible.

The ALPHA experiment at CERN is doing something more fundamental: measuring the properties of antihydrogen, an atom made of an antiproton and a positron. If antihydrogen has slightly different spectral lines than hydrogen — if antimatter atoms don't behave exactly like matter atoms — that would be direct evidence of asymmetry at the atomic level.

So far, antihydrogen looks like a perfect mirror of hydrogen. To the precision we can measure.

The KATRIN experiment in Germany is measuring neutrino masses with extraordinary precision, looking for properties that might hint at the physics needed for leptogenesis.

None of these experiments have found the answer yet.

---

## Why It Matters

The matter-antimatter problem isn't abstract. It's personal.

You exist because of a one-in-a-billion imperfection in the laws of physics. The universe is 13.8 billion years old. For the first fraction of a second, it ran a competition between matter and antimatter, and matter barely won. Everything since — every atom, every planet, every moment of life or thought or love or pain — is built from that margin.

There's a version of the universe where the competition is perfectly balanced. In that version, nothing survives. There's no one to ask why.

The fact that you're here to ask the question means the physics was asymmetric. The fact that we don't know why means we're still missing something fundamental about the laws of nature.

The asymmetry that made you possible is one we cannot yet explain. The universe is made of the survivors, and the survivors don't know how they survived.

---

*The matter-antimatter asymmetry problem — baryogenesis — remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in physics. The Sakharov conditions were formulated in 1967. More than 50 years of experiments have confirmed CP violation in multiple particle systems without finding enough to explain the observed imbalance. The source of the one-in-a-billion excess is unknown.*
