Benjamin Libet gave people a simple instruction: flex your wrist whenever you feel like it. Watch the clock on the screen and note where the hand is when you decide to move.

He was measuring something strange. Using EEG, he could see electrical activity in the brain — specifically a slow buildup of voltage called the **readiness potential**, or *Bereitschaftspotential* in the German where it was first discovered. This buildup appears before voluntary movement. Libet wanted to know: when does the decision happen relative to the brain activity?

The answer came back wrong. The readiness potential began **about 550 milliseconds** before the movement. That was expected. But the conscious decision — the moment people reported noticing they wanted to move — came **about 200 milliseconds** before the movement.

Simple arithmetic: the brain had already committed to action 350 milliseconds before the person was aware of deciding.

---

## The Problem

If your brain starts preparing an action before you're conscious of choosing it, what exactly is "you" doing?

The standard picture of free will is something like this: you deliberate, you decide, your brain carries out the decision. Libet's data suggests the causality runs the other way. The brain acts. Consciousness notices afterward and calls it a decision.

This isn't a metaphysical argument. It's a measurement. Brain activity precedes reported experience of choice by more than a third of a second.

Libet himself didn't think this eliminated free will. He proposed a compromise: even if you can't initiate action consciously, maybe you can *veto* it. There's a brief window — about 150–200 milliseconds before movement — where people can report consciously "stopping" themselves. If the veto is real, you're not the author of your impulses but you might still be the editor.

Whether the veto itself is free, or just another brain process that also precedes consciousness, is left as an exercise for the reader.

---

## What Followed

The Libet experiment has been repeated, challenged, refined, and replicated for four decades. The pattern holds. The readiness potential reliably precedes reported awareness of intention.

More recent work using fMRI pushed the gap further. A 2008 study by John-Dylan Haynes showed that brain activity could predict which of two buttons a person would press **up to 10 seconds** before they were aware of deciding. The prediction was imperfect — around 60% accuracy, not perfect — but significantly above chance.

Ten seconds is not a neurological hiccup. That's enough time to have a conversation.

---

## The Objections

The scientific and philosophical response has been vigorous, and genuinely unresolved.

**The readiness potential might not be what we think.** Aaron Schurger's 2012 reanalysis proposed that the readiness potential isn't "preparation to move" — it's random fluctuations in neural noise that, when they cross a threshold, produce movement. People report deciding at the moment the noise crosses that threshold. This would mean there's no single "decision point" at all, just ongoing fluctuation that tips into action.

**The clock is unreliable.** Asking someone to note when they "feel like" moving is a strange introspective task. The act of watching a clock and reporting a moment may itself distort when the experience actually occurred. Consciousness might be doing more than Libet gave it credit for.

**Wrist flexing isn't choice.** Critics argue that Libet studied arbitrary, meaningless actions — the experimental equivalent of fidgeting. Real decisions involve reasons, deliberation, context. Generalizing from wrist flexes to "free will doesn't exist" may be the kind of leap that makes philosophers wince.

**The neural correlate isn't the cause.** Just because brain activity precedes reported awareness doesn't mean consciousness is epiphenomenal. The relationship between neural activity and subjective experience is itself the hard problem — you can't read one off the other cleanly.

---

## Why It Won't Go Away

These objections are real. The experiment has genuine limitations. But none of them close the question.

Here's the uncomfortable thing: we don't have a good account of how conscious choice could *cause* action through physical brain processes even if it does. The mechanism would require mental events — subjective experience — to feed back into the physical system in a way that doesn't violate what we know about neurons. Neuroscience has no agreed model for how that works.

So the debate sits in an uncomfortable place. The strong version of Libet's conclusion (free will is an illusion, consciousness is just observing) is probably overreach. But the easy dismissal (the experiment is flawed, free will is fine) papers over a gap that hasn't been closed.

We do not have a scientific account of how conscious deliberation causes action. We have neural correlates. We have timing measurements. We have competing interpretations. We don't have the mechanism.

---

## What Philosophers Do With It

The free will debate predates Libet by a few thousand years, and neuroscience has not settled it.

**Hard determinists** say the brain is a physical system, physical systems follow causal laws, and "you" deciding something is just a story the brain tells about itself after the fact. Libet is one data point in a larger argument.

**Compatibilists** — probably the majority position in academic philosophy — say free will was never about uncaused causation. It was about acting for reasons, without coercion, in accordance with your own values. None of that requires consciousness to jump the gun on the brain. You can have compatibilist free will in a fully deterministic universe.

**Libertarians about free will** (the philosophical kind, not the political kind) hold out for genuine indeterminism — perhaps quantum effects in the brain, perhaps something else — that makes your choices genuinely open. This position requires a mechanism nobody has found.

The debate is active. The positions are sophisticated. Nobody has won.

---

## Where It Sits

The Libet experiment created a genuine puzzle and the puzzle remains. The readiness potential precedes conscious intention. Whether that means consciousness is an observer rather than an actor, or whether it means we're measuring the wrong thing, or whether "free will" was always a confused concept — these questions are open.

What's not open: the measurement. Your brain is not waiting for you to decide before it starts moving. What exactly that means about you is the question nobody has closed.

You're living this experiment right now. Every decision you make, the brain has already begun its preparation. Whether that matters for how you live, or just for how you talk about living — that one's up to you.

Or it was always going to be.
