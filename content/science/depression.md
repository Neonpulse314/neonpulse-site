You probably learned it in health class, or read it on the back of a prescription insert, or heard it explained on a pharmaceutical ad with a cartoon brain and a sad face.

Depression is a chemical imbalance. Specifically, not enough serotonin.

That explanation was wrong. Or at least almost certainly not the whole story. And the field of psychiatry is still dealing with what to say instead.

---

## Where the Idea Came From

The serotonin hypothesis wasn't born from careful neurochemical research. It was born from accidents.

In the 1950s, doctors noticed that iproniazid — a drug developed to treat tuberculosis — made some patients unexpectedly euphoric. Around the same time, imipramine, developed as an antihistamine, appeared to lift the mood of severely depressed patients in hospital trials. Both drugs happened to affect monoamine neurotransmitters, including serotonin and norepinephrine.

The backward inference: if drugs that raise these chemicals help depression, maybe depression is caused by low levels of those chemicals.

It's the kind of logic that feels solid until you examine it. Aspirin relieves headaches. That doesn't mean headaches are caused by aspirin deficiency.

In 1965, psychiatrist Joseph Schildkraut formalized it as the "catecholamine hypothesis." By the 1980s, it had narrowed to serotonin as the primary culprit — partly because serotonin-specific drugs were easier to develop, and partly because they had fewer side effects than older antidepressants.

In 1987, Eli Lilly launched Prozac. Within a decade it was one of the bestselling drugs in history. The company's marketing strategy, and the FDA-approved labeling, centered on "restoring the balance of serotonin."

The phrase "chemical imbalance" entered the culture. It spread through patient advocacy materials, through physician education, through popular books and magazine articles. It became one of the most widely believed scientific facts of the late 20th century.

---

## The Review That Upended the Story

In July 2022, psychiatrist Joanna Moncrieff and her colleagues published a paper in *Molecular Psychiatry* — one of the most widely read journals in psychiatry. It was an umbrella review: a review of reviews. They looked at the six main lines of evidence that had been used to support the serotonin hypothesis over the previous decades.

The conclusion: **there is no consistent evidence that depression is caused by lower serotonin concentrations or activity.**

Studies comparing serotonin levels between depressed and non-depressed people found no reliable difference. Depletion studies — which artificially lower serotonin in healthy volunteers — found no consistent increase in depression. Genetic studies looking at serotonin-related genes found no significant link. The largest and most rigorous studies available showed no connection.

The paper was not fringe. Moncrieff is a professor at University College London. The review was peer-reviewed and published in a major journal. It didn't argue that SSRIs don't work. It argued that the explanation for *why* they might work was almost certainly wrong.

The reaction was enormous. Psychiatrists argued about methodology. Some defended the hypothesis. Others said the hypothesis had already been quietly abandoned within the field years earlier — which raised its own uncomfortable question: if researchers knew, why did the marketing keep going?

---

## The Problem: The Drugs Still Work

Here's what makes this genuinely strange. Even if the serotonin hypothesis is wrong, SSRIs — drugs specifically designed to increase serotonin availability — help a significant number of people with depression.

Not everyone. Meta-analyses suggest they work better than placebo for moderate-to-severe depression, and the effect size is meaningful for severe cases. Millions of people report real improvement. Some report that these drugs changed their lives.

If depression isn't caused by low serotonin, why do serotonin-affecting drugs help?

This is not a rhetorical question. Nobody knows the answer.

Several possibilities are being explored:

**Neurogenesis.** Research since the early 2000s (Bhagya et al., 2005) has suggested that SSRIs may promote the growth of new neurons in the hippocampus — a region associated with memory, stress regulation, and emotion. If depression involves hippocampal shrinkage under chronic stress, perhaps the mechanism is neurogenesis, not serotonin signaling. SSRIs happen to trigger both.

**Neuroinflammation.** A growing body of evidence links depression to elevated inflammatory markers — cytokines, C-reactive protein. Psychiatrists Charles Raison and Andrew Miller have argued that depression may in many cases be a response to chronic immune activation, a misfired adaptation originally designed to cope with infection and social threat. SSRIs have some anti-inflammatory properties. Maybe that's the relevant mechanism.

**Network connectivity.** Brain imaging studies show that depression involves disrupted connectivity between regions — particularly the default mode network (associated with rumination and self-referential thought) and prefrontal regulation circuits. SSRIs and other antidepressants may gradually normalize this connectivity, independent of any direct serotonin effect.

**Ketamine.** The most striking recent data in depression research involves a drug that has nothing to do with serotonin. Ketamine — an anesthetic and dissociative drug — produces rapid antidepressant effects within hours, sometimes after years of failed treatment with conventional drugs. It works on glutamate receptors, not serotonin. The FDA approved a ketamine-derived nasal spray (esketamine) for treatment-resistant depression in 2019. If serotonin were the mechanism for depression, ketamine shouldn't work at all. It does.

## The Deeper Problem: Maybe "Depression" Isn't One Thing

The strangest feature of the depression research landscape is that the disease itself may not be a coherent category.

"Depression" as defined in the DSM-5 requires five or more of nine possible symptoms — depressed mood, loss of interest, weight changes, sleep disturbance, psychomotor changes, fatigue, worthlessness, concentration problems, suicidal ideation — persisting for at least two weeks. By that criteria, you can have two patients with a diagnosis of "major depressive disorder" who share only one symptom: depressed mood.

That's not a problem unique to depression — medicine is full of syndrome categories that may eventually split. But it matters enormously for research. If "depression" is actually ten different conditions that produce similar surface presentations, then looking for a single biological cause or a single effective treatment is the wrong approach.

Some researchers argue this is exactly what the evidence shows. Studies find that antidepressants work better for severe melancholic depression than for mild situational depression. Inflammatory markers predict response to some treatments but not others. Ketamine helps treatment-resistant cases that SSRIs don't touch.

The treatment picture is similarly fragmented. Cognitive-behavioral therapy works for many people. Exercise has antidepressant effects comparable to medication in some trials (Blumenthal et al., 1999). Sleep deprivation — temporarily keeping depressed patients awake — produces rapid improvement in a subset of patients. Psilocybin (from "magic mushrooms") is showing promising results in clinical trials. All of these work through different mechanisms. All of them help some people and not others.

Maybe the question isn't "what causes depression" but "which of the several things we're calling depression does this person have?"

## What We Do Know

Depression affects approximately **280 million people** worldwide. It's the leading cause of disability globally according to the WHO. Suicide is among the top causes of death for people aged 15–29.

The first effective antidepressants were discovered by accident in the 1950s. The current ones — developed using the chemical imbalance framework — work better than placebo for many people, but the remission rates are not high. The landmark STAR*D trial, the largest antidepressant study ever conducted, found that only about **30%** of patients achieved remission with their first medication. After trying multiple medications and combinations, the cumulative remission rate after a year was still under 70% — and relapse rates are high.

There is no biomarker for depression. There is no blood test. No brain scan. Diagnosis is entirely symptom-based. This is not a failure of clinical practice — it reflects a genuine absence of understanding about what the disease is at a biological level.

The serotonin hypothesis was compelling because it was simple, it came with a treatment, and it could be explained in a sentence. The truth appears to be far messier: a cluster of conditions involving interacting systems — stress hormones, immune activation, neural circuitry, neuroplasticity, genetics — that we're only beginning to map.

---

## Why It Matters That We Got It Wrong

The chemical imbalance narrative did real harm, beyond the scientific.

It encouraged people to frame recovery as medication management rather than behavioral change, social support, or addressing underlying stressors. It stigmatized those who didn't respond to medication as having a more severe imbalance rather than possibly a different condition. It discouraged research into non-pharmacological approaches for decades. And when patients heard that their imbalance was corrected but still felt depressed, many concluded they were the problem.

The 2022 Moncrieff review wasn't the end of the story. It was an honest accounting of what had been claimed versus what the evidence actually showed. That kind of accounting is what science is supposed to do.

The uncomfortable truth is that psychiatry has been treating one of the most common and disabling conditions in the world with drugs whose mechanism is not understood, targeting a cause that probably isn't the right one, producing partial results that no one can fully explain.

And the people taking those drugs, in many cases, are still getting better.

That's not a failure of medicine. It's a window into how much of medicine operates — empirically, pragmatically, honestly uncertain about the why. But it's worth knowing. Because 280 million people deserve more than a chemical imbalance narrative that was always more marketing than mechanism.
