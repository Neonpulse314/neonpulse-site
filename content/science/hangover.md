You wake up, and before you open your eyes, you already know. The light is too loud. Your mouth tastes like old carpet. Your head is running a percussion section you didn't agree to. If you've ever had a hangover, you know the feeling — and at some point you've probably wondered what's actually happening in your body to produce it.

The short answer is: several things at once, which is partly why hangovers are so hard to treat.

## The Word Has a History

The clinical term is **veisalgia** — from the Norwegian *kveis* (uneasiness after debauchery) combined with the Greek *algia* (pain). Nobody really uses it. But it's a useful reminder that this is a well-documented physiological state, not just "feeling bad." Researchers have been studying hangovers systematically for decades, and the picture that's emerged is surprisingly complex.

## The Acetaldehyde Problem

The central player is a molecule called **acetaldehyde**.

When your liver processes alcohol (ethanol), it doesn't just break it down in one step. An enzyme called alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) converts ethanol into acetaldehyde first. Then a second enzyme, aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH2), converts acetaldehyde into acetate — a harmless compound your body uses for energy.

The problem is that acetaldehyde is toxic. It's estimated to be about 30 times more toxic than ethanol itself. Under normal circumstances, ALDH2 converts it fast enough that levels stay low. But if you drink faster than your liver can keep up, acetaldehyde accumulates in your bloodstream. It causes flushing, nausea, rapid heartbeat, and headache.

This mechanism is also why some people have much worse reactions to alcohol than others. A common genetic variant of ALDH2 — found in a large percentage of people with East Asian ancestry — produces an enzyme that's significantly less efficient at clearing acetaldehyde. The result is what's called the **Asian flush**: reddening, nausea, and discomfort after even small amounts of alcohol. It's not an alcohol allergy. It's acetaldehyde poisoning.

The drug disulfiram (Antabuse), used to treat alcohol dependence, works by deliberately blocking ALDH2 — making drinking so unpleasant it functions as a deterrent.

## Your Immune System Gets Involved

Separate from acetaldehyde, alcohol triggers a systemic inflammatory response.

Alcohol damages the gut lining, which allows bacterial products called **lipopolysaccharides** to leak into the bloodstream. The immune system detects these as foreign invaders and responds by releasing pro-inflammatory cytokines — signaling proteins including IL-1β, IL-6, and tumor necrosis factor (TNF-α). These are the same molecules responsible for the fatigue, brain fog, muscle aches, and general misery of influenza.

This is not a metaphor. The reason a bad hangover feels like the flu is because the immune mechanisms are partially overlapping. Anti-inflammatory medications (like ibuprofen) can blunt some hangover symptoms for exactly this reason.

Studies measuring cytokine levels in people experiencing hangovers have found significantly elevated concentrations compared to baseline — and those elevated cytokine levels correlate with hangover severity. People who report worse hangovers tend to have stronger inflammatory responses to alcohol.

## Dehydration — Real, but Overstated

Everyone knows alcohol causes dehydration. The mechanism is real but often misunderstood.

Alcohol suppresses a hormone called **vasopressin** (also called antidiuretic hormone, ADH — confusingly the same abbreviation as alcohol dehydrogenase). Vasopressin normally tells your kidneys to reabsorb water. When alcohol suppresses it, your kidneys stop reclaiming water efficiently, and you produce much more urine than you take in as liquid. That's why you're in the bathroom constantly after a few drinks, and why you wake up dehydrated.

But here's the nuance: researchers have measured how much fluid loss actually occurs and found it's rarely severe enough to explain the full severity of a hangover. Dehydration contributes — especially to headache and lethargy — but it's probably not the dominant cause. Drinking water before bed helps, but it doesn't prevent a hangover.

## The Glutamate Rebound

Alcohol is an inhibitor of **NMDA receptors** — one of the main types of glutamate receptors in the brain. Glutamate is the brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter. When you drink, the brain compensates for the inhibitory effect of alcohol by upregulating glutamate receptors and producing more glutamate.

When the alcohol clears your system — which typically happens in the early morning hours — your brain suddenly has too much excitatory activity. This is the glutamate rebound, and it's responsible for some of the more unpleasant hangover symptoms: **heightened sensitivity to light and sound**, anxiety, and difficulty getting back to sleep.

This mechanism is the same one that drives severe alcohol withdrawal in dependent drinkers — just at a milder level. It also partially explains why "hair of the dog" (drinking more alcohol to treat a hangover) temporarily relieves symptoms: a small amount of alcohol re-inhibits the glutamate system and calms the rebound. It delays rather than cures, and most clinicians advise against it.

## Sleep Disruption

Alcohol helps you fall asleep — this part most people know. What's less obvious is what it does to sleep quality.

Alcohol suppresses REM sleep (rapid eye movement sleep) during the first half of the night. REM is the restorative phase associated with memory consolidation and emotional processing. As alcohol metabolizes and blood levels drop, there's a **REM rebound** in the second half of the night — vivid, fragmented dreams and frequent waking. Net result: you sleep a full night and wake up feeling like you didn't sleep at all.

This is measurable on polysomnography. Studies consistently show fragmented sleep architecture after drinking, even moderate amounts.

## Blood Sugar Crashes

The liver is your primary site for **gluconeogenesis** — manufacturing glucose from non-sugar sources when blood glucose drops. But when you've been drinking, the liver is busy processing alcohol and can't prioritize glucose production efficiently.

The result can be mild **hypoglycemia**: low blood sugar that contributes to shakiness, weakness, irritability, and difficulty concentrating the next morning. This is part of why eating — especially carbohydrates — can make you feel somewhat better after a hangover.

## Congeners: The Dark Liquor Effect

Different alcoholic drinks aren't just different concentrations of ethanol. Fermentation and aging produce a range of byproducts — trace amounts of methanol, isopropanol, acetone, tannins, and other compounds collectively called **congeners**. Darker drinks like bourbon, whiskey, brandy, and red wine contain significantly more congeners than clear spirits like vodka or gin.

In 2010, Rohsenow and colleagues published a well-designed study in *Alcoholism: Clinical and Experimental Research* comparing hangovers from bourbon versus vodka, with alcohol intake controlled to reach the same blood alcohol concentration. Bourbon drinkers reported significantly worse hangovers. Cognitive performance the next day was impaired more after bourbon.

This isn't about alcohol content — it's about what comes along for the ride. The exact mechanisms by which congeners worsen hangovers aren't fully characterized, but the effect is robust enough that it shows up consistently in research.

## What Actually Helps

This is where the science is both honest and a little disappointing: **there is no proven cure for a hangover**. Multiple substances have been tested — aspirin, vitamin B6, various herbal preparations, activated charcoal, electrolyte drinks — and none have shown reliable evidence of significant hangover prevention or treatment in controlled trials.

What helps, based on evidence:
- **Time**: most hangovers resolve within 24 hours as the body clears acetaldehyde and inflammation settles
- **Water**: addresses the dehydration component (helps, doesn't cure)
- **Electrolytes**: replaces what was lost through diuresis
- **Food**: stabilizes blood sugar, gives the liver something else to work with
- **Ibuprofen** (not acetaminophen): has anti-inflammatory effects; acetaminophen is metabolized by the liver via pathways that interact with alcohol metabolism, increasing toxicity risk
- **Sleep**: gives the brain time to complete REM rebound cycles

Coffee — despite its popularity — doesn't eliminate hangover symptoms, though caffeine can temporarily address fatigue. It may worsen dehydration.

## The Honest Mystery

After all this, it's worth noting that the science of hangovers is still incomplete. Hangover severity varies enormously between individuals who drink the same amount. Twin studies suggest genetic factors explain a substantial portion of this variation — beyond just ALDH2 variants. But the specific genetic architecture of hangover susceptibility hasn't been mapped.

There's also debate about whether hangovers serve any evolutionary purpose or whether they're simply the biological consequence of consuming a molecule the body was never designed to handle at high doses. The answer to that is almost certainly the latter — but it's not entirely settled.

What is settled: the next morning, your body is dealing with toxic metabolites, an activated immune system, disrupted neurotransmitter balance, poor sleep, low blood sugar, and dehydration, all at once. The conviction that you'll never drink again is entirely rational. It just tends to fade.
