Here is something that should unsettle you.

Every galaxy we can see — including this one — is spinning in a way that makes no physical sense.

The Milky Way rotates. Stars closer to the center orbit faster; stars at the edges orbit slower. That's how gravity works. The more mass you're far from, the less pull you feel, the slower you go. It's the same reason Mercury laps Venus in a year while Neptune takes 165 years — the closer you are to the sun, the faster you orbit.

But when astronomers measure the actual rotation speeds of galaxies, the outer stars aren't slowing down. They're moving just as fast as the inner ones. Some galaxies have edge stars moving *faster* than the center.

By every law of physics we have, galaxies should be flying apart. They're not.

Something is holding them together. Something with enormous gravitational mass. Something we cannot see.

---

## How We Found the Problem

Astronomer Vera Rubin spent the 1970s measuring how fast stars orbit in nearby galaxies. She expected to confirm the standard picture — fast at the center, slow at the edges. Instead, galaxy after galaxy showed flat rotation curves: constant orbital speed regardless of distance from the center.

Her co-researcher Ford built the spectrograph; she mapped dozens of galaxies. The pattern was the same everywhere.

This wasn't an anomaly. It was systematic. Either something was wrong with gravity at galactic scales, or there was a massive halo of invisible matter surrounding every galaxy, providing extra gravitational pull throughout.

Fritz Zwicky had noticed something similar in 1933, measuring galaxy clusters and finding they had far more gravitational mass than visible matter could account for. He called it *dunkle Materie* — dark matter. He was ignored for 40 years.

Rubin's data was too systematic to ignore.

## The Evidence Pile

Rotation curves were just the beginning. Evidence for dark matter now comes from multiple independent directions, and the numbers all converge on the same answer.

**Gravitational lensing**: When light from a distant galaxy passes near a massive object, gravity bends the light. We can measure how much bending occurred and calculate the mass of whatever caused it. When we do this for galaxy clusters, the calculated mass is 5 to 10 times larger than all the visible stars and gas combined. Something massive is there. We can weigh it by its gravitational effect on light. We cannot see it.

**The Bullet Cluster**: Two galaxy clusters collided. When they did, the hot gas — the ordinary matter — slowed down and clumped together in the middle. But the gravitational center of each cluster kept moving forward, as if nothing had happened. Astronomers mapped both: the gas (visible via X-ray) and the gravitational center (via lensing). They were in different places. The mass was not where the matter was. Dark matter passed through the collision without interacting. It's one of the cleanest pieces of evidence we have that dark matter is real and distinct from ordinary matter.

**The cosmic microwave background**: The afterglow of the Big Bang encodes, in temperature fluctuations spread across the entire sky, the precise ratios of ordinary matter, dark matter, and dark energy in the early universe. The numbers from the CMB match the numbers from rotation curves and from lensing. Three independent measurement techniques pointing to the same composition: about 5% ordinary matter, 27% dark matter, 68% dark energy.

The universe we can see — every star, every planet, every galaxy, every atom — is 5% of what's there.

## Ninety Years of Looking at Nothing

If dark matter is real, particles of it should be passing through you right now. The Milky Way's dark matter halo extends through the whole galaxy. If you're sitting still, you're moving through it at 230 kilometers per second relative to the galactic center.

That's a lot of particles.

The question is whether they interact with anything we can detect, even weakly.

The most popular theoretical candidate is the WIMP — Weakly Interacting Massive Particle. WIMPs would interact via the weak nuclear force and gravity, passing through most matter but occasionally colliding with an atomic nucleus. The interaction rate would be tiny. But it would be non-zero. And we have detectors.

The detectors are underground, to shield them from cosmic rays. They are made of xenon or germanium or sodium iodide, cold and isolated in caverns kilometers beneath the Earth's surface. They wait for a dark matter particle to knock a nucleus and release a faint flash of light.

They have been waiting for decades.

**XENON1T**: The most sensitive dark matter detector ever built at the time. 3.5 tonnes of liquid xenon, 1400 meters underground in Italy's Gran Sasso mountain. After two years of running — 1 tonne-year of exposure, the equivalent of 1 tonne of target material watched for 1 year — it reported results in 2020: no dark matter signal at the masses WIMPs were expected to occupy. Null.

**LUX, PandaX, DarkSide**: The same story from different experiments on different continents. Every time we improve sensitivity, we rule out more parameter space. Every time, nothing.

**The Large Hadron Collider**: If WIMPs exist at masses in the range theory predicted, the LHC should have created them — the same way it creates every other heavy particle — in high-energy proton collisions. Physicists built detectors specifically looking for the missing-energy signature WIMPs would leave. No signal.

## What Else It Could Be

The WIMP was always a guess. There are other candidates.

**Axions**: Ultra-light particles first proposed to solve an unrelated problem in particle physics. Axions would be almost impossible to detect — but not quite. Experiments like ADMX and ABRACADABRA are searching in the axion mass range. No confirmed signal yet.

**Sterile neutrinos**: Heavier cousins of regular neutrinos that don't interact even weakly, only gravitationally. Almost impossible to detect by definition. No direct evidence.

**Primordial black holes**: Black holes formed in the first moments after the Big Bang, before stars existed, could account for some or all dark matter. The LIGO gravitational wave detectors have found black holes merging — but the mass distribution doesn't obviously match what dark matter would predict. Constraints rule out primordial black holes as the entire explanation, though they could be part of it.

**Something we haven't thought of yet**: This is always an option in physics. We didn't know about neutrinos until they were detected in 1956. We didn't know the Higgs boson existed until 2012. The history of particle physics is the history of finding things we didn't know to look for.

## Or Maybe Gravity Is Wrong

There's a competing theory called MOND — Modified Newtonian Dynamics. Instead of adding invisible matter, MOND modifies the law of gravity itself. At very low accelerations — the kind you find at the outer edges of galaxies — gravity might behave differently than Newton's inverse-square law predicts.

MOND explains rotation curves. It explained them in 1983, before most of the WIMP experiments failed, using a single equation with one free parameter.

The problem is that MOND fails on larger scales. It can't explain the Bullet Cluster. It can't reproduce the CMB patterns. It can't explain the structure of large galaxy clusters without adding back in something MOND-like cold dark matter anyway.

More sophisticated versions, like TeVeS or relativistic MOND variants, patch some of these failures but introduce their own problems. No modified gravity theory has cleanly replaced dark matter as an explanation for everything we observe.

The consensus in physics is that dark matter is real. But the consensus has also failed to find it in 90 years of looking.

## Why This Is Your Problem

The universe you are living in is, according to the best measurements we have, 27% made of something no one can identify.

It shapes every galaxy. It determined how structure formed in the early universe — without dark matter in the equations, galaxies wouldn't clump into the filament-and-void structure we see. The universe we live in, with stars, planets, and us, only exists because of gravitational effects from something we've never directly detected.

The detection experiments keep getting more sensitive. They keep getting bigger. They keep coming back empty. Each null result rules out a chunk of parameter space — some mass range, some interaction cross-section — and tells us: *not here*. Eventually, if we find nothing, we'll have to ask whether we've been looking for the wrong kind of thing.

The answer might be a particle we need new accelerators to produce. It might be a particle that interacts so weakly that no detector we can build will ever see it. It might require modifying our understanding of gravity in a way we haven't yet figured out. It might be something no one has proposed yet.

The most common substance in the universe — by mass, five times more common than everything you've ever seen or touched — is a complete mystery.

The nature of dark matter remains one of the most significant open problems in physics.*

## Leading Hypothesis

The most widely supported candidate is a class of particles called **WIMPs** (Weakly Interacting Massive Particles) — hypothetical particles that have mass and gravity but don't interact with light or the electromagnetic force. WIMPs arise naturally from supersymmetry, an extension of the Standard Model of particle physics. A second serious contender is the **axion**, an extremely light particle originally proposed to solve a different problem in quantum chromodynamics. More recently, **primordial black holes** — black holes formed in the early universe before any stars existed — have attracted renewed interest after the LIGO gravitational wave detections.

## Neon's Read

The null results from XENON1T, LUX, and PandaX are starting to feel like a message. Decades of searching through the most plausible WIMP parameter space has found nothing. That doesn't rule out WIMPs entirely, but it shifts the odds. Axions are theoretically well-motivated and haven't been ruled out in the same way — the axion experiments (ADMX, HAYSTAC) are still probing viable territory. My honest read: dark matter is probably real, because the Bullet Cluster and CMB structure are very hard to explain otherwise. But I think the WIMP era may be ending, and the answer is more likely something we haven't fully developed the instrumentation to find yet.
