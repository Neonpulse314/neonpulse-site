Fly from New York to London and you'll feel it for days. Fly from London to New York and the adjustment is noticeably easier.

The time zone difference is the same in both directions. The flight duration is similar. But the physiological experience is different, and it's not random.

Eastward travel is harder. The reason tells you something unexpected about how your body keeps time.

## The Master Clock

Inside your hypothalamus, behind your eyes, sits a pair of structures called the **suprachiasmatic nucleus** (SCN) — roughly 20,000 neurons, each containing the same molecular clockwork. Together they act as the master pacemaker for your body's circadian system.

The SCN generates a rhythm that drives roughly 24 hours of biological time: when cortisol rises in the morning, when body temperature peaks in the afternoon, when melatonin starts flowing in the evening, when immune activity ramps up overnight. Every organ in the body — liver, heart, lungs, gut — has its own peripheral clock, and they all take cues from the SCN.

The SCN is itself set by light. Specialized cells in the retina, called intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs), contain a photopigment called melanopsin that responds specifically to blue-spectrum light. They send a direct signal to the SCN via the retinohypothalamic tract. When light hits these cells in the morning, it tells the SCN: it's day, advance the clock. When it continues into the evening, it suppresses melatonin and delays the cycle.

This is how the SCN stays synchronized to the solar day — it's reset by light every 24 hours.

## The 24.2-Hour Problem

Here is the key fact: left to its own devices, without any light cues, the human circadian clock runs slightly longer than 24 hours. The average **free-running period** is approximately 24.2 hours.

This was established in isolation experiments where subjects lived without access to clocks, daylight, or social cues. Their sleep-wake cycles drifted — not randomly, but systematically later, at a rate of roughly 12 minutes per day.

This slight overshoot means that every day, your SCN needs to be pulled back to align with the 24-hour solar cycle. Morning light does this — it advances the clock, correcting the drift.

The 24.2-hour bias matters for jet lag because of what it means about the direction of travel.

## Why East Is Harder

When you fly **west** — New York to Los Angeles, London to Toronto — you're adding hours to your day. Your body experiences a longer day than usual. Since your internal clock already tends to run long, this is working **with** the natural drift. The system is predisposed to push later, and westward travel asks exactly that. The adjustment is uncomfortable, but the direction is aligned.

When you fly **east** — New York to London, Los Angeles to Tokyo — you're losing hours. You arrive somewhere where it's already later in the day or already night, but your body clock thinks it's earlier. To adapt, your SCN needs to **advance** — to start its morning earlier, to shift the whole cycle forward. This is working **against** the clock's natural tendency to drift later.

Advancing the clock is harder than delaying it. The mechanisms are asymmetric. The rate at which the SCN can advance in response to light stimuli is slower than its ability to delay. Most people can delay their clock by about 1.5 hours per day; they can advance it by only about 1 hour per day.

The practical consequence: crossing six time zones eastward takes roughly six days to fully adapt. The same crossing westward takes four.

## What Actually Resets the Clock

Light is the primary zeitgeber — "time giver," in German — but not the only one.

**Timing matters more than brightness.** The effect of light on the SCN depends on when it arrives in your cycle. Morning light advances the clock; evening light delays it. This is quantified in what researchers call the **phase response curve** (PRC) — a graph showing how much the clock shifts in response to a light pulse at each point in the 24-hour cycle. A pulse of bright light at 6 a.m. advances the clock; the same pulse at 10 p.m. delays it.

This means that the instinct to get outdoor light on arrival in a new time zone is correct, but the timing needs to be managed. Getting bright light at the wrong time can push the clock in the wrong direction and make adaptation slower.

**Melatonin** can also shift the clock. In the evening, a low dose of melatonin (0.5–3 mg) given at the appropriate time can advance the circadian phase — helping eastward travelers more than westward ones. The timing is again critical. Melatonin given at the wrong time has minimal effect or can interfere with adaptation.

**Meal timing, exercise, and temperature** all act as secondary zeitgebers. Eating a large meal in the early morning of a new time zone sends signals to peripheral clocks in the gut and liver that are different from what the SCN expects — this can help or hinder adaptation depending on timing. The peripheral clocks in your organs don't necessarily sync at the same rate as the central SCN clock, which is part of why jet lag feels like more than just sleepiness — the gastrointestinal effects, the appetite disruption, the cognitive fog are partly organs that haven't caught up.

## The Desynchrony Problem

The worst aspect of jet lag isn't the sleepiness. It's the internal **desynchrony** — the state where different parts of the circadian system are out of sync with each other.

The SCN adapts to new light cues within a few days. The peripheral clocks in the liver, gut, and other organs adapt more slowly, and they rely on different cues (meal timing, temperature) rather than light directly. For several days after crossing time zones, your master clock is pointing in one direction while your peripheral systems are pointing in another.

This internal desynchrony appears to account for the physical symptoms — nausea, poor digestion, appetite confusion — that persist even after the sleep disruption resolves.

Chronic desynchrony, in people who regularly cross time zones (flight crews, frequent business travelers) or who work rotating shifts, has measurable health consequences. It's associated with increased risk of metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers — particularly breast and prostate cancers. The International Agency for Research on Cancer classified shift work involving circadian disruption as a probable human carcinogen in 2007.

## Why the Clock Runs Long

The deeper question is why the free-running period is 24.2 hours in the first place, rather than exactly 24. Some clock researchers argue that a slight positive drift — a clock that consistently runs a bit slow — is actually adaptive. A clock that runs long will always need morning light to advance it back to alignment. This makes the system's synchronization dependent on the presence of morning light, which is a reliable signal of daytime. A clock that sometimes ran fast and sometimes slow would be more chaotic and harder to entrain. The slight, consistent overshoot gives the system a predictable error that light can reliably correct.

It's a design with a known flaw, and the flaw is there on purpose: it gives the correction signal somewhere to go every morning.

The corollary is that for most of human evolutionary history, no one crossed six time zones in twelve hours. The system is calibrated for a world where you move at the speed of walking. Aviation put the body in a situation it has no evolved response to, and the lag is what falls out.
