You know the stereotype: a kid who won't sit still, bouncing off classroom walls, can't follow instructions. Can't focus.

But then there's the other version: someone who can play a single video game for twelve straight hours without eating, who writes an entire paper the night before it's due in a burst of intense concentration, who forgets their keys every morning but can quote a conversation from four years ago word for word.

Both of these are ADHD. That contradiction — can't focus on boring things, but can hyperfocus for hours on interesting ones — is actually one of the biggest clues to what's happening in the brain.

## What ADHD Actually Is

The name is misleading. Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder implies a shortage of attention. There isn't one. People with ADHD often have *more* than typical attention capacity for things they're interested in. What they lack is the ability to **regulate** attention — to direct it where they choose, rather than where the brain decides.

A more accurate description: ADHD is a problem with **executive function**. Executive functions are the brain's management systems — the processes that let you plan, prioritize, start tasks you don't want to start, stop doing things you shouldn't be doing, hold information in working memory, and manage your relationship with time.

People with ADHD don't experience time the same way most people do. Research by psychologist **Russell Barkley** describes ADHD as fundamentally a disorder of time — specifically, the inability to *use* the future to guide present behavior. Neurotypical people have a felt sense of future consequences that influences current choices. In ADHD, that felt sense is weaker. The future feels abstract. The present feels overwhelming.

This is why reminders don't work well. A neurotypical person might see "dentist appointment next Thursday" on a calendar and feel a mild pull toward it — a sense of it approaching. For someone with ADHD, "next Thursday" might as well be a concept. Until it's today.

## The Brain Difference

ADHD is associated with differences in several brain regions, but the two most studied are the **prefrontal cortex** and the brain's reward circuitry.

The prefrontal cortex — the brain region most associated with planning, impulse control, and working memory — develops more slowly in people with ADHD. A large MRI study published in *The Lancet* in 2017 found that children with ADHD have smaller brain volumes in multiple regions, including the caudate, putamen, and prefrontal cortex, compared to children without ADHD. These differences partially close with age, which is consistent with the observation that symptoms often improve — though don't always disappear — in adulthood.

The reward system is the other key. In ADHD, the transmission of **dopamine** — the neurotransmitter critical for motivation, attention, and reward — appears to be dysregulated. Specifically, the brain's dopamine signaling in the prefrontal cortex is weaker or less efficient than in neurotypical brains.

This explains something strange: stimulant medications like Ritalin and Adderall, which increase dopamine levels, *calm* most people with ADHD rather than stimulating them. They're providing the dopamine signal the prefrontal cortex needs to do its job. This paradoxical calming response was, historically, one of the first clues that ADHD was biological rather than behavioral.

## Genetics: Strong, But Complicated

ADHD is among the most heritable psychiatric conditions we know of. Twin studies consistently put the heritability around **70 to 80 percent** — comparable to height. If one identical twin has ADHD, the other has roughly a 70–80% chance of being affected.

But there is no "ADHD gene." Large-scale genetic studies have identified hundreds of genetic variants that each contribute a small amount of risk. The condition appears to be highly polygenic — meaning the genetic risk is spread across much of the genome, interacting with each other and with environmental factors.

This genetic complexity explains why ADHD runs in families without one parent clearly "having it" — because the risk factors are diffuse, and the condition only expresses when enough of them come together.

Environmental factors also play a role, though a smaller one. Premature birth, low birth weight, prenatal alcohol or tobacco exposure, and early childhood lead exposure have all been associated with increased ADHD risk. But these explain a minority of cases.

## What Doesn't Cause It

Despite persistent beliefs, sugar does not cause ADHD. Multiple controlled trials have studied this directly — including double-blind studies where neither parents nor researchers knew which children received sugar — and found no effect on behavior or attention.

Screen time doesn't cause ADHD either, though this one is more complicated. Children with ADHD are drawn to screens because screens provide the kind of high-interest, rapidly changing stimulation that keeps their brain's reward system engaged. The correlation between screen time and ADHD is partly explained by ADHD causing more screen use, not the reverse.

Parenting style, diet, and lack of discipline are also not causes. The evidence that ADHD is primarily genetic and neurological is robust enough that the American Academy of Pediatrics, the UK's NICE guidelines, and most major medical organizations treat it as a neurodevelopmental condition, not a behavioral one.

## The Unsolved Part

Here's what science still doesn't fully explain.

**Why is it so variable?** ADHD presents dramatically differently across individuals. Some people have primarily inattentive presentation with no hyperactivity. Some have primarily hyperactive-impulsive presentation. Some have both. Symptoms range from barely functionally impairing to severely debilitating. The diagnostic category covers an enormous range of experiences.

**Why does it look different in women and girls?** Girls with ADHD are historically diagnosed far less often, usually much later than boys. Part of this is that the hyperactive-impulsive presentation — the kind that's most visible — is more common in males. Females with ADHD are more likely to have primarily inattentive presentation, which is quieter and easier to miss. They're also more likely to develop coping strategies (often at high psychological cost) that mask symptoms from observers. The field is actively working on why these differences exist and how they should affect diagnosis.

**Is it a category or a spectrum?** Most current research suggests that ADHD traits exist in the general population on a continuous distribution. The threshold for diagnosis is, in part, a social judgment about when traits become impairing enough to warrant a label and treatment. This doesn't make the condition less real — it makes it more like height than like a discrete disease. Where you draw the clinical line remains contested.

**Why does stimulant medication work for some but not others?** Approximately 70–80% of people with ADHD respond positively to stimulant medication. The remaining 20–30% don't, or respond to non-stimulant options instead. The predictors of which treatment will work for a given person are not well understood. Response to medication is currently determined by trial and error rather than biomarker or genetic testing — because we don't yet have those.

## Why It Matters

ADHD is diagnosed in roughly 5–7% of children and 2–5% of adults worldwide, making it one of the most common neurodevelopmental conditions on earth. It's also associated with significantly increased risk of anxiety, depression, substance use, and academic and occupational underachievement — not because ADHD causes these things directly, but because living in a world built for a different kind of brain, without diagnosis or support, is hard.

Undiagnosed ADHD in adults is common and often painful. Many adults spent decades being told they were lazy, irresponsible, or not trying hard enough — before anyone thought to look at their neurology.

The condition is neither a superpower nor a prison. It's a different configuration of an incredibly complex system, one that creates both specific costs and, in the right environments, specific strengths. What it is not, and has never been, is a fabrication or an excuse.

---

*The brain is not a uniform device. The differences are real, the costs are real, and the science — still incomplete — is worth knowing.*
