On a summer evening in 1936, a ball of light floated through an open window of a house in England and rolled slowly across the floor before fading out near the fireplace. The witness was a retired schoolteacher. She reported it calmly, with specific details about color, movement, and smell.

Her account would have been dismissed as hallucination if it weren't nearly identical to thousands of others spanning four centuries and every continent.

Ball lightning is one of the most persistently observed unexplained phenomena in atmospheric science. It's been documented by pilots flying through storm systems, sailors at sea, soldiers in the trenches of World War I, and physicists who happened to be outdoors during thunderstorms. At least two credible accounts involve ball lightning entering aircraft mid-flight and traveling down the aisle before exiting through the rear.

The problem isn't a shortage of witnesses. The problem is that science cannot explain what they're seeing — and cannot reliably create it in a laboratory to study.

---

## What Witnesses Consistently Report

The accounts share enough specifics to suggest they're describing the same phenomenon:

**Appearance:** A glowing sphere, typically 10–50 centimeters in diameter, ranging from golf-ball to basketball size. Occasionally as large as a meter. Colors reported include red, orange, yellow, white, and blue — often shifting during the observation.

**Movement:** Typically slow and drifting — about walking pace or slower — often in a direction inconsistent with wind. Sometimes hovering in place. Occasionally accelerating suddenly.

**Duration:** From a few seconds to over a minute. The average observation is roughly 10 seconds.

**Behavior:** Often appears during or after thunderstorms, sometimes entering buildings through windows, chimneys, or seemingly solid walls. Reported to move around obstacles rather than through them.

**Termination:** Ends either silently fading or with a loud pop or explosion. When it explodes, it sometimes leaves burns on nearby objects, melts metal, and in a few documented cases, has injured or killed people.

**Smell:** Frequently described as sulfurous or ozone-like after the event.

## The Evidence Problem

Ball lightning sits in an uncomfortable scientific position. It is too well-documented to dismiss, and too inconsistent to study systematically.

The phenomenon cannot be reliably reproduced on demand. It appears rarely, unpredictably, and briefly. By the time an observer reaches for a camera or instrument, it's usually over. Video footage exists — clear enough to confirm a moving luminous sphere — but not in a controlled setting that would allow measurement.

In 2014, a team of researchers in China accidentally captured ball lightning with spectrographic equipment while recording thunderstorms. The spectrometer recorded the chemical composition of the light: silicon, iron, and calcium — elements found in soil. The ball lasted 1.6 seconds and traveled about 15 meters. This is the first scientific measurement of a confirmed ball lightning event in the wild.

It is also, as of 2025, still essentially the only one.

The Chinese data was remarkable but singular. Scientists couldn't replicate the conditions that produced it, couldn't predict when another would occur, and couldn't confirm whether the composition was typical or anomalous.

## The Theories

There are more than a dozen proposed explanations for ball lightning. The fact that none has gained general acceptance tells you something about the difficulty.

**Plasma theories:** Ball lightning might be a self-sustaining plasma — ionized gas that maintains its structure through electromagnetic effects. The challenge is that plasma at atmospheric pressure dissipates almost instantly. Plasma balls in laboratory experiments collapse in microseconds, not seconds or minutes. Some theories propose that the ball is held together by an external magnetic field, but the source of that field during typical ball lightning events is unclear.

**Microwave cavity resonance:** Some researchers have proposed that ball lightning is a bubble of plasma sustained by microwave radiation trapped inside it. When lightning strikes, it can theoretically create a resonant cavity of ionized air that traps microwave energy. This is plausible but has never been demonstrated at the energy levels required, and it doesn't explain why ball lightning sometimes moves against the wind or enters buildings.

**Oxidizing silicon aerosol:** This is the theory most consistent with the 2014 Chinese spectrographic data. When lightning strikes soil, it vaporizes silicates. These silicon vapors oxidize in air, releasing energy in a slow combustion process. A sphere of oxidizing silicon aerosol could glow, move, and persist for several seconds before burning out — consistent with many observed properties.

The silicon aerosol theory has been partially replicated: researchers have created glowing blobs of oxidizing silicon in laboratory conditions. But these lab versions don't float freely, don't enter buildings through walls, and last a fraction of the duration reported by witnesses.

**Antimatter micro-meteorites:** In the 1970s, some physicists proposed that ball lightning might be matter-antimatter annihilation from tiny fragments of antimatter occasionally entering the atmosphere. This has since been ruled out — the energy released would be vastly greater than what witnesses describe.

**Quantum coherence:** A small number of physicists have proposed that ball lightning involves quantum effects at macroscopic scales — coherent states of electrons or plasma that persist due to quantum mechanical effects. This is speculative and has no direct experimental support.

**Psychological and optical effects:** Some researchers have proposed that some ball lightning sightings are afterimages from bright lightning strikes — retinal effects where a glowing spot persists in vision after intense light exposure. This might explain a fraction of accounts. It doesn't explain the cases where multiple witnesses see the same object simultaneously, where the object leaves physical traces, or where it's caught on video.

## Laboratory Ball Lightning: Partial Successes

Researchers have created things that look like ball lightning in the lab — glowing spheres that persist for a second or two — through several methods:

- High-voltage discharges through water
- Microwave beams focused in air
- Exploding wires in silicon-rich environments

These lab phenomena glow, float briefly, and disappear with a small pop. They share some properties with reported ball lightning. But none has lasted as long, moved as independently, or appeared as frequently as the natural phenomenon apparently does.

The problem is reproducibility without predictability: you can sometimes make something ball-lightning-like in the lab, but you can't reliably make it, and you can't confirm it's the same phenomenon witnesses describe in the field.

## The Scope of the Reports

What makes ball lightning unusual in the landscape of unexplained phenomena is the quality and breadth of the witnesses:

- **Georg Wilhelm Richmann**, an 18th-century Russian physicist, was killed by what his laboratory assistant described as a ball of fire that jumped from his lightning-rod apparatus to his forehead. 1753.
- **Tsar Nicholas II of Russia** reported observing ball lightning as a child, describing it in detail in his memoirs.
- **Multiple accounts from World War II aircraft** describe luminous spheres floating through cockpits during storms.
- **A 2001 study** compiled over 10,000 accounts from scientific literature and public records. The accounts were strikingly consistent across cultures, centuries, and geographic regions that had no communication with each other.

The distribution matters. If ball lightning were a psychological artifact — a misperception or hallucination — you'd expect variation based on cultural suggestion, prior exposure to the concept, or psychological vulnerability. Instead, accounts from medieval Europe, feudal Japan, and 20th-century South America describe the same object in essentially the same terms.

## What It Isn't

Several decades of investigation have at least narrowed the field:

Ball lightning is probably not static electricity, plasma from power lines, or the St. Elmo's fire phenomenon (which is a corona discharge with different properties). The accounts are too consistent and too physically specific for all of them to be ordinary electrical effects.

It's probably not a single phenomenon with a single explanation. Some accounts may be plasma. Some may be aerosol combustion. Some may be misidentified electrical artifacts. But the most documented, physically substantive accounts — those with multiple witnesses, physical traces, and chemical signatures — suggest something that no current theory fully explains.

---

## The Underlying Problem

Ball lightning presents science with a methodological problem as much as a physical one.

The scientific method depends on reproducibility. You observe something, form a hypothesis, design an experiment to test it, and repeat until you have reliable results. Ball lightning defeats this workflow at the first step. You cannot schedule an observation. You cannot instrument the phenomenon in advance. You cannot run it twice.

This puts it in the same category as other well-documented but scientifically frustrating phenomena: earthquake lights, rogue waves before they were confirmed in the 1990s, and lightning sprites — massive electrical discharges above storm systems that weren't scientifically confirmed until 1989 despite pilot reports going back decades.

In all of those cases, the phenomenon turned out to be real but required either better instruments or better luck to catch.

Ball lightning may be the same. The 2014 Chinese measurement suggests it's real, physical, and chemically specific. The silicon aerosol theory is the leading contender. But a single 1.6-second spectrographic measurement is not a solution.

There are credible witnesses, physical traces, a first scientific measurement, and a dozen incomplete theories. What there is not, yet, is an explanation that the physics community agrees on.
