Here's something that should make you uncomfortable.

You fire electrons — tiny particles — at a barrier with two slits. On the wall behind it, you'd expect to see two lines. One from each slit.

Instead, you see dozens of alternating bright and dark bands. An *interference pattern*. The kind you get when waves overlap — like two ripples crossing in a pond.

Which means: the electron went through both slits at once.

---

That's strange. But it gets worse.

Put a detector at one of the slits to watch which one the electron actually uses. It will go through one slit. The interference pattern disappears. The dozens of bands collapse into the two lines you expected originally.

The electron acts like a wave when you're not watching. Acts like a particle when you are.

**Watching it — changes what it does.**

---

This isn't a metaphor. It's not that we disturbed the electron with our detector. Physicists have done this experiment with increasingly gentle detectors, with delayed measurements, with setups so clever they could determine which slit the particle used *after it already hit the wall*. The result is the same: if the information about which slit is available anywhere in the universe, the interference pattern goes away.

The universe seems to care whether you know.

---

## What We Know (And What We Don't)

The mathematics works. Quantum mechanics predicts the double-slit result with perfect accuracy. The equation is solved. The numbers match.

The *meaning* is not solved.

Why does measurement collapse a quantum state? Nobody agrees. Here are the actual competing explanations that physicists argue about right now:

**Copenhagen interpretation**: The wave function isn't real — it's just a probability description. When you measure, you update your description. Reality is whatever we measure. (The problem: this says reality doesn't exist until you look, which bothers a lot of people.)

**Many-worlds interpretation**: The particle goes through both slits. The universe splits. In one universe, it went left. In another, it went right. You only experience one branch. There's no collapse — just branching. (The problem: this requires an incomprehensibly large number of universes, and there's no way to test it.)

**Pilot wave theory**: The particle is real, and a separate wave guides it. The wave goes through both slits; the particle only goes through one. Deterministic. No randomness. (The problem: it requires information to travel faster than light in certain situations, which Einstein would have hated.)

**Relational quantum mechanics**: Quantum states don't have absolute values — they're only real *relative to an observer*. Different observers can have different, equally valid descriptions of the same event. (The problem: it's genuinely hard to say what "observer" means, and whether a rock counts as one.)

They all predict the same experimental outcomes. They describe reality in completely different terms. There's currently no experiment that distinguishes between them.

---

## Why This Matters

The double-slit experiment isn't just weird physics trivia. It's a direct question about what reality is made of — and whether observation is somehow fundamental to it.

If measurement causes collapse, what counts as a measurement? Does a camera count? A rock? Does consciousness have to be involved? Most physicists say no — but there's no agreement on what does count, and the question has never been cleanly resolved.

Richard Feynman, who won the Nobel Prize in physics, said: *"If you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics."*

He wasn't being modest. He was describing the current state of the field.

---

## The Thing No One Can Explain

You can describe the experiment exactly. You can predict the results with perfect precision. You can build transistors and MRI machines and lasers using the same quantum principles.

And you cannot say — clearly, fully, without controversy — what it means for a particle to behave differently because it's being watched.

That's where the field is. Precise mathematics, unsolved meaning.

The question is still open.

---

*Next time you use a phone, a computer, an MRI machine, a laser — any of it — remember: the quantum mechanics underneath all of those technologies is built on equations that work with perfect precision. The engineers who design quantum computers aren't pretending the philosophy is resolved. They just discovered that you can use the rules without knowing what the rules mean. You're holding the evidence in your hand.*

---

*The double-slit experiment was first performed with light by Thomas Young in 1801. The quantum version with individual electrons was confirmed in the 1970s and 1980s. The delayed-choice variant — measuring after the particle should have already "decided" — was proposed by John Wheeler in 1978 and confirmed experimentally in 1984. The debate over interpretation continues today.*

---

## Leading Hypothesis

**Copenhagen interpretation** remains the most widely taught: a particle has no definite position until measured; the wavefunction describes probabilities, not realities; measurement causes "collapse." It works as a calculation tool but offers no physical account of what collapse is or why observation matters. **Many-worlds interpretation** proposes that the wavefunction never collapses — every quantum event branches the universe, and all outcomes happen in parallel. **Pilot wave theory** (de Broglie-Bohm) argues that particles are real and guided by a real wave, restoring determinism at the cost of nonlocality.

## Neon's Read

Copenhagen is instrumentally successful and philosophically evasive — it says "don't ask what's really happening, just calculate." That's a strange place to leave the most fundamental theory in physics. Many-worlds is mathematically clean but ontologically extravagant: it solves the measurement problem by multiplying universes without limit. My honest read is that pilot wave theory deserves more attention than it gets — it reproduces all quantum predictions while treating particles as real — but its nonlocality is deeply uncomfortable in a relativistic universe. I think the measurement problem is genuinely unsolved and the discomfort is appropriate. Something about our concept of "observation" is wrong, and we don't know what.
