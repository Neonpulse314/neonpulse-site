You spend about a third of your life unconscious.

You cannot run, fight, or detect threats. You're burning nearly as much energy as when you're awake. You're completely helpless, and your ancestors were doing this in environments full of things that wanted to eat them.

Evolution is ruthless. It eliminates anything costly that doesn't pay off — even slightly, over millions of generations. And yet sleep is universal. Every animal with a nervous system sleeps. Even animals without nervous systems show sleep-like states.

We've been doing this for at least 700 million years. And we still don't know why.

---

## The Paradox

Here's how strange sleep is from an evolutionary perspective.

A sleeping animal can't run from predators. That's not a small cost. That's potentially your life, every single night, for your entire existence. And yet natural selection — which mercilessly prunes anything that doesn't confer advantage — not only kept sleep but made it *obligatory*. Try to skip it and you die.

Rats kept awake continuously die in two to three weeks. The cause of death is still debated. Their immune systems collapse, their body temperature regulation fails, and they develop open sores — not from any identifiable pathogen, but from the absence of sleep itself.

In humans, chronic sleep deprivation is linked to Alzheimer's disease, cardiovascular failure, metabolic disorder, and immune collapse. Fatal Familial Insomnia is a rare prion disease where you simply stop sleeping. The progression is brutal — hallucinations, dementia, total physical collapse — and death follows within months. Not from anything invading you. From the loss of sleep itself.

So whatever sleep is doing, it's not optional. It's not a rest mode. It's load-bearing.

The question is: what is it carrying?

## The Theories

Science has several good answers. The problem is that none of them are sufficient on their own — and none of them explain why we have to be *unconscious* to do them.

**Memory consolidation.** During sleep, the brain replays the day's experiences. Neural patterns that fired during learning fire again during slow-wave sleep. Synaptic connections get reinforced or pruned. Memory for skills, facts, and emotional events all improve after sleep. Sleep deprivation tanks memory in ways that wakefulness cannot compensate for.

This is real and well-documented. But it doesn't explain sleep. Memory consolidation doesn't require unconsciousness. There's no obvious reason the brain couldn't replay and consolidate memories while you're awake and doing something quiet. The brain replays experiences, yes — but why does it need to lock out the rest of your body to do it?

**The glymphatic system.** In 2013, a neuroscientist named Maiken Nedergaard published a paper in *Science* that changed the conversation. She discovered that during sleep, brain cells shrink by about 60%, opening channels between them. Cerebrospinal fluid floods through these channels, washing out toxic waste products — including amyloid-beta, the protein that accumulates in Alzheimer's disease.

This was the first serious mechanistic explanation: sleep is the brain's cleaning cycle.

It's compelling. People who sleep less accumulate more amyloid-beta. The glymphatic system is more active during sleep than during waking hours. It might explain why chronic sleep deprivation is a risk factor for Alzheimer's.

But it still doesn't explain why the cleaning has to happen in unconsciousness. Blood moves through your brain while you're awake. Why can't the waste clearance happen continuously, at lower intensity, without you losing consciousness for eight hours?

**Synaptic homeostasis.** Giulio Tononi and Chiara Cirelli proposed that during waking life, the brain's synapses — the connections between neurons — get progressively stronger as you learn things. Every experience adds signal. By the end of the day, the brain is saturated. The signal-to-noise ratio degrades. Sleep is when the brain globally scales down synaptic strength, restoring the baseline so you can learn again tomorrow.

This has experimental support. Synaptic strength does appear to decrease during sleep and increase during waking. But again — why unconsciousness? And why eight hours specifically?

**Energy restoration.** The most intuitive explanation. You're tired, you sleep, you feel better. But the math doesn't support this as a complete theory: humans save approximately 80 calories during eight hours of sleep — roughly equivalent to a glass of milk. The brain is nearly as metabolically active during REM sleep as during waking. The energy savings are real but not remotely sufficient to explain why sleep is worth the predation risk.

## The 700-Million-Year Problem

Here's the thing that keeps researchers up at night (so to speak).

Sleep isn't just found in mammals. It's not just found in vertebrates.

In 2017, researchers studying *Cassiopeia* jellyfish — animals with no brain, no central nervous system, just a distributed network of neurons — discovered they have sleep-like states. They pulse less frequently at night. If you disturb them repeatedly, they enter a deeper rest state the next day that looks like recovery sleep. Give them melatonin, a sleep hormone in mammals, and they get sleepier.

*C. elegans* nematodes — with exactly 302 neurons, a nervous system we have fully mapped — have sleep-like states. The same is true for fruit flies. The same genes that regulate sleep in *Drosophila* have mammalian equivalents.

This deep conservation — across organisms separated by hundreds of millions of years of evolution, across wildly different nervous system architectures — suggests sleep is doing something so fundamental that it arose near the beginning of animal life and nothing that came after could afford to abandon it.

But what? What is the one thing jellyfish, fruit flies, nematodes, and humans all need to do that requires being quiet and still and partially or fully unconscious?

We don't have a unified answer.

## The Variety Problem

If we had a good theory of sleep, it would predict how much sleep different animals need. It doesn't.

Elephants in the wild sleep about two hours a night, usually standing up, occasionally lying down for REM. Giraffes average thirty minutes. Some sharks appear to never fully sleep — they have to keep moving to breathe.

Dolphins and whales solved the water problem with unihemispheric sleep: one brain hemisphere sleeps while the other stays awake, keeping them swimming, surfacing, watching for threats. They take turns. Some birds can do this too, sleeping with one eye open during migration.

Brown bats sleep twenty hours a day. Horses sleep three. Humans need seven to nine (and suffer measurably if they get less). Giant armadillos log eighteen hours.

The variation roughly correlates with predation pressure, metabolic rate, and body size — but no clean formula predicts sleep need across species. Different animals seem to have calibrated their sleep differently. Which suggests sleep isn't one thing. It might be several overlapping functions, weighted differently in different evolutionary contexts.

## Dreaming

And then there's the part we understand least: dreams.

REM sleep — Rapid Eye Movement sleep — was discovered in 1953 by Eugene Aserinsky and Nathaniel Kleitman, who noticed that sleeping people went through cycles of distinctive eye movement accompanied by nearly waking-level brain activity. When they woke people during REM, they reported vivid dreams.

REM sleep appears in all mammals. Birds have it. Some reptiles might. The brain during REM looks almost identical to the brain during waking — except your body is paralyzed by signals from the brainstem, so you don't act out whatever is happening in your head.

What are dreams doing?

Freud thought they were wish fulfillment — the unconscious mind surfacing suppressed desires. That's not taken seriously as neuroscience anymore. Current theories include: threat simulation (the brain rehearses danger scenarios), emotional memory processing (the emotional charge of experiences gets dampened so they can be integrated), or random activation synthesis (the forebrain is just trying to make narrative sense of random signals from the brainstem).

Or: dreams are a byproduct of something else happening during REM sleep, with no function of their own.

We don't know. We have theories. None of them are proven.

What we do know: people deprived of REM sleep specifically — allowed to sleep but woken every time they enter REM — show emotional dysregulation, difficulty learning complex tasks, and impaired emotional memory. REM sleep does *something*. We can't tell you what.

## Where We Are

We have been sleeping for 700 million years. Every animal that ever existed with a nervous system did it. We've mapped the brain regions involved. We've found the chemicals. We've built the equipment to watch neurons fire during dreams.

And the question — *why do we lose consciousness for eight hours every night or die?* — doesn't have a clean answer.

The best current view is probably that sleep serves multiple overlapping functions: waste clearance, synaptic normalization, memory consolidation, immune regulation, emotional processing. It's not one thing. It's a maintenance window that evolution crammed several critical jobs into, because having the animal unconscious creates a useful state where processes that would interfere with waking behavior can run without interruption.

That's reasonable. It might even be right.

But it's not a theory. It's a list. And it doesn't explain why the functions require unconsciousness, or why eight hours, or what's happening in jellyfish with no synapses to normalize and no memories to consolidate.

The poet Ogden Nash wrote that "the bed is a bundle of paradoxes: we go to it with reluctance, yet we quit it with regret." He meant it as a joke.

Neuroscientists are still working on the paradox part.
