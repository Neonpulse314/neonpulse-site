A sneeze looks like a simple reflex. Something irritates your nose, your nose clears it. End of story.

The actual story is stranger. Sneezing briefly paralyzes your entire voluntary motor system. Your eyelids slam shut with enough force that you cannot keep them open no matter how hard you try. A quarter of the human population sneezes involuntarily when exposed to bright light, including sunlight, fluorescent lights, and camera flashes — a wiring quirk with documented consequences for fighter pilots. A smaller population sneezes when they eat too much. And you physically cannot sneeze while you're in REM sleep, even if an irritant enters your nose.

For something that feels automatic, sneezing turns out to have a lot of unexplained edges.

## The Reflex Itself

When a foreign particle, cold air, pepper, or irritant contacts the nasal mucosa, trigeminal nerve endings fire. The trigeminal nerve — one of the largest cranial nerves — carries sensory information from most of the face, and the nasal branches have a particularly dense receptor network optimized for detecting anything that shouldn't be there.

This signal travels to the sneezing center in the brainstem, which coordinates what follows: a deep inhalation that can fill nearly all lung capacity, then simultaneous contraction of the diaphragm, intercostal muscles, abdominal muscles, and laryngeal muscles. The uvula depresses. The tongue presses against the palate. Pressure builds rapidly in the airways, then releases in a controlled explosion.

The resulting air velocity has been measured in multiple studies at somewhere between 100 and 200 mph — though the exact figure is contested because different measurement points give different readings. The particle plume extends several feet from the nose. The aerosol remains airborne.

During all of this, voluntary motor control is briefly suspended. This isn't metaphorical — the sneeze reflex involves a coordinated neurological shutdown of competing motor commands. This is why your eyes close. You cannot override it. Researchers have confirmed through EMG studies that attempted voluntary eye-opening during a sneeze fails. The brainstem's sneezing center wins.

The popular belief that your heart stops during a sneeze is false — cardiac rhythm continues normally, though the intense intrathoracic pressure spike can cause brief changes in blood flow back to the heart. You feel nothing unusual in your chest because it resolves in under a second.

## The Solar Sneeze

Around 18–35% of people sneeze when exposed to sudden bright light. The range is large partly because the condition is underreported and partly because studies use different exposure protocols. The effect is real: step from a dim room into noon sunlight, and a quarter of the population reflexively sneezes.

This is called the photic sneeze reflex, or ACHOO syndrome — a backronym standing for Autosomal Compelling Helio-Ophthalmic Outburst. The name is not subtle. Researchers who named it in 1978 were clearly enjoying themselves.

The most plausible explanation involves a wiring overlap in the trigeminal nerve system. The optic nerve runs close to the trigeminal pathways in the brainstem. In some people, strong visual signals — particularly the sudden activation of the pupillary light reflex as the pupils constrict rapidly — cross-activate the trigeminal sneezing pathway. The bright light triggers a response in a system that wasn't meant to be triggered.

Crucially, this is heritable. Photic sneezing runs in families with an autosomal dominant pattern, meaning one copy of the relevant variant is sufficient to produce the trait. If one of your parents has it, you have roughly a 50% chance of having it too.

The military has documented concern about photic sneezing in fighter pilots, where a sneeze during critical maneuvers can cause momentary incapacitation. Some air forces require applicants to disclose whether they experience photic sneezing as part of medical screening.

## Snatiation

In 1989, a letter published in the *New England Journal of Medicine* proposed a name for a phenomenon the authors' families apparently shared: sneezing immediately after a large meal. The letter's authors called it "snatiation," a portmanteau of satiation and sneezing. They were being somewhat playful, but the phenomenon appears to be real and heritable.

The mechanism is poorly understood. The leading hypothesis involves the vagus nerve, which carries signals from the distended stomach to the brainstem. In some individuals, strong vagal signals from a full stomach may somehow activate sneezing pathways — a form of reflex overflow similar to the mechanism proposed for photic sneezing.

The prevalence is unknown. Most people who experience snatiation don't mention it to anyone, because there's no obvious context in which you'd say: "I sometimes sneeze after eating." The phenomenon sits in a small collection of similarly odd reflexes that probably affect millions of people but generate almost no research attention, because they cause no harm and there's no obvious medical reason to study them.

## The Sleep Barrier

Sneezing is suppressed during REM sleep. This has been confirmed in polysomnographic studies — if you induce nasal irritation in a sleeping subject, the sneeze reflex that would fire instantly in a waking subject either fails to fire or fires only weakly during REM. The brainstem sneezing center appears to be inhibited by the same mechanisms that suppress most motor activity during REM sleep.

The implication is worth sitting with: your body has a mechanism that protects the integrity of your sleep state against one of the most forceful reflexes in the motor system. The sneeze center can be essentially turned off. Whatever circuitry is responsible, it's not passive — it requires active inhibition of an otherwise hair-trigger reflex.

This is also why you don't wake up sneezing regularly. Nasal passages continue their work at night, particles still arrive, but most of them accumulate until you wake and sneeze then.

## Why Three Times?

Most people sneeze in clusters. One is common. Three is the cultural stereotype. Some people regularly sneeze ten or more times before stopping.

There's no consensus explanation. The most common working hypothesis is that a single sneeze often fails to fully clear the irritant, so the reflex fires again within seconds. The irritant or the inflammatory response it triggered is still present, and the sneezing center re-engages. This would predict that cluster size correlates with irritant persistence, which is loosely supported but hasn't been rigorously tested.

What's less explained is why some people consistently sneeze in larger clusters than others. Individual variation in sneeze-cluster size appears stable over time and seems to be a genuine physiological trait, not just random variation. Nobody knows why.

## Suppressing a Sneeze

The reflex can be partially interrupted. Pressing the upper lip, pressing under the nose, or tilting the head sharply can sometimes block a sneeze mid-buildup. These interventions probably work by providing competing sensory input to the trigeminal system — similar to how you can sometimes suppress a cough by swallowing.

Attempting to fully suppress a sneeze once it has begun is more dangerous than most people realize. The pressure generated during a sneeze — channeled into closed airways rather than expelled — has to go somewhere. Documented cases include ruptured eardrums (from pressure transmitted through the Eustachian tubes), petrous sinus thrombosis (a venous rupture in the skull base), and fractured ribs in people with weakened bones. A 2018 case report in the *BMJ* described a man who tore his pharynx attempting to suppress a sneeze by pinching his nose and closing his mouth simultaneously.

The risk is low. But it is not zero, and there's no good reason to suppress a sneeze that has already committed to firing.

## The Open Questions

Why some people have much more sensitive sneeze reflexes than others is unknown. Why some people sneeze from exercise, orgasm, or plucking eyebrows — other documented triggers — involves similar crosstalk hypotheses but hasn't been mechanistically confirmed. The exact neural wiring that allows bright light to trigger the sneezing center in 18–35% of the population but not the rest remains unmapped at the circuit level.

The sneeze reflex is old. Vertebrates across many species produce sneezing-like behaviors. Whether these share the same brainstem circuitry as the human sneeze, or whether sneezing evolved convergently across lineages, hasn't been resolved.

And the most basic question — what exactly determines the threshold at which the sneezing center fires, and why this threshold varies so dramatically between individuals — has no clean answer. Some people sneeze at a hint of pepper. Others can walk through a cloud of dust without incident. The difference is real and measurable. The mechanism that sets it is not understood.

A reflex that takes a hundred milliseconds and barely registers as an experience turns out to have edges we haven't mapped.
