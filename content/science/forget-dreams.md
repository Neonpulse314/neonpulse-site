You just woke up. The dream was vivid — people you recognized, a place that made no sense but felt completely real, something happening that mattered intensely. You lie there for a moment, trying to hold it.

By the time you reach for your phone, half of it is gone. By the time you finish breakfast, it's entirely gone.

The brain that can store your first day of school, a phone number you heard once, and the lyrics to a song you hate — can't hold a dream for ten minutes. Why?

## The Chemical That's Missing

Memory formation isn't just about experience. It requires chemistry.

One of the key molecules is noradrenaline (also called norepinephrine). During waking life, neurons in the locus coeruleus — a cluster of cells deep in the brainstem — constantly release noradrenaline throughout the brain. This chemical modulates attention, arousal, and crucially, memory consolidation. It's part of what makes waking experiences stick.

During REM sleep — the phase where most dreaming happens — the locus coeruleus goes almost entirely silent. Noradrenaline levels drop to near zero.

Harvard sleep researcher Robert Stickgold and his colleagues have pointed to this as a central reason why dreams evaporate so quickly: the neurochemical environment during REM is specifically unsuited to forming lasting memories. The experience happens. The machinery to preserve it is shut down.

This is why you remember dreams you have right before waking — as sleep lightens in the early morning, noradrenaline begins returning. The closer to waking you dream, the more likely some trace survives.

## A Memory That Was Never Formed

There's a distinction worth making: forgetting implies a memory existed and was then lost. But with dreams, it may be that most of the experience never converts to a durable memory at all.

In waking life, the hippocampus acts as a critical hub — it's where short-term experiences get encoded and transferred toward long-term storage. During REM sleep, the hippocampus is active, but its connections to the prefrontal cortex — the brain region associated with self-awareness and narrative processing — are significantly weakened.

The result: you have the experience, but the architecture that would normally route it into retrievable memory is operating differently. The dream may not so much vanish as never properly land.

Waking up interrupts this. It snaps the prefrontal cortex back online. If you can consciously attend to the dream in that narrow window — before the waking mind fills with other concerns — you can sometimes catch and hold fragments. But the window closes fast.

## The Crick and Mitchison Hypothesis

In 1983, Francis Crick (co-discoverer of DNA's structure) and Graeme Mitchison proposed something stranger: maybe we're supposed to forget.

Their "reverse learning" hypothesis suggested that REM sleep functions like a kind of neural garbage collection — the brain processes and discards associations that are unwanted, redundant, or parasitic. Dreams, on this view, are the byproduct of this pruning process. And if you remembered them vividly, you'd be re-encoding exactly the patterns the brain was trying to clear out.

Forgetting, on this hypothesis, isn't a glitch. It's the point.

The hypothesis remains controversial — there's no clean way to test it — but it captures something interesting: the same brain that seems designed to remember almost everything may be specifically designed not to remember this.

## Why Attention Matters

If the chemical environment of REM makes memory formation hard, attention at the moment of waking can partially compensate.

People who keep dream journals — who wake up and immediately write down everything they can catch — show measurably better dream recall over time. This isn't just because they're practicing. It's because intention shapes what the brain attends to. Expecting to remember something, and having a routine for capturing it, creates a kind of bridge between the waking and sleeping states.

The implication: the dreams that survive aren't necessarily the most vivid ones. They're the ones you happened to reach for.

## What We Still Don't Know

The noradrenaline hypothesis is compelling, but it doesn't explain everything. Some people are consistently better dream recallers than others, and the differences don't map cleanly onto noradrenaline levels. Personality factors, sleep architecture, and how people orient to their inner life all seem to matter — but how, exactly, is unclear.

There's also a deeper question that runs underneath all of this: what is the relationship between experience and memory? Waking experience feels continuous precisely because we remember it. Dreams may be the clearest case of experience without memory — a night's worth of vivid, emotionally loaded events that leave almost no trace. Whether that means the experience "counted" in some meaningful sense is a question that neuroscience isn't currently equipped to answer.

For now, the best working model is this: your brain each night constructs something that feels, while it's happening, like the most real thing in the world. Then it systematically dismantles the record. You wake up holding smoke. And by the time you notice it's smoke, it's already gone.
