In 1987, a man named Kenneth Parks drove 23 kilometers from his home in Pickering, Ontario to his in-laws' house in Scarborough. He parked the car, went inside, attacked his father-in-law, and killed his mother-in-law with a tire iron. Then he drove to a nearby police station and told them he thought he had killed someone.

His hands were so badly cut from the attack that he barely felt them. He had no memory of any of it.

Parks was acquitted. After extensive sleep studies, neurological tests, and expert testimony, the jury accepted that he had been sleepwalking. Not faking it. Actually asleep — in some meaningful sense — while carrying out a sequence of complex actions that ended in a death.

This is the thing about sleepwalking that most accounts understate: it isn't just walking. And it isn't something that happens to your brain. It is your brain, running without you.

## What Sleepwalking Actually Is

To understand sleepwalking, you need to know a basic fact about sleep architecture: sleep isn't one thing.

Sleep cycles through stages. There's light NREM sleep (stages 1 and 2), deep NREM sleep (stage 3, also called slow-wave sleep), and REM sleep, where most vivid dreaming occurs. A full night involves four to six cycles of this sequence, each roughly ninety minutes long.

Sleepwalking occurs during **stage 3 — deep slow-wave sleep**. This is important because it's the opposite of what most people assume. Sleepwalkers aren't acting out dreams. Dreams happen in REM. In REM, the brain actually paralyzes the body to prevent exactly this kind of thing — a mechanism called **REM atonia**, where motor signals from the brain are blocked at the spinal level.

Slow-wave sleep has no such lock.

What sleepwalking appears to be is an **incomplete arousal** from deep sleep. The parts of the brain responsible for basic motor function — the brainstem, the cerebellum, the motor cortex — wake up enough to generate movement. But the **prefrontal cortex**, the region responsible for executive function, judgment, conscious awareness, and memory encoding, stays in deep sleep.

The result is a brain that can navigate, avoid obstacles, open doors, and sometimes drive a car — while the part of the brain that would know it was doing any of this remains switched off.

## What the Brain Looks Like During It

For most of sleep research's history, sleepwalking was difficult to study because it's unpredictable. You can't schedule it.

In 2012, a team led by sleep researcher Francesca Siclari at the University of Lausanne managed to capture high-density EEG recordings of people during sleepwalking episodes. What they found was strange: during sleepwalking, certain brain regions showed activation patterns resembling wakefulness or even REM sleep, while other regions — particularly the frontal lobes — remained in deep slow-wave sleep.

It wasn't sleep. It wasn't wakefulness. It was something in between — a **dissociated state** where different parts of the brain were doing different things simultaneously.

This matters because it complicates the intuition that consciousness is all-or-nothing. The sleepwalker is not simply awake and confused, or asleep and inert. They are a functional mixture of both, and the mixture produces behavior that's purposeful enough to be dangerous without being accompanied by anything we'd recognize as awareness.

## Who Sleepwalks and Why

About 2 to 3 percent of adults are regular sleepwalkers. Among children — who spend proportionally more time in slow-wave sleep — the number is closer to 15 to 30 percent. Most children grow out of it as the amount of deep sleep they get naturally decreases with age.

The genetics are striking. If both parents sleepwalk, their children have roughly a 60 percent chance of doing so as well. If one parent sleepwalks, the risk is around 45 percent. Twin studies confirm a strong heritable component. What exactly is being inherited — a lower arousal threshold from slow-wave sleep, a more unstable transition between sleep stages — is still being worked out.

Several factors reliably trigger sleepwalking episodes in people who are already prone to them:

**Sleep deprivation** makes slow-wave sleep more intense when it does occur. The brain essentially rebounds into deep sleep more forcefully, and the incomplete arousals become more frequent.

**Fever** disrupts normal sleep architecture and appears to destabilize the slow-wave/wake boundary.

**Alcohol** suppresses REM sleep and fragments the sleep cycle. The compensatory deep sleep that follows is often intense and unstable.

**Certain medications** — particularly sedative-hypnotics like zolpidem (Ambien) — have been associated with complex sleep behaviors including sleepwalking, sleep-eating, and in reported cases, sleep-driving. The FDA requires black-box warnings on these drugs specifically for this reason. The mechanism is thought to involve the drugs' action on GABA receptors, which may facilitate motor activity during sleep in ways that aren't fully understood.

**Stress and anxiety** are consistently associated with increased sleepwalking frequency. The mechanism there is less clear but probably involves disruption of sleep architecture and increased arousal system activity.

## Complex Behaviors and the Consciousness Problem

The behaviors sleepwalkers can perform extend well beyond wandering.

**Sleep-eating** involves preparing and consuming food, sometimes bizarre combinations, with no memory afterward. **Sleep-talking** is common and occasionally coherent. There are documented cases of people playing piano, painting, writing (sometimes more legibly than when awake), and having sustained conversations — conversations that appear contextually appropriate enough that the other person doesn't realize anything unusual is happening.

And then there are the violent episodes. Sleep-related violence is real and documented, distinct from the Parks case: cases of people attacking bed partners, falling down stairs, punching walls, leaving their home and becoming disoriented in the street.

What these cases create is a genuine philosophical problem. If someone performs a complex, directed action — navigating to a specific location, operating a vehicle, engaging in conversation — what does it mean that they weren't "conscious" during it? The standard view is that consciousness is what makes behavior meaningful and responsible. Sleepwalkers seem to contradict that: meaning and responsibility can apparently come apart from awareness.

Courts have grappled with this. The Parks case in Canada was not unique. Several countries have acquitted defendants in violent crimes on the basis of sleepwalking, treating it as a state of **automatism** — behavior produced by the body without the mind's participation. Whether this reflects the actual neuroscience or is a convenient legal framework remains contested.

## What We Don't Know

The biggest open question in sleepwalking research is the arousal threshold problem: why do some brains reliably fail to complete the transition out of slow-wave sleep, while others never do?

The current model — an unstable boundary between slow-wave sleep and wakefulness that allows motor regions to activate while the frontal lobes stay asleep — is descriptively accurate but mechanistically thin. We don't know what controls the depth or stability of that boundary, why it varies so much between individuals and across nights, or what precisely in the genetics of high-risk families is being passed down.

There's also an interesting question about what sleepwalking tells us about the relationship between consciousness and cognition more broadly. The sleepwalking brain is not random. It's navigating, responding, in some cases communicating. Something is happening that looks like cognition without looking like consciousness.

Whether those two things can genuinely come apart — whether cognition can be real without consciousness being present — is one of the harder questions that sleepwalking puts directly in front of us and refuses to answer.
