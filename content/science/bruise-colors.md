A bruise is a clock you can read with your eyes.

The purple-black of a fresh bruise, the green at the edges a few days later, the yellow-brown before it fades — none of that is random. Each color corresponds to a specific chemical compound your body is producing, metabolizing, and clearing away. You're watching a biochemical pathway unfold in real time, visible through translucent skin.

The same pathway, run in a different organ and for different reasons, is what causes jaundice.

## What Happens at Impact

When tissue gets struck hard enough to rupture small blood vessels — capillaries and venules — red blood cells leak into the surrounding tissue. They're not in circulation anymore. They're stranded in a space they were never meant to occupy.

Initially the area looks red, then rapidly darkens. Fresh blood is rich in oxyhemoglobin — hemoglobin with oxygen attached — which gives it its bright red color. Under the skin without exposure to air, and in increasing quantities, the blood pooling beneath the surface appears purple-black. As the oxygen in those trapped red blood cells gets consumed over the following hours, hemoglobin shifts to its deoxygenated form, deepening the bruise's color and accounting for the characteristic dark blue-purple of a bruise at its worst.

None of this is damage per se. It's blood in the wrong place. The damage was the vessel rupture. The visible bruise is the consequence of blood sitting where the lymphatic and immune system now has to deal with it.

## The Body Begins Recycling

Within a day or two, macrophages — immune cells that roam tissue clearing debris — arrive and begin engulfing the stranded red blood cells. This is where the chemistry gets visible.

Inside the macrophage, an enzyme called heme oxygenase breaks open the heme group — the iron-containing ring structure at the center of hemoglobin that actually binds oxygen. The porphyrin ring doesn't just dissolve; it gets cleaved at a specific point, which produces a molecule called **biliverdin**.

Biliverdin is green. This is not a metaphor or approximation — it is specifically, chemically green, for the same reason bile is green. When biliverdin accumulates in bruised tissue, the bruise turns green. This typically happens around days three to five, and tends to appear first at the edges of the bruise where macrophage activity is densest and the blood is thinnest.

The process then continues. Another enzyme — biliverdin reductase — converts biliverdin to **bilirubin**. Bilirubin is yellow. This accounts for the yellow-orange color that appears days five through ten as the green phase ends. Bilirubin is eventually picked up by the bloodstream, carried to the liver, conjugated for excretion, and eliminated in bile — the same as the bilirubin produced by normal red blood cell turnover throughout life.

The iron released when heme is broken down doesn't disappear either. It gets sequestered in a storage protein called **hemosiderin**, which appears tan to brown. The iron will be recovered and eventually recycled into new hemoglobin.

## The Same Process, Somewhere Else

Newborn jaundice is caused by bilirubin.

Infants are born with more red blood cells than they need for extrauterine life. When those excess cells are broken down, the liver — still immature and not yet fully up to speed — can't conjugate and excrete the bilirubin fast enough. It accumulates in the bloodstream and deposits in skin and the whites of the eyes, producing the characteristic yellow color.

The chemistry is identical to what's happening in a bruise. Hemoglobin → biliverdin → bilirubin. The difference is location, quantity, and why the red blood cells were destroyed in the first place.

Severe jaundice in newborns is treated with phototherapy — blue light in the 450–490 nm range, which breaks down bilirubin in the skin directly through a photoisomerization reaction that makes it water-soluble and excretable without liver processing. The same light would do nothing to a bruise, because the bilirubin in a bruise is already being handled by local tissue rather than accumulating in the blood.

## The Black Eye Is a Special Case

A periorbital hematoma — a black eye — behaves differently from a bruise on the arm or leg, for a structural reason.

The tissue around the eye is unusually loose and poorly anchored. When blood leaks into this space, it spreads easily. Gravity pulls it downward. A blow to the forehead or cheekbone that ruptures vessels above the eye will see blood migrate downward into the orbital region over the following twelve to twenty-four hours, producing a bruise whose location doesn't match the site of impact. A blow above the eye that produces no immediate bruising can result in a dramatic black eye the next morning.

This spreading tendency, combined with the large blood volume that can accumulate in loose orbital tissue, explains why black eyes take longer to resolve than comparably sized bruises elsewhere — there's simply more blood distributed through more tissue.

## Can You Speed It Up?

Popular remedies for bruise resolution include arnica gel, bromelain supplements, and vitamin K cream. The evidence is thin. A few small trials of topical arnica show modest reductions in bruise visibility, but the effect sizes are small and the trials are not high quality. Bromelain (a pineapple enzyme) has anti-inflammatory properties but no strong evidence it specifically accelerates the heme → biliverdin → bilirubin pathway. Vitamin K plays a role in coagulation, not in bruise clearance.

The metabolic sequence has its own pace. Heme oxygenase, biliverdin reductase, and the downstream liver processing all take time, and there's no known intervention that meaningfully accelerates the timeline. Cold applied immediately after injury can limit the initial bleeding by causing vasoconstriction, which reduces bruise size. But once the blood is in the tissue, the body processes it at its own rate.

Bruise resolution time varies considerably between individuals. Age is the clearest factor — older people bruise more easily (thinner skin, more fragile capillaries, slower immune response) and take longer to clear bruises. Medications like aspirin and warfarin impair clotting and increase bruise size. Nutritional deficiencies, particularly in vitamin C (which is required for collagen synthesis and capillary integrity) and vitamin K, also affect both susceptibility and healing rate.

## Forensic Bruise Aging

The color progression — purple to blue to green to yellow — has obvious forensic appeal. If each stage corresponds to a predictable time window, bruise color could indicate when an injury occurred.

In practice, this turns out to be far less reliable than it sounds.

A 2001 review of forensic literature found that yellow coloration does not appear before 18 hours post-injury, which gives a useful lower bound — if a bruise is yellow, it's at least eighteen hours old. But the upper bounds are much less predictable. The rate of color change varies with bruise depth, skin tone, nutritional status, location on the body, ambient temperature, and individual variation in immune response. Different regions of the same bruise can be in different color stages simultaneously.

Courts and forensic guidelines now treat bruise color as weak circumstantial evidence of timing, not as a reliable clock. The 18-hour yellow floor is the most defensible single finding. Everything else is approximate.

## Why Do Some Bruises Leave Permanent Marks?

Most bruises resolve completely without any lasting trace. Some leave a permanent brownish discoloration, particularly large or deep bruises, and bruises in people with certain conditions.

The permanent discoloration is hemosiderin — the iron storage molecule produced when heme is broken down. Normally, hemosiderin is mobilized and the iron is recycled. In some cases, particularly with large volumes of extravasated blood or impaired macrophage function, hemosiderin accumulates in the tissue and can remain indefinitely. The cells that have taken it up may not clear it efficiently. The iron oxidizes and produces a stable brownish color that doesn't fade.

Why some bruises cause permanent hemosiderin staining while others of similar apparent severity do not is not fully understood. Depth of injury, individual macrophage efficiency, and local tissue oxygenation all likely play roles, but the specific predictors of permanent staining haven't been well characterized.

A bruise most people never look at closely turns out to be a visible trace of several interlocking metabolic systems, each doing its specific job in a specific sequence, under the skin, in plain sight.
