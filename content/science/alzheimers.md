In 1906, a German psychiatrist named Alois Alzheimer examined the brain of a 55-year-old woman who had spent the last five years of her life losing her mind. She had become confused, aggressive, disoriented, paranoid. By the end, she didn't know where she was or who anyone was.

Under his microscope, Alzheimer found two things: sticky protein deposits between neurons — plaques — and twisted protein fibers inside the neurons — tangles.

A century later, those same plaques are at the center of the biggest failure in modern medical research.

---

## The Hypothesis That Ate the Field

In 1992, neuroscientists John Hardy and Gerald Higgins published a paper proposing the **Amyloid Cascade Hypothesis**: a sticky protein called amyloid-beta (Aβ) accumulates into plaques, and those plaques trigger the cascade of destruction — the tau tangles, the neuroinflammation, the neuron death — that produces Alzheimer's disease.

The logic was compelling. People with Down syndrome (who carry an extra copy of the amyloid precursor protein gene) almost universally develop Alzheimer's by their 40s. Rare genetic mutations that increase amyloid production also cause early-onset Alzheimer's. Clear the plaques, the theory went, and you should clear the disease.

The field went all in. For three decades, amyloid was the target.

---

## The Trials That Kept Failing

Between 2002 and 2022, the pharmaceutical industry ran more than **200 clinical trials** targeting amyloid in Alzheimer's patients. The success rate: roughly 1%.

Not 50%. Not 20%. **1%.**

Drug after drug successfully cleared amyloid from the brain. And patients didn't get better. In several trials, they got worse.

Solanezumab. Bapineuzumab. Gantenerumab. Aducanumab — which the FDA controversially approved in 2021 over the objections of its own advisory panel, then restricted. All of them could reduce amyloid. None of them could stop the disease.

The pharmaceutical industry spent roughly **$50 billion** over those two decades. Eli Lilly, Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson, Biogen — all walked away with failed trials and no cure.

---

## The Paper That May Have Changed Everything — and Then Collapsed

In 2006, University of Minnesota neuroscientist Sylvain Lesné published a paper in *Nature* that seemed to finally explain *why* amyloid was toxic. He identified a specific form of the protein — a cluster called Aβ*56 — that appeared to impair memory in mice when injected into healthy brains. The paper was cited more than 2,300 times. It reinvigorated the amyloid field.

In 2022, independent neuroscientist Matthew Schrag ran the paper's images through forensic analysis software. He found what appeared to be extensive image manipulation — figures that looked spliced, duplicated, or altered.

*Science* magazine investigated. More than a dozen papers co-authored by Lesné appeared to contain manipulated images. The 2006 *Nature* paper was retracted in 2024.

The field had spent 16 years building on a foundation that may not have existed.

---

## The Haunting Anomaly

Here's what makes Alzheimer's genuinely mysterious, beyond even the hypothesis failure:

**Some people have massive amyloid deposits and never get Alzheimer's.**

Autopsy studies have found individuals with plaques dense enough to have classified them as Alzheimer's during life — but who were cognitively sharp until the day they died. Other people develop the disease with relatively modest amyloid burdens.

This is called **cognitive reserve**, and nobody fully understands it. Education, social engagement, bilingualism, and exercise all seem to confer some protection. But the mechanism is unclear. Amyloid might be necessary but not sufficient. It might be a bystander. Or the relationship might be deeply nonlinear in ways we haven't mapped.

The plaques might be a symptom, not a cause.

---

## What Else Might Be Going On

The field has slowly — painfully — started exploring alternatives:

**Tau tangles.** The second thing Alzheimer spotted in 1906. Tau protein normally stabilizes the internal skeleton of neurons. In Alzheimer's, it hyperphosphorylates and forms tangles that kill neurons from the inside. Tau pathology actually correlates better with cognitive decline than amyloid does. Some researchers now think tau is more central than the plaques.

**Neuroinflammation.** The brain has its own immune cells called microglia. In Alzheimer's, they become chronically activated, creating inflammation that may accelerate neuron death. Genetic risk variants like TREM2 affect microglial function — suggesting the immune response might be as important as the protein deposits.

**The herpes virus hypothesis.** Harvard neuroscientists Robert Moir and Rudy Tanzi proposed in 2016 that amyloid might actually be an *antimicrobial* defense — a response to viral infection, particularly herpes simplex virus, which is dormant in most adult brains. Alzheimer's, on this view, might be the collateral damage of a brain trying to fight an infection. This remains controversial and unproven.

**Metabolic dysfunction — "Type 3 diabetes."** The brain is an insulin-dependent organ. Some researchers have found that insulin signaling breaks down in Alzheimer's brains, and that the disease may partly be a metabolic disorder. This explains why Type 2 diabetes is a significant risk factor. Whether this is cause, effect, or parallel consequence is unclear.

**Synaptic failure.** Neurons communicate at synapses. Before neurons die, their synapses fail. Some researchers argue synapse loss — not plaque accumulation — is the real driver of cognitive decline. Synapses are invisible to most brain scans, making this hard to study in living patients.

---

## The Lecanemab Question

In 2023, the FDA approved **lecanemab** (Leqembi), an amyloid-clearing antibody that showed modest clinical benefit in trials — slowing cognitive decline by about 27% over 18 months. It was the first amyloid drug to show any clinical efficacy.

The reaction in the scientific community was split.

Supporters: proof that the amyloid hypothesis was right, we just needed to intervene earlier. The disease starts accumulating amyloid 15-20 years before symptoms appear. The trials that failed might have treated patients too late.

Critics: a 27% slowing in decline is not the same as improvement. The drug causes brain microbleeds in a significant percentage of patients. The clinical benefit, while statistically real, is small enough to be invisible in daily life for most patients.

The debate continues.

---

## What We Don't Know

After 120 years, we still don't know:

- **What causes Alzheimer's.** Amyloid? Tau? Inflammation? Metabolic failure? Infection? Some combination? In what order?
- **Why some people are resilient** to massive amyloid accumulation.
- **Whether the disease is one thing** or several diseases with similar symptoms and different causes.
- **When the disease actually starts** — it may be a decades-long process that begins long before any cognitive symptoms appear.
- **How to stop it** once it has started.

There are 55 million people living with dementia worldwide. That number is projected to reach 153 million by 2050.

The science has never been more sophisticated. The mystery has never felt more urgent.

And we still don't have a cure.
