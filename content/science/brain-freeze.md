You're eating ice cream too fast. Within seconds, a sudden stabbing pain hits somewhere behind your eyes or across your forehead. It lasts about 20 to 30 seconds, then disappears as quickly as it came.

Here's what's strange about this: the cold never touched your brain. Your forehead never touched anything cold. Your brain has no pain receptors to begin with. So what just happened?

## What's Actually Getting Cold

When you eat something very cold quickly, it makes contact with the back of your mouth — specifically the area near the junction of the hard and soft palate, and the posterior pharynx. Behind this region sits a structure called the **sphenopalatine ganglion (SPG)**: a small cluster of nerve cells tucked behind the nose, just above the roof of the mouth.

The SPG is one of the largest collections of nerve cells outside the brain. It serves as a relay station for the trigeminal nerve — the nerve that handles sensation for your entire face, forehead, sinuses, and teeth. When something cold hits the back of the palate quickly, the SPG detects a sudden temperature drop and responds.

That response involves the blood vessels.

## The Dilation Problem

To protect warm tissue from cold, the blood vessels near the palate briefly **dilate** — flooding the area with warm blood to counteract the temperature drop. This is a standard vascular defense response.

The problem is that the SPG interprets this sudden vascular change as a threat signal and fires. The pain signal travels up the trigeminal nerve. Your brain then has to decide where the pain is coming from.

And it gets the location wrong.

## Referred Pain

Your brain has never needed to distinguish "pain at the sphenopalatine ganglion" from "pain at the forehead" before — because under normal circumstances, these aren't separate things you'd need to tell apart. The trigeminal nerve serves all those regions, and signals from its branches can get misattributed.

The result is **referred pain**: pain that originates in one place but is felt in another. The same phenomenon explains why heart attacks cause left arm pain (the cardiac nerves and left arm nerves share spinal roots, so the brain misidentifies the source). Your palate is in pain — but you feel it in your forehead, temples, or behind your eyes, because that's where the trigeminal nerve's territory points.

The technical name for brain freeze, sometimes used in medical literature, is **sphenopalatine ganglioneuralgia** — literally, "pain in the sphenopalatine ganglion."

## The Blood Flow Component

A 2012 study by Serrador and colleagues at Harvard used transcranial Doppler ultrasound to measure blood flow in the brain during experimentally induced brain freeze. They had volunteers drink ice water through a straw pressed to the roof of the mouth, and monitored cerebral circulation in real time.

They found that brain freeze correlated with a sudden increase in blood flow through the **anterior cerebral artery** — one of the main arteries supplying the front of the brain. The artery dilated rapidly when the cold hit the palate, then constricted again as the headache faded.

This suggests brain freeze isn't just referred pain from the SPG — there's also a genuine vascular event happening in the brain itself, likely triggered by the same neural cascade. The sudden dilation of cerebral arteries may contribute to the pain signal directly.

When blood flow returned to normal, the headache stopped. This gave the researchers an idea.

## The Migraine Connection

The vascular pattern in brain freeze — rapid arterial dilation followed by constriction, mediated by the same neural pathways — closely mirrors what happens during a **migraine**. Migraines also involve the trigeminal nerve, the sphenopalatine ganglion, and aberrant dilation of cerebral blood vessels.

People who get migraines are significantly more likely to experience brain freeze, and their brain freeze episodes tend to last longer and feel more intense. This isn't coincidental — they share the underlying circuitry.

Researchers have started using experimentally induced brain freeze as a **non-pharmacological migraine model**: it activates the same pathways, it's reproducible in the lab, it's self-limiting, and it doesn't require drugs. If they can figure out how to stop brain freeze, they may learn something directly applicable to stopping migraines.

The SPG itself has become a migraine treatment target. Sphenopalatine ganglion blocks — anesthetic injections near the SPG — are now used for cluster headaches and some migraine cases. Brain freeze and cluster headaches may be different expressions of the same system misfiring.

## The Cure That Actually Works

Press your tongue firmly to the roof of your mouth.

This works because your tongue is warm, and the problem was a cold surface near the SPG. Re-warming the palate reverses the vascular response before it fully resolves on its own, which is why the headache stops faster. Drinking warm water works for the same reason.

Some people tilt their head back or press their thumb to the roof of their mouth. Same mechanism — warm contact, palate re-warming, vascular normalization.

The fact that this works — that you can halt a pain signal mid-process by addressing its cause rather than the pain itself — is a good illustration of what's actually happening. It's not a headache in the usual sense. It's a transient alarm triggered by a temperature event in a specific spot, misread by a brain that's never had to localize that particular sensor before.

## What We Don't Fully Know

The rough mechanism is understood, but some questions remain:

- Why do some people get brain freeze much more easily than others, even controlling for migraine history?
- Why does the SPG seem to fire preferentially when cold hits the posterior palate rather than the front teeth or tongue (which can also get very cold without triggering the same response)?
- Whether the cerebral arterial dilation Serrador measured is a cause of the pain, or a response to the SPG signal, or both simultaneously
- Why the episodes are almost always self-limiting within 30 seconds, even without intervention

The headache has been known in medical literature since at least the 1930s. It got its formal name in the 1990s. The migraine connection is still being actively researched.

It's one of the most universal human experiences — almost everyone who's eaten ice cream quickly has had it — but the exact chain of events inside your skull when it happens wasn't understood until recently. And some of it still isn't.
