Every year, the world's power grids lose roughly 10% of the electricity they carry. Not to bad weather or broken equipment — to resistance. The wires themselves fight the current. That friction becomes heat. The heat is waste.

That's billions of dollars and hundreds of millions of tonnes of CO₂ per year, gone before the electricity even reaches a building.

We have known how to eliminate this problem since 1911. Materials exist that conduct electricity with zero resistance — not low resistance, not almost zero, but exactly zero. Send a current through them, and it flows forever without any energy loss. No friction. No heat. No waste.

The catch: making them work requires cooling to temperatures near absolute zero. That's colder than deep space.

For most of the 20th century, that was considered a fundamental physical barrier. Superconductivity required extreme cold. That was the law.

Then in 1986, a pair of physicists found something that should have been impossible: a material that went superconducting at temperatures that seemed, by the theory, completely unreachable. The Nobel Committee awarded them the prize the following year — the fastest Nobel in physics history.

Forty years later, we still can't fully explain how it works.

## The Discovery and the Theory

In 1911, Dutch physicist Heike Kamerlingh Onnes was experimenting with mercury cooled to within a few degrees of absolute zero — about −269°C. At that temperature, something strange happened: electrical resistance in the mercury dropped to exactly zero.

This was superconductivity. Onnes was awarded the Nobel Prize in 1913.

For the next four decades, superconductivity was an experimental curiosity. It happened in a handful of metals and alloys. It required temperatures achievable only with liquid helium, which was expensive, difficult to produce, and barely existed outside specialized labs. Nobody could explain the mechanism.

The explanation came in 1957, from three physicists at the University of Illinois: John Bardeen, Leon Cooper, and John Schrieffer. Their theory — BCS, from their initials — was one of the triumphs of 20th-century physics.

The core idea: in a normal conductor, electrons bump into the atomic lattice as they move through it. That's resistance. In a superconductor, something different happens. Electrons form pairs — Cooper pairs — through a subtle interaction with the vibrating lattice itself. One electron distorts the lattice slightly as it passes, leaving a tiny positive charge in its wake. A second electron is attracted to that distortion. The two electrons become linked — not touching, not chemically bonded, but quantum-mechanically correlated, moving together as a unit.

Cooper pairs behave fundamentally differently from solo electrons. They're bosons, which means they can all occupy the same quantum state. A current of Cooper pairs is a quantum fluid — a coherent, frictionless flow that can travel forever without losing energy.

BCS theory made specific, testable predictions: which materials would superconduct, at what temperatures, with what properties. It was right about everything. Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer shared the Nobel in 1972.

And then the predictions put a ceiling on what was possible.

From BCS theory, you could calculate the maximum theoretical temperature at which conventional superconductivity could occur. The phonon-mediated Cooper pair mechanism had limits. Based on the physics, the upper bound appeared to be around −218°C — about 30 degrees above absolute zero.

That was considered close to definitive. If you wanted a room-temperature superconductor, BCS theory said forget it.

## 1986: The Prize That Broke the Theory

Georg Bednorz and K. Alex Müller were researchers at IBM's lab in Zurich, working on ceramic metal oxides — materials most physicists considered poor candidates for superconductivity. Ceramics are insulators. They resist electricity. Why would they superconduct?

Bednorz and Müller had a hunch, not widely shared, that these materials were worth investigating. In 1986, they found one — a copper oxide ceramic — that went superconducting at about −238°C.

That number matters. It's still brutally cold. But it's well above the ceiling that BCS theory had predicted for conventional superconductors. This wasn't an incremental discovery. It was outside the allowed range.

Within months, researchers around the world were racing to test variations of the material. By early 1987, copper oxide compounds had been found that superconducted at −178°C — warm enough to be achieved with liquid nitrogen rather than liquid helium. Liquid nitrogen costs about as much as milk. Liquid helium costs about as much as fine whiskey.

The announcement at the March 1987 American Physical Society meeting was so chaotic that the session ran past midnight. Physicists were shouting, crowding the aisles. One attendee compared it to a rock concert. It became known as the Woodstock of Physics.

The Nobel Committee awarded Bednorz and Müller the prize in the same year they published — 1987. The only time physics has moved that fast.

The new materials were called cuprates, or high-temperature superconductors. "High temperature" is relative — they're still extremely cold by human standards — but they crossed a threshold that BCS theory said wasn't crossable.

BCS didn't explain them. The Cooper pairs in cuprates don't form through phonon interactions. Something else is happening. After 40 years of intensive research — thousands of papers, dozens of Nobel laureates working on it, billions in funding — that something else remains unknown.

## What We Know and Don't Know

Cuprate superconductors have a layered copper-oxide structure. The superconductivity appears to live within those copper-oxygen planes. The pairing symmetry is different from conventional superconductors — d-wave rather than s-wave, which tells physicists something fundamental about the nature of the pairing mechanism.

Theorists have proposed hundreds of explanations. The most prominent candidates involve magnetic fluctuations — the idea that spin waves in the material, rather than lattice vibrations, facilitate Cooper pair formation. But the details don't close. Predictions don't consistently match experiments. Competing theories contradict each other.

The problem is partly that cuprates behave strangely even when they're not superconducting. Below a certain temperature but above the superconducting transition, they enter a phase called the "pseudogap" state — electrons seem to pair up, but not quite into the coherent quantum fluid that conducts without resistance. Above the superconducting transition, they show "strange metal" behavior: resistivity that scales linearly with temperature in a way no conventional theory predicts.

The cuprate phase diagram — a map of how the material behaves under different conditions of temperature and electron density — is one of the most complex and contested in condensed matter physics. Physicists have been arguing about it for four decades. The consensus on what's happening has shifted repeatedly.

Some researchers believe the pseudogap and strange metal phases are essential clues. Others think they're separate phenomena that distract from the core superconductivity question. The field has been described, by people working in it, as having too many theories and not enough resolution.

What makes this especially strange: we are building things with superconductors we don't understand.

MRI machines use superconducting magnets cooled with liquid helium. Particle accelerators — the Large Hadron Collider among them — require kilometers of superconducting cable. Experimental fusion reactors are being built around superconducting magnets that must maintain precise fields for years without interruption. Quantum computers use superconducting circuits as qubits.

All of this works. The engineering has outrun the theory.

## The Room-Temperature Dream and the LK-99 Moment

The ultimate goal — the result that would change energy, transport, computing, and medicine in ways difficult to overstate — is a material that superconducts at room temperature and ordinary pressure.

This is not a fantasy. There is no known theoretical reason it can't exist. BCS theory had a ceiling, and we've broken it. The ceiling for unconventional superconductivity is unknown.

In 2020, a team at the University of Rochester reported a compound — hydrogen, carbon, and sulfur, under extreme pressure — superconducting at about +15°C. Room temperature. The catch: the pressure required was roughly 2.6 million times atmospheric pressure — the pressure at Earth's center. Useless for any practical application, but proof that room-temperature superconductivity isn't physically impossible.

In 2023, a small team in Korea published a paper claiming they had synthesized a material called LK-99 — a modified lead apatite ceramic — that superconducted at room temperature and normal atmospheric pressure.

The claim went viral in ways that physics papers almost never do. Within days, replication attempts were underway in labs around the world. Videos appeared of small samples of LK-99 appearing to levitate — a hallmark of superconductors, which expel magnetic fields. Researchers were staying up through the night trying to reproduce the result.

Within weeks, the levitation was explained: contamination with a different compound, a copper sulfide, was producing a visual effect that looked like, but wasn't, magnetic levitation. The superconductivity claims fell apart under replication. LK-99 is not a room-temperature superconductor.

But the reaction — the global sprint to check, the overnight replications, the frantic excitement — illustrated how much this matters to people in the field, and why.

A room-temperature, ambient-pressure superconductor would allow power transmission without loss. Maglev transport without expensive cooling infrastructure. Computing devices that don't need to manage heat as a primary constraint. Medical scanners that don't require liquid helium. Fusion power plants with viable magnets.

That material may exist. We don't know how to find it. We're not sure we would recognize the mechanism if we did find it, because we still don't fully understand why the materials we already have work.

## The Deeper Problem

The embarrassing part is not that we haven't found room-temperature superconductivity. It's that we've had high-temperature superconductors for 40 years and the theoretical explanation is still not settled.

This is not for lack of effort. Condensed matter physics has thrown extraordinary talent and resources at this problem. The issue is that the materials are complex — cuprates have multiple competing phases, sensitive dopant dependence, subtle structural variations — and the interactions between electrons in them are strongly correlated, which means the math is extremely hard. Perturbation theory, the standard tool of quantum field theory, doesn't work when interactions are strong.

Computer simulation has helped but not resolved the problem. Numerical approaches can model the behavior of small clusters of atoms, but scaling to real materials involves approximations that may miss the key physics.

There is still no agreement on what mechanism causes Cooper pair formation in cuprates. There are serious researchers who believe the problem is conceptually close to solved, and serious researchers who believe the fundamental theory hasn't been written yet.

Meanwhile, the room-temperature hunt continues. New classes of hydrogen-rich superconductors — hydrides under pressure — have pushed critical temperatures up steadily. The record, as of 2020, is a LaH₁₀ compound at −23°C under extreme pressure. Every year the pressure requirement comes down a little, the temperature goes up a little.

The physics that would allow us to design a room-temperature, ambient-pressure superconductor from first principles — rather than searching by trial and error — requires understanding we don't yet have.

We discovered this phenomenon before the theory of relativity was published. We still don't know how half of it works.
