Here's a problem.

The sun burns through about 600 million tonnes of hydrogen every second. That fusion releases the energy that keeps you alive — warmth, light, photosynthesis, everything downstream.

But the sun isn't hot enough to do it.

---

For two protons to fuse, they have to get close enough that the strong nuclear force can grab them — about 0.000000000000001 meters apart. The problem is that both protons are positively charged. Like poles repel. The closer they get, the harder the force pushing them apart. At the temperature of the sun's core — around 15 million degrees Celsius — protons don't move fast enough to overcome that repulsion.

The math says they should bounce off each other. Every time. Forever.

The sun should be cold. You should not exist.

---

And yet.

What actually happens is that protons *tunnel* through the repulsion barrier. They don't go over it. They don't have enough energy to go over it. Instead, they appear on the other side — as if the barrier was never there.

This is quantum tunnelling. It's not a metaphor. It's measurable. We have built entire technologies around it: the flash storage in your phone uses quantum tunnelling to write and erase data. Tunnel diodes. Scanning tunnelling microscopes that image individual atoms. Radioactive decay — the process that killed dinosaurs and the one that heats Earth's core — is driven by particles tunnelling out of atomic nuclei.

The phenomenon is real. We use it constantly.

We don't fully understand it.

---

## What We Know (And What We Don't)

The mathematics works. A particle in quantum mechanics isn't at a fixed location — it's described by a wave function that gives the *probability* of finding it in each place. When that wave function hits a barrier, it doesn't stop abruptly. It decays exponentially through the barrier — and if the barrier is thin enough, there's still meaningful probability on the other side.

The particle has a chance of being found there. Sometimes, it is. That's tunnelling.

What we can't fully explain: **how long does it take?**

This sounds like a simple question. It's not. And the answer might be deeply strange.

Quantum mechanics doesn't give time the same status as position or momentum. There's no clean "time operator" in the equations the way there is for other quantities. Asking "how long did the particle spend inside the barrier?" turns out to be more philosophically fraught than it sounds.

Several possible answers have been proposed: the Büttiker-Landauer time, the Wigner delay time, the Larmor time. They give different numbers. Physicists have argued about this for decades.

---

## The Faster-Than-Light Problem

In 2020, a team at the University of Toronto led by physicist Aephraim Steinberg measured the time it takes a rubidium atom to tunnel through a laser barrier. Their answer: about **0.6 milliseconds**.

Here's the strange part: the barrier was wide enough that a classical particle crossing it at the same average speed would have taken longer. The tunnelling atom appeared to move through the barrier *faster than light* — if you just divided distance by time.

This isn't as wild as it sounds, because the atom wasn't actually traveling through the barrier in any classical sense. The wave function doesn't "travel." The atom simply has a higher probability of being found on the far side than you'd expect — and the specific atoms that do tunnel appear to have been the faster-moving members of the quantum distribution to begin with.

But the interpretation is genuinely contested. The apparent superluminal transit time doesn't allow you to send information faster than light — the causality violations don't materialize. The question is why. And the question of what the particle was actually *doing* inside the barrier — whether "doing" even means anything in that context — remains open.

---

## Why This Matters

Quantum tunnelling isn't exotic. It's everywhere.

Every atom in your body is stable because of quantum effects. Radioactive decay — when an alpha particle tunnels out of a nucleus — powers the geothermal heat that drives plate tectonics. The universe had enough tunnelling during the Big Bang to form the elements. The sun fuses hydrogen because protons tunnel. Life on Earth exists because of a quantum effect that classical physics says shouldn't happen.

The technology is immediate too. The flash drive storing your photos writes data by using electrons that tunnel through a thin insulating layer. Modern transistors — the ones at the heart of every computer chip — are now so small that quantum tunnelling is a design problem engineers have to work around. The barrier that's supposed to stop current from flowing is thin enough that electrons leak through it anyway. The physics that made the sun is now making your processor run hot.

---

## The Thing No One Can Explain

You can calculate the probability of tunnelling with precision. You can engineer devices that depend on it. You can watch it happen.

You cannot say, cleanly, what "being inside the barrier" means for a particle that isn't allowed to be there by classical physics. You cannot say how long it takes in a way that satisfies all the different frameworks physicists use to measure time at the quantum scale. And you cannot resolve the deeper question sitting underneath all of it: what is the wave function, actually?

Is it a real physical thing that exists in space? Is it just a mathematical tool for calculating probabilities? If a particle tunnels, was it ever "in" the barrier at all?

These aren't new questions. They're the same questions at the heart of the double-slit experiment, the measurement problem, the whole strange structure of quantum mechanics. Tunnelling just makes them concrete — because here, the weirdness isn't abstract. It's why you're warm.

---

*The next time you charge your phone, you're using quantum tunnelling. The electrons writing data to your flash storage are passing through barriers they classically shouldn't be able to cross — the same process that powers the sun, that drives radioactive decay, that kept the early Earth geologically active long enough for life to start. We've been engineering with this effect for decades. The philosophical questions underneath it are still open.*

---

*Quantum tunnelling was first described mathematically in 1928, independently by George Gamow and by Ronald Gurney and Edward Condon, to explain radioactive alpha decay. The tunnelling time controversy has persisted since the 1980s. The 2020 University of Toronto experiment (Ramos et al., Nature) measured tunnelling time in rubidium atoms and reignited debate about apparent superluminal transit. No consensus exists on the correct definition of tunnelling time.*
