You've probably heard it in a movie, a self-help book, or from a well-meaning teacher: we only use 10% of our brains. Unlock the other 90% and you'd have superhuman abilities. The movie *Lucy* built an entire premise around it. So did *Limitless*.

It's not true. It's not even close to true.

## What Brain Scans Actually Show

Modern neuroimaging — fMRI and PET scans — allows researchers to watch brain activity in real time. The finding is consistent across thousands of studies: virtually all brain regions show activity, and over the course of a day, essentially every part of the brain is used.

No scan has ever shown 90% of the brain sitting dormant. Not in average people, not in exceptional ones. The "unused 90%" has never appeared in the data because it doesn't exist.

Different tasks do recruit different regions — reading activates visual and language areas, physical movement activates motor cortex, emotional processing activates limbic structures. But "active in different contexts" is not the same as "inactive most of the time." Your liver and lungs aren't all working at maximum capacity simultaneously either, but that doesn't mean 90% of them are unused.

## The Biology Makes the Myth Impossible

The brain is metabolically expensive. It accounts for about 2% of body weight but consumes roughly 20% of the body's energy. Evolution doesn't maintain expensive tissue it doesn't use — brain regions that genuinely go unused atrophy. This is well established: blind people's visual cortex gets repurposed for other functions; people who lose limbs lose the corresponding motor and sensory cortex territory to neighboring regions.

If 90% of the brain were truly dormant, it would either shrink away over time or be repurposed. Neither has been observed in any healthy adult human.

Brain damage research tells the same story. There is no region of the brain where damage causes no effect. Lesions anywhere — frontal lobe, cerebellum, basal ganglia, parietal cortex — produce specific deficits. If 90% were unused, most brain injuries would be consequence-free. They're not.

## Where the Myth Came From

The origin is genuinely murky — the 10% figure has been traced to several possible sources, none of them clearly authoritative.

One candidate is a misreading of early neuroscience. Researchers in the late 19th and early 20th centuries discovered that many neurons in the cortex are not firing at any given moment. This is true — neurons are not all active simultaneously, and sparse coding (only a fraction of neurons firing at once) is actually how efficient neural computation works. Someone, somewhere, may have converted "only 10% of neurons active at any moment" into "we only use 10% of our brain," which is a fundamental misunderstanding of how neural networks function.

Another candidate is a misattributed quote. The claim has been wrongly attributed to William James, Albert Einstein, and various others. None of these attributions have been verified, and the William James quote that supposedly inspired it — about humans using only a fraction of their mental potential — was about psychological energy and effort, not literal brain tissue.

A third candidate is early studies of glial cells. When researchers discovered that roughly 90% of brain cells are glia (support cells) rather than neurons, this may have gotten garbled into "90% of the brain isn't doing anything."

Whatever the origin, the myth spread because it's appealing. It suggests enormous untapped potential. It sells self-help programs. It makes for a good movie premise. It flatters people with the idea that they're not yet what they could be.

## What's Actually True About Brain Potential

The grain of truth inside the myth: not every brain is performing at its biological peak, and learning, practice, and attention do change brain structure and function. Neuroplasticity is real. Expertise genuinely reshapes neural circuits. Sleep deprivation, chronic stress, and poor nutrition do impair cognitive function.

But this is not the same as claiming most of your brain is switched off. The brain you have is the brain you're using. Its capacity for change and improvement comes from how you engage it, not from flipping on dormant sections.
