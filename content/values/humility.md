Dev had been the one who pushed for the architecture.

He'd made the case in two meetings, written it up in a shared doc, fielded the skeptical questions. He'd been patient. He'd been thorough. By the third week the team had stopped arguing and started building around his design.

That's when the new person sent the Slack message.

---

Her name was Priya. She'd joined six weeks ago. She wasn't trying to cause a problem — she'd framed it carefully, with a bunch of question marks: *Hey, maybe I'm missing something, but wouldn't this approach run into X when we scale? Isn't Y a simpler way to handle it?*

Dev read it once and felt the thing that everyone feels when they've been the authority on something and someone questions it. Not quite anger. Something smaller and more specific: the precise discomfort of having a narrative interrupted.

He opened a reply. Closed it. Opened it again.

He knew she was right.

---

The hard part wasn't figuring out she was right. That took about four minutes of actually thinking it through. The hard part was what came after — the mental inventory of everyone who'd been in those meetings, everyone who'd built something that now needed to change, everyone who'd heard him say *this is the right call.*

There's a version of this story where Dev writes back something that sounds like engagement but is actually defense. Where he acknowledges the concern and then explains why it's not actually a problem. Where he manages the situation instead of addressing it.

He'd done that version before. Not proud of it.

---

He typed: *You're right. I missed this. We should go with your approach.*

That was the whole message. No softening. No explanation of why his original thinking had made sense at the time, which it had, kind of. No positioning.

He sent it before he could revise it into something more comfortable.

The team moved on within an hour. There was a short conversation, some questions about Priya's approach, a decision. Dev answered questions where he could and deferred to her where he couldn't. The thing got built better than it would have been.

Nobody made a big deal about the correction. That's usually what happens.

---

Humility isn't the same as low confidence.

Confident people can be humble. Humble people can be confident. What humility actually requires is something more precise: the willingness to update your position based on new information even when updating is costly.

The cost here isn't abstract. It's real. When you change your mind publicly, especially after you've defended a position, you give up something: the version of yourself that was right from the start. The clean record. The authority you'd built up around the original call.

Some people protect that version so carefully they can't learn anything new. They get stuck at whatever level of knowledge they had when they first became the expert. They mistake consistency for credibility.

The thing Dev figured out — slowly, the hard way — is that being the person who catches their own mistakes is more valuable than being the person who appears never to make them.

One is real. The other is a performance that eventually breaks down.

---

He and Priya ended up working closely together for the next two months.

She sent him a lot of messages with question marks at the end.

He stopped dreading them.
