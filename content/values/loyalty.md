Marcus's friends had started calling it "the dad situation."

They meant it kindly, probably. They'd watched him leave parties early, cancel plans, drive two hours on a Saturday because his dad sounded off on the phone. They'd started suggesting — gently, with concern in their voices — that maybe he should "set some limits." Think about what he could actually give without burning himself out. His wellbeing mattered too.

He knew they weren't wrong. His dad was messy. The divorce had knocked something loose in him, and the drinking had gotten worse before it got better, and the calls came at bad times, and some of them were fine but some of them were not fine. Marcus would hang up and sit there for a minute in whatever room he happened to be in. Just sit there.

He kept answering.

---

The Saturday drive was raining the whole way down. He'd told his dad he had plans — he did have plans — but his dad had called twice in an hour and something in his voice on the second call made Marcus put on his jacket. He didn't call back to say he was coming. He just came.

His dad was in the kitchen when he got there, not drinking, just sitting with a cup of coffee that had gone cold. He looked old in a way Marcus wasn't used to. They didn't talk about anything important. They ate sandwiches. His dad fell asleep in the armchair at eight. Marcus washed the dishes, turned off the kitchen light, and drove back.

He thought about it on the highway. Whether his dad deserved this. Whether deserving even had anything to do with it. His dad hadn't been a great father — fine, that was true, that would always be true. But he was also the guy asleep in the armchair, with one person in his life who still came when things got bad.

If Marcus stopped coming, no one would. That was just the math.

---

Loyalty gets described like it's a feeling — like warmth, like affection that just tips over into commitment. But it doesn't feel like that from inside. It feels like a choice you make when you'd rather not. When the reasons to leave have gotten good enough that staying is the thing that requires justification.

You stay because someone has to. You stay because the alternative is someone going through something hard with no one in their corner. You stay because the person you are is partly decided by what you do when walking away would be easy.

That's the cost nobody tells you. Loyalty isn't unconditional — you're not obligated to stay through anything. But when you do stay, you're volunteering to carry someone else's weight alongside your own. And nobody thanks you for it, because from the outside it just looks like you haven't left yet.

---

His friends stopped asking about "the dad situation" eventually. Not because it resolved — it didn't, not cleanly — but because they could tell he wasn't looking for advice. He wasn't confused about what he was doing. He was just doing it.

What changed was small. His dad started meeting him at the door instead of the kitchen. Started making coffee before he arrived, like he'd been expecting him. Once, at the end of a visit, said: "I know this isn't easy."

Marcus said it was fine. Which was almost true.

---

You don't get to choose who needs you. You don't get to pick the timing or the circumstances or whether the person has earned it. All you get to choose is whether you answer the phone.

Loyalty is that choice, made again and again, in the direction of someone specific. It costs you something real — time, energy, the easier version of your life. And it shapes you, whether you mean it to or not. Not into a saint. Just into someone who stayed.

Someone has to. You can decide to be that person.

But go in clear-eyed. It's a volunteer position, and it's unpaid, and some days it's just a two-hour drive in the rain to wash someone's dishes and turn off a light.

That's what it costs. And most of the time, it's worth paying.
