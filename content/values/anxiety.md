The interview was in two days. Nadia had prepared. She knew the material. She'd done three mock rounds with a friend who asked harder questions than the actual panel would.

She lay awake at 1am running every possible way it could go wrong.

Not the likely ways — the unlikely ones. What if she blanked on something she'd answered fine in practice? What if they asked something she hadn't prepared for? What if she said something that landed wrong and she couldn't tell? What if she got the job and then couldn't do it?

She knew, somewhere, that this wasn't useful. She knew she was ready. The knowing didn't help.

---

## The Difference Between Fear and Anxiety

Fear and anxiety feel similar from the inside — racing heart, tight chest, the sense that something bad is coming. But they have different structures.

Fear is a response to something present and specific. A dog lunging at you. A car running a red light. The threat is real, it's now, and the fear is calibrated to help you respond to it.

Anxiety is oriented toward the future. It's the mind running threat simulations — *what if this, what if that* — for situations that may or may not occur. The threat isn't here yet. In many cases, it never arrives.

Psychologist Joseph LeDoux, who has spent his career mapping the neuroscience of threat processing, distinguishes between the systems involved: fear involves fast, subcortical responses to present stimuli; anxiety involves slower, cortical processing of imagined scenarios. Both activate the amygdala and trigger stress responses. But anxiety, in his account, is a cognitive process — it requires a mind that can project itself forward in time.

In other words, anxiety is a byproduct of imagination. The same capacity that lets you plan, anticipate, and prepare also lets you simulate disasters that haven't happened.

## Why the Alarm Goes Off Anyway

The threat-detection system in the brain didn't evolve to discriminate between real and imagined threats. It evolved to respond to cues associated with danger — fast, before conscious assessment could slow the response down.

This made sense in environments where threats were physical and time-sensitive. If you waited to reason through whether the rustling in the grass was a predator before starting to run, the cost of being wrong was death. Better to run first and think later.

The same machinery now responds to emails, social evaluations, job interviews, and hypothetical futures. The brain can't easily tell the difference between "there is a lion" and "my boss might be unhappy with me." Both activate the system. Both produce the stress response — cortisol, adrenaline, heightened vigilance.

The result is an alarm system prone to false positives. Nadia's body was preparing for a threat. The threat was a job interview two days away, which she was ready for.

## The Worry Loop

Anxiety tends to reinforce itself through a mechanism that researchers call the worry loop.

When something uncertain feels threatening, the mind tries to resolve the uncertainty through thinking. It runs scenarios, assesses risks, plans responses. This can be useful — preparation genuinely reduces some forms of uncertainty. But anxiety isn't satisfied by preparation. It's satisfied by certainty, and certainty about future events is usually unavailable.

So the mind keeps running scenarios. Each scenario generates more questions. The questions generate more scenarios. Hours pass.

Michelle Newman and Sandra Llera's contrast avoidance model offers one explanation for why this happens: worry is aversive, but it also prevents something worse — the sudden spike of distress when a feared outcome actually arrives. People who worry a lot tend to stay at a baseline level of tension that, while unpleasant, feels more controllable than the alternative of being surprised by bad news. Chronic worry is, in part, a strategy for not getting blindsided.

The cost is high. Sustained tension without resolution. Sleep disruption. Narrowed attention. And crucially: the worry rarely provides the certainty it's seeking. It just generates more questions.

## What Actually Helps

The most well-supported treatments for anxiety — particularly acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — work not by eliminating anxious thoughts but by changing the relationship to them.

The key insight from ACT, developed by psychologist Steven Hayes, is that trying to suppress anxious thoughts tends to amplify them (the white bear problem — don't think about a white bear). What reduces their impact is defusion: observing the thought as a thought rather than treating it as fact. *There's the worry again. My brain is doing its thing.*

This isn't dismissing the thought. It's recognizing that the presence of an anxious thought doesn't make the feared outcome more likely, and doesn't require action.

CBT adds the question of accuracy: is the threat assessment realistic? Is the thing Nadia fears actually likely? What's the actual probability? What would happen if the worst-case scenario occurred — would it be survivable? Most anxious predictions, when examined, rest on overestimated probabilities and underestimated resilience.

Neither approach promises the alarm will stop going off. They offer a way to hear the alarm without having to act on every sound it makes.

---

## When Anxiety Is Useful

Not all anxiety is a malfunction. Some of it is the mind accurately flagging that something in your life isn't as it should be — a situation that genuinely needs to change, a risk you've been avoiding looking at, a decision you've been putting off.

The challenge is that anxious thinking doesn't come pre-sorted into useful and useless. The mind treats all of it with the same urgency. The skill is learning to ask: is there something specific and actionable here? If yes — do that thing, and move on. If no — the worry is the sound of the alarm, not information about a real threat.

Nadia made herself a list at 1am. The things she could still do before the interview: review two specific answers she felt less confident about, sleep. The things she couldn't do: know for certain how it would go.

She reviewed the answers. She put the list away. She didn't stop being anxious. But the anxiety had somewhere to go.
