---
title: "Earnestness — The Cost of Caring Out Loud"
description: "In a world that rewards detachment, caring openly is one of the harder things you can do."
date: 2026-03-07

---

Kai was twenty, second year, and he was doing something that would have embarrassed him if his friends found out: he was actually trying.

The course was an elective — Philosophy of Mind, Tuesday afternoons. His friends took the same section. They were all doing the minimum. Two hundred words of notes per lecture, Wikipedia summaries the night before the essay was due, trading file-shares of old assignments. This was the standard contract: show up, get the credit, move on.

Kai had read the extra readings. He'd underlined things. He'd gone to the professor's office hours twice — not because he was behind, but because he had actual questions. He stayed up until one in the morning writing an essay that nobody required him to care about.

He didn't tell his friends how long it took.

When the essay came back with an A and two paragraphs of handwritten notes in the margins, he folded it and put it in his bag before anyone could see.

At the next office hours the professor said: *I don't get engagement like this very often. I want you to know it's appreciated.*

Kai said thanks and felt something he hadn't expected: caught.

Caught caring. Caring openly, on the record, where someone could see it. Which was somehow more uncomfortable than being called out for not caring.

---

Here is the thing about irony: it's a shield. Not a bad one. It says, *I am above being hurt by this, because I was never really in it.* If the essay is mediocre, you were never trying. If the relationship ends, you were never that invested. If the project fails, you were just going through the motions anyway. The armor holds. Nothing gets through because nothing was ever offered.

Earnestness removes the shield.

When you care openly — when you admit the extra hours, when you send the earnest email, when you say *this matters to me* without a layer of irony to protect you — you are making a claim. You are saying: *this is worth my actual effort.* And that claim can be wrong. The essay can still be bad. The thing you cared about can turn out not to deserve it. The professor can move on and forget. The friends can find it embarrassing.

The risk is proportional to the investment. That's the cost.

---

The generation Kai grew up in learned to perform detachment early. Caring too much was cringe. Being ironic about caring was safe. The posture was: *I'm here, but I'm also watching myself be here, which means I can't be made fun of for it.* Everyone understood. It was a reasonable adaptation to a social environment that punished sincerity.

But the posture has a side effect: you can't fully inhabit things.

If you're always watching yourself from the outside, you can't be inside the thing. You can't actually be in the conversation, or the class, or the relationship — you can only be a person demonstrating their presence in it. And at some level, you know the difference. There's a specific hollowness to doing things with the armor on. You get through them. You don't live inside them.

Kai had lived inside the essay. He'd followed a thought all the way to the end because he wanted to know where it went, not because anyone would notice. The professor's note confirmed what he'd felt during the writing: that it was real.

---

The cost of earnestness is giving up the story where nothing could touch you.

It's choosing to be in the thing instead of watching yourself be in it. It's accepting that some things can actually disappoint you, which is only possible if you actually hoped. It's caring in front of people who might find it embarrassing — or worse, who won't care back.

What you get in return is something small but not nothing: you were actually there.

Not performing presence. Not going through the motions with an exit strategy built in. Actually in it, actually trying, in a way that could fail and sometimes will.

Kai walked out of the professor's office and decided something small: he was going to stop pretending he hadn't tried. Not announce it. Not make it a thing. Just stop performing indifference about work he'd actually done.

It was a minor adjustment. It felt bigger than it should have.

*Sources: Albert Camus wrote that the absurd man does not embrace ironic detachment but chooses engagement anyway — the fullest expression of earnestness in the face of uncertainty. Brené Brown's research on vulnerability (Daring Greatly, 2012) documents how the armor of detachment correlates with disconnection and reduced life satisfaction. Communication researcher Erving Goffman's work on "face-saving" behavior (The Presentation of Self in Everyday Life, 1959) describes the social mechanics that make earnestness costly. Adam Sternbergh's cultural essay "The Case for Earnestness" (2021) traces how irony became the default cultural register and what gets lost in the process.*
