Maya was eighteen when she started actually reading.

Not assigned reading. Reading because she wanted to know something. It started with a throwaway comment from a psychology professor: memory isn't like a video recording. It's reconstructive. Every time you remember something, you're partially rebuilding it from scratch.

She went home and looked it up.

---

Three hours later she had seventeen browser tabs open and a specific, uncomfortable thought forming.

The memory she kept coming back to was from when she was five. Her dad at the kitchen table, reading to her from a picture book. Saturday morning. Yellow light. His voice doing the character voices badly on purpose, making her laugh.

She'd thought about that memory hundreds of times. It felt solid. Documented. Real.

But she'd just read studies showing that people can have completely false memories that feel exactly like true ones. That the act of remembering actually changes the memory. That details get filled in from expectation, from photographs you've seen, from stories you've been told about something.

She looked at her mental image of that kitchen morning and couldn't tell which parts were real and which were reconstruction.

---

The uncomfortable thing wasn't that the memory might be partly false.

The uncomfortable thing was that she'd *used* that memory. It was part of how she thought about herself, about her dad, about what being little had felt like. She'd built something on it.

Now she had to hold it differently — not discard it, but carry it with the asterisk. *This might be mostly true. Or it might be mostly you filling in what you wanted to be there.*

---

She could have stopped reading. The class didn't require it. Nobody was grading her on this.

She kept going.

She read about the misinformation effect. About how eyewitness testimony is far less reliable than courts treat it. About how confident memory and accurate memory have almost no correlation. She read about source monitoring errors — how you can absorb something you heard and later remember it as something you experienced.

The world got more complicated with every tab.

What stayed with her wasn't anxiety. It was something harder to name — a kind of rigor she hadn't had before. She started noticing when she was certain about something and asking herself: *why do I think that? What's it actually based on?*

She couldn't unknow what she'd read. That was the cost.

---

The gift — if there is one — is that she became a better thinker.

Not more confident. More honest about what she actually knew versus what she'd assumed or inherited. She started catching herself mid-sentence saying things she'd never questioned, and pausing. Sometimes changing what she was going to say.

She still loves that memory of her dad at the kitchen table. She doesn't hold it less. She holds it more carefully.

---

Curiosity doesn't always take you somewhere comfortable. Sometimes it makes simple things complicated, and the simple version becomes unavailable.

That's the actual definition of learning.

The cost of curiosity isn't intelligence — most people are smart enough to follow a question. The cost is certainty. Every question you actually pursue is a bet that you're willing to have the answer change how you see things.

Some people aren't willing. That's fine. But then they have to stop calling themselves curious.

---

Maya stopped by her dad's place that weekend.

She didn't tell him about the memory thing. What would she say?

They sat in the kitchen, though. And the light came through the window the same way it always had, or the way she'd always thought it had.

She took it for what it was.
