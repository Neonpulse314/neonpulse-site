---

Dominic was three months into being a team lead when he put Yara on her first solo client call.

She was twenty-three, fast, good with data, still learning how to be in a room with someone who had more money than she'd ever managed. The client was a regional distribution company — not a big account, but real. The kind of call where something could go wrong and it would matter.

He told himself he was joining as support. He'd speak if he needed to.

He turned on his camera and settled in to listen.

---

About twelve minutes in, Yara made a mistake.

She misquoted a margin figure — said 18% when the actual number was 14.2%. Not a huge error, but audible. The kind of thing where, if the client was paying attention, they'd catch it later and wonder.

Dominic knew immediately.

He had his mic. He had the chat. He had his camera on. He could have typed a quiet correction. He could have said, *sorry to interrupt — Yara, I think we had a slightly different figure there.* He could have stepped in smoothly, shown the client he was on top of the details, saved the moment.

He didn't do any of that.

---

He watched Yara keep going.

She moved to the next section, walked through the delivery timeline, asked the client a good question about their warehouse capacity. The call continued. The client didn't flag the margin number — he either hadn't caught it or was waiting to check it himself.

At the end, they scheduled a follow-up and hung up.

Dominic stayed on a second.

"Yara," he said. "The margin figure on slide six — you said 18."

She pulled up the deck. A pause. "Oh. God. That's 14.2."

"Yeah."

She looked at him. "Why didn't you—"

"Because you were handling it," he said. "We'll send a correction in the recap email. Happens."

---

Here's the part Dominic didn't say out loud:

He'd wanted to step in.

Not to save the client relationship — the error was small, correctable. He'd wanted to step in because he knew the right answer and she didn't. Because he was the lead and that meant something. Because there's a particular satisfaction in being the one who catches things, the one who knows, the one who fixes it.

That's not leadership. That's just needing to be the smartest person in the room.

Stepping in would have taken twelve seconds and cost Yara something she couldn't easily get back: the experience of handling a live call, including its mistakes. You don't learn to recover from errors by watching someone else recover from your errors.

He knew the right answer. He sat on it. That was the job.

---

Restraint is not the same as passivity.

Dominic wasn't disengaged. He was paying attention to every word. He was ready to step in — genuinely ready — if the error had been serious, if the client had looked confused, if Yara had started visibly floundering. The line between stepping back and abandoning someone is real, and he was watching for it the whole time.

The skill isn't knowing when to use what you have. It's knowing when not to.

Most people with authority have been taught, implicitly or explicitly, that their job is to use it. To catch things. To fix things. To be present in a way that's visible. The quieter skill — the one that takes longer to develop — is recognizing when your presence in a situation is about you, not about the situation.

---

The recap email went out an hour later. Yara wrote it. She included a corrected margin figure with a brief note: *wanted to flag an error from our call — the correct margin is 14.2%, not 18% as I mentioned.* Clean, direct, no drama.

The client wrote back: *Thanks for catching that — appreciate the attention to detail.*

Dominic didn't point this out to her. She'd handled it.

---

The version of that call where he stepped in isn't obviously worse. The number gets corrected faster. The client sees the lead is engaged. Nothing bad happens.

But Yara learns, in that version, that someone is behind her ready to catch her falls. Which means she'll lean on that. Which means the next solo call, and the one after that, she'll be a little less on her own footing, a little more looking over her shoulder, a little less the person who writes her own correction emails and owns her own mistakes.

You don't become capable by having someone fix your errors. You become capable by fixing them yourself.

Restraint is what makes that possible. It's using what you have to hold back — not because you don't care, but because you're playing a longer game than the next twelve seconds.

The power you don't use is still a choice. Sometimes it's the harder one.
