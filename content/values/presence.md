---
title: "Presence — The Place You Actually Were"
summary: "Kai had the clips to prove he was there. He just couldn't remember it the way his friends could."
section_label: "Values"
tags: ["presence", "attention", "connection", "mindfulness"]
---

Kai had been looking forward to this show for four months.

Three of them — him, Dev, and Priya — had been in the same city for exactly one weekend. Dev was doing his residency in Calgary. Priya had just taken a job in Montreal. The tickets had been Kai's idea, bought the same week the show was announced, before anyone could say they might have plans.

The band was one of those things the three of them had grown up on together. First heard them at nineteen, in Dev's dorm room, on a laptop with one working speaker. The album they were playing tonight was the one that had soundtracked a specific, irreplaceable two years of their lives.

It was the kind of show you don't get many chances at.

---

The opener had barely finished when Kai took out his phone.

It wasn't a conscious decision. The lights shifted, the crowd moved, and his hand found the phone the way hands find pockets. He got a good clip of the opening — the room going dark, the first notes, the surge of noise from the crowd around them. It looked good. It sounded good. He posted it.

The next song started and he put the phone away.

Then someone beside him held up a lighter and the image was too good, so he took it out again. Then he wanted to get Dev's face during the bridge of the third song, because Dev had the exact look people get when something hits them and it seemed like the kind of moment that deserved to be captured. Then there was the angle from their spot on the floor, just off center, where you could see the whole stage and the lighting rig and it seemed like a waste not to document it.

He told himself he was still there. He was in the room. He could hear it.

---

The set ran two hours.

On the drive home, Dev was in the front seat and couldn't stop talking. He kept referencing moments — the key change in the fifth song, the part where the guitarist forgot a chord and laughed about it, the encore they almost didn't play. Priya kept finishing his sentences. They were doing that thing people do after a good show, passing the memory back and forth like they were checking to make sure it was real.

Kai was driving. He laughed when they laughed. He said *yeah* and *I know* in the right places.

But he kept waiting for his memory to catch up.

He'd been there for the key change. He knew he'd been there. He had a clip of the guitar moment — he'd almost posted it. But when Dev described it, what Kai could see was the clip. The small rectangle. The tiny version of the room he'd been standing in.

He couldn't find the actual memory. The way it had felt to be in that space, in that moment, with those people. It was like reaching for something just out of arm's reach, always a few inches behind his hand.

---

He went through the clips later that night.

They were good. The audio wasn't — phones never capture rooms — but visually they told a story. You could see the crowd, the lights, the stage. You could see Dev in one of them, head down, eyes closed, moving slightly. You could see the room the way someone who'd watched it on a screen would see it.

What you couldn't see was Kai. Not because he wasn't there. Because he was behind the camera.

He'd been there for four months. He'd been in the room for two hours. He had the receipts. And still, somehow, Dev and Priya had gone to a different show — a fuller one, lived at full resolution — and he'd been next to them the entire time.

---

Presence isn't a discipline.

That's the part people get wrong. You can't achieve it by locking your phone in your car, or setting a screen time limit, or making a rule. Those things might help, but they're dealing with the symptom. The actual question is simpler and harder: *What are you here for?*

Kai knew what he wanted from the night. He wanted the memory. He wanted the thing that would still be there in five years when they were in different cities and this version of their friendship was further back in the rearview. He wanted to have been there.

But he spent the night building a record of the experience instead of having it. The two things look the same from outside. They feel completely different from inside.

---

He texted Dev the next morning. *Last night was good.*

Dev wrote back: *Good? It was the best. That encore almost killed me.*

Kai smiled at his phone. He'd been at the encore. He had a clip of it.

He just couldn't feel it the way Dev clearly could, couldn't reach back into the room and put himself in his own body during those three minutes. The experience had a surface — he'd documented it carefully — but no depth. He'd been present enough to record it. Not present enough to keep it.

---

The shows you remember aren't always the best ones.

They're the ones you let happen to you. The ones where you forgot to check whether the moment was worth capturing and just stood inside it, undefended, letting it land. Those are the ones that stay. Not because you documented them. Because you were actually there.

Presence is what it costs you to be somewhere fully. The price is everything else — the phone, the framing, the question of how it looks. You have to pay that price up front, before you know if the moment will be worth it.

Most of the time it is.
