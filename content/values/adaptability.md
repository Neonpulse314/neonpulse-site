Nina had been pre-law since she was seventeen.

She knew this about herself the way she knew she was right-handed. It wasn't a decision anymore; it was a fact. She had taken the classes, read the cases, interned at the firm junior year, scored in the 90th percentile on the LSAT on her first try. People introduced her at parties as "going to law school." She introduced herself that way too.

The internship was three months. By week six, she was technically excellent and quietly miserable.

---

She couldn't name the feeling at first. It wasn't the hours — she'd expected the hours. It wasn't the work — contracts and briefs and filings, serious and precise. She was good at it. Her supervisor said so directly, which almost never happened.

It was something smaller and harder to locate.

The partners lit up talking about their cases. Rebecca, a senior associate she had lunch with twice, had this specific way of leaning forward when explaining a legal argument — the way people lean forward when the thing they're talking about *actually matters to them*. When the case was interesting, Rebecca's whole face changed.

Nina tried to notice if that happened to her.

It didn't.

---

She kept going. She was three weeks from the end of the internship and her law school applications were already outlined, recommenders already asked. She told herself she just hadn't found the right area of law. Criminal. Environmental. Health policy. Something would click.

At their last lunch, Rebecca asked her a question: "If law didn't exist — if someone told you tomorrow it was gone — what would you do instead?"

Nina opened her mouth to answer.

Nothing came out.

She had an answer for what law school she wanted. She had an answer for her five-year plan. She did not have an answer for this.

Rebecca wasn't trying to unsettle her. She was asking the way someone asks who genuinely wants to know. She said she'd asked herself the same question in year two at the firm, and that the answer had surprised her enough that she'd spent a year sitting with it before she trusted it.

"The people who stay," Rebecca said, "mostly had a real answer."

---

Nina spent the next month fighting the obvious conclusion.

She told herself she was just tired. She told herself the internship was an incomplete sample. She told herself that lots of people don't love their jobs and still have good lives, which is true but is also a thing people tell themselves when they're not ready to look at something directly.

The thing she kept running into was this: she was not afraid of law school. She was not afraid of the work. She was afraid of giving up the plan. And those are not the same thing.

The plan had started as a vehicle — a way to get somewhere. Somewhere meaningful, financially stable, respected. But somewhere along the way the plan had become the destination itself. The law degree was no longer the path to the life she wanted; it had become the proof that she was the kind of person who followed through. And she'd started optimizing for the proof.

This is what psychologists call identity foreclosure: choosing a path early, committing hard, and stopping the search — not because you've found what you want, but because the searching feels too costly.

---

The sunk cost question came next.

*Did I waste six years?*

This is the wrong question, but it's the one everyone asks, because it's the one that makes changing course feel like self-betrayal. If you leave, you're saying the investment was a mistake. You're admitting you were wrong. And who wants to be wrong for six years?

But the investment isn't erased by changing direction. Nina had learned to work at the pace that serious things require. She had learned to read precisely, argue clearly, tolerate ambiguity in a document and hold contradictory facts without flinching. She had learned that most people quit when the material gets hard, and she wasn't one of them.

None of that disappears because she decides to use it differently.

The six years aren't a loss. They're just not a mortgage on the next thirty.

---

Adaptability is not the same as resilience.

Resilience is recovering from a blow — getting knocked down, getting back up, continuing. It assumes the path is correct and the problem is the obstacle.

Adaptability is something harder: updating the map when the territory changes. Or when you realize the map was drawn from memory, and the memory was wrong.

It requires a specific kind of honesty — not the honesty of admitting failure, but the honesty of admitting that the thing you worked hard for might not be the thing you actually want. That your plan might have been right for who you were at seventeen, and you're not seventeen anymore, and that's not a tragedy.

The cost is real. You have to grieve the version of yourself you thought you were becoming. You have to explain yourself to people who knew the plan. You have to tolerate the stretch of time when you've let go of what you were doing but haven't yet found what you're doing instead — that empty stretch that feels like failure from the inside and usually just looks like thinking from the outside.

---

Nina didn't know what she was going to do.

That was the honest position when she finished the internship: she didn't know. She had some ideas. She was paying attention to different things. She noticed that the parts of the work she'd actually liked — synthesizing complex information, finding the argument that clarified instead of confused, making complicated things legible to people who needed them — existed in a lot of fields, not just one.

She didn't reapply to law school that cycle.

She didn't have a new plan. She had a direction, and she had the discipline she'd been building for six years, and she had a clearer picture of what she was actually optimizing for.

That turned out to be enough to start.

---

The thing about the map is that it's not the territory. It's your best current guess about the territory. And your best current guess at seventeen, or twenty, or twenty-three, is made with the information available at the time — which is always incomplete.

Updating the map isn't betraying the journey. It's what the journey is for.
