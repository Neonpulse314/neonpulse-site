Dev, 23, had wanted to do graduate engineering since his second year of undergrad.

Not vaguely — specifically. He'd researched programs, talked to professors, built his thesis toward it. He'd applied to four schools and gotten into two. The one in Montreal was the better one. He'd told his parents the night the acceptance came. His dad had gotten up from the dinner table and hugged him in the particular way he did, which was to put both hands on Dev's shoulders and hold on for a moment without saying anything.

His dad had the stroke in May.

It wasn't fatal. Partial paralysis on the left side, speech mostly intact, some cognitive effects that were hard to map in the early weeks. His mom was 59 and worked full-time and had never lived alone. His sister was in Vancouver with two kids under five. The math was not complicated.

Dev deferred. He told the program he'd had a family medical situation. They were kind about it. He sent the email, closed his laptop, and went back to the hospital waiting room where his mom was sitting in one of the orange chairs with a Styrofoam cup she wasn't drinking from.

He didn't tell most of his friends the whole story. When people asked why he wasn't going to Montreal, he said he wasn't ready yet. It was easier than the real answer, and the real answer made people say things he didn't want to hear, things about how selfless he was or how proud his parents must be, which were true but beside the point.

The first year was hard in ways he hadn't predicted. Not the logistics — those he handled, the drive to his parents' house, the grocery runs, the medical appointments, the paperwork he'd learned to read. That part was just work. What he hadn't predicted was who he was becoming in the absence of the other thing.

His friends from undergrad were in programs now. He followed them on Instagram without much feeling — labs, apartments, cities, the low-key chaos of first-year seminars. He registered the images without envy exactly. Something flatter than envy. Something like watching a movie of a version of his life and knowing you're not in it.

He told himself he'd reapply in a year.

Two years later, when his father had stabilized enough, he found himself looking at the graduate admissions page. The application was the same. The deadline was the same. He sat with it for a long time.

The person who had written those application essays — the one who'd gotten into Montreal — had been 22 and wanted things with the clean, uncomplicated hunger of someone who hadn't yet had to trade a want against a need. He'd been built by different things: late nights in the library, a particular professor who'd pushed him, the accumulating sense that something was coming.

He was 25 now. He'd been built by different things.

He hadn't gone anywhere. But he'd been changed by the staying the same way he would have been changed by the going. He was better with his parents than he'd ever been. He understood his father differently now, the way you understand someone when you've driven them to enough appointments and sat in enough silences. He'd become the person his family called when something needed deciding, not because he'd earned it exactly, but because he'd been there and that had turned into something.

He wasn't sure who the guy who moved to Montreal would have become. That was the thing he had to sit with: not just the opportunity but the self. The version of him that had been built by that program, those years, that city. That person didn't exist. Not because Dev had failed to become him, but because the choice had been made at a fork and you only walk one path.

He didn't think the choice was wrong. That wasn't the point.

The cost of sacrifice isn't the thing you give up. It's the version of yourself that would have been formed by the thing you gave up. That version doesn't wait for you. It doesn't get built in the background while you're doing something else. It just — doesn't happen. The fork was real. You walked one way.

Dev reapplied the following year. He got in. He went.

He's doing the thing he wanted to do. He's good at it. The years at home didn't hollow out the capability — they changed its shape. He's older than his cohort and less impressed by things that used to impress him and more patient than most of them in ways he can't always explain.

He still thinks sometimes about the version of himself that would have gone at 23. Wonders what that person would have figured out that he didn't. Whether it would have been better.

He'll never know. That's not a complaint. It's just what sacrifice is — the permanent not-knowing of the road you didn't take.

## Sources

- **Roese, N. J. (1997).** Counterfactual thinking. *Psychological Bulletin, 121*(1), 133–148. — comprehensive review of "what if" thinking; how people mentally simulate alternatives and the emotional consequences of upward counterfactuals (imagining better outcomes that didn't happen).
- **Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1991).** Loss aversion in riskless choice: A reference-dependent model. *Quarterly Journal of Economics, 106*(4), 1039–1061. — losses loom larger than equivalent gains; applies to opportunity cost and what is foregone in sacrifice.
- **Erikson, E. H. (1968).** *Identity: Youth and Crisis.* Norton. — identity formation in young adulthood; how the choices made in this period constitute the self, not merely express it.
- **Frankl, V. E. (1959).** *Man's Search for Meaning.* Beacon Press. — meaning-making through chosen suffering; the distinction between suffering that is chosen vs. imposed, and how sacrifice becomes meaningful through the orientation taken toward it.
- **Schulz, R., & Sherwood, P. R. (2008).** Physical and mental health effects of family caregiving. *American Journal of Nursing, 108*(9 Suppl), 23–27. — empirical review of how caregiving transforms identity, relationships, and life trajectory; confirms that the caregiver role is not merely a temporary interruption but a formative experience.
