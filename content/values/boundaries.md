Rosa had a reputation at work for being easy to work with.

She took the extra task when someone asked. She stayed late when the timeline slipped. She answered messages on weekends without being asked, because she could see they were there and ignoring them felt worse than answering.

She was twenty-two. She thought this was what being good at your job looked like.

---

It was a coworker named Devon who made it visible.

Devon was fine. He wasn't cruel or calculating. He just had good instincts for who would say yes. And Rosa had made herself easy to read.

Over eight months, it became a pattern: the extra slides, the coverage when he had a conflict, the projects that were "just going to be fifteen minutes." Each one was reasonable on its own. None of them were unreasonable. But stacked up, they occupied hours of her week that she never accounted for.

She found herself thinking about Devon's next request before it arrived, already building the argument for why she couldn't take it this time, then second-guessing herself before she had to say anything, then saying yes.

She was tired in a way she couldn't explain to anyone, because nothing specific had happened.

---

The thing that changed was minor.

Devon sent a message on a Friday afternoon. A document that needed review before Monday. She read it. She sat with it for a moment.

She typed: *I can't take this one. I'm already at capacity this week.*

She sent it before she finished deciding whether to.

His reply came back in three minutes. *No worries. I'll figure it out.*

That was it.

---

She didn't feel victorious. She felt unsettled in a way she needed to think through.

Because the world had not broken. Devon was fine. The work got handled. Nothing she'd been afraid of happened.

But now she understood something she'd been able to avoid before.

Every time she'd said yes in the past year, she'd told herself some version of: *I don't really have a choice, I don't know how to say no, it would be awkward.* Those stories were protecting her from having to choose. She was agreeable by default, which meant she was never responsible for the agreeableness.

She'd said no once. On a Friday afternoon. And now she knew it was possible.

Which meant every previous yes had been a choice. Just a choice she'd made without calling it that.

---

Here's what nobody tells you about setting a limit: you don't find the line. You draw it.

There's no natural place where you're supposed to stop. The work will expand to fill whatever you make available. The people around you — even the good ones, even the ones who aren't Devon — will adjust to what you offer without necessarily thinking about what it costs you.

That's not malice. That's just how it works.

The limit is yours to place. And until you place it, you're not agreeable — you're just undefined.

---

The cost of setting a boundary is that you lose the story that you couldn't.

That story was useful. It kept you innocent. You weren't choosing to give everything away, you just didn't know how to stop.

Once you've said no once — quietly, briefly, to something small — that story is gone. Now you know you can draw the line. Every time you don't, you're making a decision. You're accountable for it in a way you weren't before.

That's heavier in some ways. You can't point at the circumstances anymore.

But it's also more honest. You're not a victim of your own availability. You're the person who decides what you make available.

And that person gets to choose differently tomorrow.
