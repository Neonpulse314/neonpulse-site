Sasha had been writing for three years.

He was a reader before he was a writer — the kind who would finish a short story and sit with it for a minute, just feeling the weight of how it ended. He'd been doing that since he was fifteen. By twenty-one, his taste was genuinely good. He could tell within two pages whether something had the quality he was looking for. He could tell you exactly why a scene wasn't working, what the writer had been reaching for and where they'd missed it.

He joined a fiction workshop his second year of university. He was one of the better readers in the room. His feedback was sharp. He saw things other people missed.

What he couldn't show anyone was his own work.

---

It wasn't that he didn't write.

He wrote all the time — notebooks, documents, folders on his desktop with names like *abandoned* and *not yet* and *maybe*. He had the beginnings of forty or fifty stories. A few of them had real openings, the kind that made you want to keep reading. He'd start a draft, get twelve or fifteen pages in, and hit the moment where what was on the page failed to match what was in his head. Where the writing went ordinary. Where he could feel the gap between what he was making and what he wanted it to be.

He'd close the document.

Not because he was stuck exactly. He knew where the story was going. He just couldn't make it get there the way it deserved.

After three years, he'd finished six things. Two he'd shown to anyone. One he submitted somewhere and it came back rejected, which he knew in advance it would be because it wasn't ready, which is why he'd almost not submitted it, which is why submitting it felt like evidence of something he'd rather not look at directly.

---

His workshop professor was a woman who'd published three novels. She held office hours on Tuesday afternoons, in a small office that smelled like coffee and old paperbacks. Sasha went once, in October, with the opening of a new story.

She read it in front of him, which felt like being watched through glass.

When she finished she said: "This is a strong opening. You have good instincts." A pause. "How many stories have you finished?"

He said six.

"In how long?"

"Three years."

She set the pages down and looked at him with something that wasn't quite pity but was adjacent to it. "The writers I've watched develop — the ones who actually got somewhere — they all have one thing in common. It's not talent. It's not taste. They finished things. A lot of things. Things they knew weren't working."

He said he understood that, but that finishing something that wasn't good felt like—

She cut him off, gently. "What does it feel like?"

He had to think about it. "Like making the bad thing permanent. Like proving I can't do what I think I can do."

She nodded. "That's the gap," she said. "Your taste is ahead of your skill. That's true for every good writer. The difference is what you do with it. Some people use it as an excuse to stop. The ones who don't — they treat the gap as the terrain. They walk through it. They finish the bad story. Then the next one. They don't shortcut past the gap by protecting their standards. They just work until the skill catches up."

---

He thought about that on the walk home.

The thing is, he'd told himself he was protecting his standards. Every abandoned draft was a choice not to compromise. He wasn't going to publish something that wasn't ready. He wasn't going to put his name on something that fell short of what it was trying to be.

But his professor hadn't asked about publishing. She'd asked about finishing.

There was a distinction there he'd been collapsing. Finishing wasn't publishing. Finishing was making the thing all the way through — taking it from the idea to the ending, even when the middle went flat, even when the third act was weaker than it should be, even when the whole thing landed ten percent short of the version in his head. Finishing was the practice run. The draft. The thing you made so the next thing could be better.

He'd been protecting himself from the evidence of his own skill. Which meant he'd been protecting the gap. Keeping it intact. Making sure it never had to close.

---

He went home and opened the most recent abandoned file — seventeen pages of a story about two brothers and an inheritance dispute that had stopped being interesting to him somewhere around page twelve.

He finished it. It took him two weeks. It wasn't good. The ending was abrupt. The older brother wasn't differentiated enough from the younger one. There was a scene in the middle that served no purpose he could defend.

He submitted it to workshop anyway.

The feedback was what he expected — the problems he already knew, plus a few he hadn't seen. His professor said the ending was rushed but the voice was distinct throughout, and she could feel him working toward something in the middle that didn't quite arrive yet.

*Yet.*

He started the next one the following Monday.

---

Creativity isn't taste. That's the part people confuse.

Taste is what draws you in. Taste is what tells you the thing you just made isn't good enough. Taste is easy — or at least, it's cheap. It costs nothing to recognize quality. It costs nothing to know that your work falls short of it.

Skill costs something specific. It costs you the protection of never being wrong in public. It costs you the clean self-image that comes from only showing the finished, polished version of yourself. It costs you the comfort of not being measured.

The gap between taste and skill is where most creative people stop. Not because they don't have what it takes. Because the gap is uncomfortable, and finishing the bad thing is uncomfortable, and it's very easy to tell yourself you're waiting for the moment when you can do it right.

That moment doesn't come from waiting. It comes from finishing.

The bad story is the bridge. Every one you finish is a step closer to the version you can see but can't make yet. You don't shortcut to the other side. You walk through.

---

Sasha published his first story three years later — not the workshop piece, something he wrote in a single weekend two summers after. A journal picked it up. Small, but real.

He'd finished thirty-four stories by then. He thought most of them were bad. He thought maybe eight were actually good.

Thirty-four was how he got to eight.
