Kai was 21 when his father asked if they could start over.

It was a text. Three sentences. *I know I wasn't there. I want to do better. If you're open to it.*

He sat with it for two days.

---

His father hadn't left. He'd just... not been in the room. Same house for 18 years, but always working late, always tired, always available to other people in ways that never seemed to have enough left over for Kai. The kids at school whose dads had actually left — at least those dads were a story. Kai's dad was absent in the confusing way that doesn't have a good name.

He'd learned not to need things from him. Not a decision. Just over time, request by unanswered request, until the channel quietly closed.

---

The text had come after his grandmother's funeral. Something about watching his father stand alone at the graveside — 62 years old, softer than Kai remembered, not knowing where to put his hands — had shifted something. Not sympathy, exactly. More like noticing that his father was a person and not just a deficit.

Still. Two days on the text.

Here's what kept stopping him: the story of being the kid whose dad wasn't there had explained things. His difficulty asking for help. The way he'd always been comfortable alone. The specific competence he'd developed for not needing anyone to come through. It had made sense of a lot of things. It had made *him* make sense to himself.

If he forgave his father — really forgave, not performed forgiveness while quietly keeping score — he'd have to give up the explanation. He'd still have the things the absence shaped. He'd still be the person who'd learned to do everything on his own. But he'd lose the story of why. He'd just be that way, without anyone to blame for it.

---

He texted back: *Sure. Let's get coffee.*

Not because the absence hadn't been real. Not because the missed games and dropped promises hadn't cost him anything. Because he was 21 and his father was 62 and one of them was going to die first, and the thing Kai was holding — the rightness of it, the legitimacy of his grievance — weighed something. And he was tired.

---

Forgiveness isn't amnesia. It doesn't say the thing didn't happen.

It just means you stop making the person pay for it — including yourself. Because you were paying too, every time you tended the grievance. Every time you made sure you were still the one who'd been wronged.

You put the weight down. You don't stop being the person who was shaped by the lifting. You just stop carrying it now.

That's the cost: you lose the story. The explanation. The thing that made you make sense to yourself.

You're still you. You just don't know whose fault that is anymore.
