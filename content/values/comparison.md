Priya was 22 and finishing her last semester when the announcements started.

LinkedIn first, then Instagram. Her classmates were posting job offers, internships in cities that sounded real — New York, London, Vancouver. One of them had a photo on a rooftop in Tokyo with a caption about "starting the next chapter." Another had already moved in with her boyfriend, furnished apartment, plants on every surface. A third had 4,000 followers and was apparently making money just by having opinions.

Priya was living at home with her parents, job applications going nowhere, eating cereal at 11am in the same kitchen she'd eaten cereal in since she was eight. When she put her phone down, she felt a specific kind of tired that had nothing to do with sleep.

It wasn't envy exactly. It was more like the slow accumulation of evidence that everyone else had figured something out that she hadn't.

---

In 1954, a psychologist named Leon Festinger published a theory that explained what Priya was experiencing — and why she couldn't stop doing it.

Festinger's **Social Comparison Theory** starts from a simple observation: humans have a drive to evaluate themselves, and they do it by comparing to others. Not because they're insecure or vain, but because comparison is one of the primary tools we use to understand where we stand. How good am I at this? Is my opinion reasonable? Do I have enough? These questions don't come with objective meters attached. We answer them by looking sideways.

It's a navigation system. You use the people around you as landmarks to figure out where you are.

The problem is that the system was built for a world where you only knew a few hundred people — people you saw in full, not in curated excerpts. When Festinger was writing, your comparison group was your actual neighborhood. You saw your neighbor struggling with her marriage and your coworker anxious about money and your cousin who seemed confident but drank too much. You had the whole picture. The comparison had *information*.

Social media handed that system a firehose of highlight reels and called it data.

---

The research on what this does is not ambiguous.

A 2014 study by Vogel, Rose, and Roberts exposed participants to Facebook profiles of people who presented either extremely positively, neutrally, or not at all. Those who spent more time with the positive profiles rated themselves lower on attractiveness, success, and how well their life was going. Not lower than reality — lower than before they'd looked. The comparison didn't reveal something. It created something.

A 2015 study by Verduyn and colleagues found that *passive* social media use — scrolling, observing, not engaging — was specifically linked to lower wellbeing, while active use (commenting, messaging, actually talking to people) wasn't. The damage came from watching. From consuming a stream of other people's carefully assembled moments without any of the context that makes them make sense.

What Festinger called **upward comparison** — comparing to people who seem better off — can be useful when you have access to *how* they got there. "She got better at this by doing X" gives you something actionable. Watching someone's vacation photos gives you nothing except a vague feeling that your life doesn't measure up.

---

Here is what the comparison is actually doing wrong.

You know your full experience. You know about the three applications you sent last week that got no response. You know what your 2am thoughts sound like. You know the fights you had, the plans that didn't work out, the days where you did nothing useful and couldn't explain why.

You know exactly one person's full, unedited life: yours.

Everyone else you compare to is constructed from what they've chosen to show you. The rooftop in Tokyo is real. What you don't see is the panic attack the night before the flight, or the relationship she's not sure about, or the job offer that felt exciting to announce but already feels hollow. Not because she's hiding the bad stuff on purpose — but because we all narrate outward from the moments that seem worth narrating. The highlight reel isn't dishonest. It's just incomplete, in a very specific and one-directional way.

So the comparison is structurally rigged. You're putting your full unedited interior life up against someone else's published version. It's not even close to a fair contest. It's like comparing a rough draft to a finished book.

---

Priya eventually noticed something she couldn't unsee: the people she compared herself to most weren't the ones she knew best.

She knew her friend Danielle well enough to see past the Instagram caption. Danielle had the city job and the apartment — and also the bone-deep exhaustion, the loneliness she hadn't admitted yet, the credit card debt she wasn't talking about. Knowing Danielle well didn't make Priya feel better about her own situation. But it made the comparison real instead of cartoonish. Two actual people navigating two different hard things. No clear winner.

The comparison that hurt was always with someone she knew at the surface level. Enough context to be impressed, not enough to be accurate.

---

Festinger also identified **downward comparison** — comparing yourself to people who seem worse off. People do this too, and it works, in the short term. It produces a little lift. The problem is that it requires someone else to be suffering for you to feel better, which is not a great foundation for how you think about yourself or other people. And the lift doesn't last.

What actually works — what the research and the harder-won human experience both point toward — is something closer to what Jordan Peterson describes in a line that sounds simple until you try it: *compare yourself to who you were yesterday, not to who someone else is today.*

This isn't motivational-poster advice. It's a genuinely different information source. Yesterday's version of you is someone you know fully. You have access to all the context. The comparison is fair, because it's the same person. And the direction of the gap tells you something useful — not "I'm behind," but "I moved" or "I didn't move" and here's what that might mean.

---

The problem isn't comparison. You can't turn off the navigation system — and you wouldn't want to. What you can do is notice when it's using bad data.

When you feel the familiar heavy feeling after scrolling, the question worth asking isn't *why am I not where they are.* It's: *am I comparing my full experience to their published version again?*

Almost always, the answer is yes. And that doesn't make the feeling go away — but it does make it less like evidence.

Priya didn't stop looking at Instagram. She did stop treating it as an accurate report on where other people's lives actually were. She started thinking of it more like advertising: made by people who wanted to show themselves well, which is human and understandable, but not the same thing as true.

She was still in her parents' kitchen. The applications were still going nowhere. But she stopped using other people's highlight reels to determine whether her life was adding up.

That gap — between her and the rooftop in Tokyo — turned out to be a comparison between two different categories. Not two lives with a clear winner.

Just two lives, with different visible parts.
