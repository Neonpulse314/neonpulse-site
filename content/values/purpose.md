---
title: "Purpose — The Weight of Having One"
summary: "Theo had a decent job and no complaints. Then one Saturday volunteer shift changed the shape of everything — and comfortable drift stopped being an option."
section_label: "Values"

---

The Saturday that changed things, Theo almost didn't go.

He'd signed up for the youth coding workshop on a Tuesday, feeling civic-minded. By Friday he was tired and the weather was grey and his couch was right there. He went anyway — because he'd committed, because it felt like the kind of thing you shouldn't back out of.

He was twenty-four. He had a marketing job that was fine. He wasn't unhappy. He just didn't know what any of it was for.

---

Three hours into the session, a fourteen-year-old named Priya got her first loop to run correctly. The console printed "hello" eight times. She looked up with an expression Theo didn't know how to describe — something between shock and ownership, like she'd just discovered she could do a thing she thought belonged to other people.

He helped her debug the next problem. Then the one after that.

On the drive home, he knew.

Not "I should probably do something more meaningful with my life." Not "maybe I'd like teaching someday." *Knew*, the way you know when something fits — not because it's easy but because the discomfort has a different quality to it. Good difficult instead of meaningless difficult.

---

Here's what nobody tells you about finding your purpose: it doesn't feel like freedom.

It feels like a door closing.

Before that Saturday, Theo had options. Marketing was fine. He could have moved into strategy, or product, or started something. He could have drifted for another few years and called it exploring. None of those paths were wrong, exactly. They just hadn't been weighed against anything.

After that Saturday, they were.

That's the cost. Once you know what you actually want, comfortable drift stops being an option — because now you know what you're drifting away from. The fine job becomes a clock counting the wrong thing. The reasonable path becomes a choice you're making, over and over, to not be where you want to be.

---

He enrolled in a teaching certification program. His salary dropped by half. His parents asked careful questions. His friends said things like *that's so admirable* in a tone that meant *I wouldn't do that*.

He didn't need them to understand it. That was new — not needing approval to hold the thing.

But he also couldn't pretend it was easy. Purpose doesn't protect you from doubt or exhaustion or the months when nothing seems to be working. It doesn't make you certain. It just makes the uncertainty point somewhere specific.

Before, he'd had no direction. That felt like freedom. It wasn't. It was just the absence of anything that could demand something from him.

---

Purpose demands.

That's the part people don't say out loud, because it sounds like a complaint about a good thing. But it's real: once you have it, you're accountable to it. You can't undiscover it. You can ignore it, defer it, talk yourself out of it — but you'll know you're doing that, and the knowing changes what silence costs you.

Before you find your purpose, you can blame circumstance for where you are.

After, there's only you.

---

Theo teaches seventh grade now. The job is harder than marketing in every measurable way — less money, more noise, more that can go wrong on any given day.

He doesn't regret the Saturday he almost skipped.

He does, sometimes, think about the version of himself who never went. Who kept the decent job and no complaints. Who didn't know what he was for.

That version had more options.

He's not sure anymore whether that was a good thing.
