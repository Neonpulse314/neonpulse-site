Jordan had never eaten at a restaurant alone.

This sounds like a small thing until you count the evidence: not a coffee shop, not a food court, not a twenty-minute lunch. She'd sat alone in waiting rooms, on buses, between classes — but those were transitional spaces, not spaces where you were visible. Where you sat down and stayed. Where a hostess looked at you and said *just one?* and you had to say yes out loud.

She didn't have a reason for this, exactly. She just preferred company. Always had. Since she was fourteen she'd had the same six or seven people she moved through the world with, and when they weren't available she made herself unavailable too. She was good at it. She'd gotten so good at filling her schedule with other people that she'd almost forgotten she was doing it.

---

In October of her junior year, her roommate went home for a long weekend.

Jordan usually planned around this — called someone, made plans, made the emptiness brief. This time the timing went wrong. She woke up Friday morning and the apartment was quiet and her phone had died in the night and the cord was across the room.

She lay in bed for a while. Long enough that it stopped being accidental.

She could get up, get her phone, start the day. That would have been normal. Instead she lay there in the quiet and felt something she didn't have a word for. Not loneliness exactly — loneliness assumed there was someone absent. This was more like being in a room with a stranger. Except she was the only one in the room.

---

She got up eventually.

She didn't get her phone. She made coffee and sat at the kitchen table and tried to figure out what she felt like doing.

This took longer than it should have.

She'd been asked *what do you want to do* her whole life, and she'd always had an answer — not a real one, but a social one. The thing that worked for the group. The thing that got general approval. She was good at figuring out what other people wanted and making it sound like what she wanted too. She'd told herself this was flexibility. Being easy to be around.

Sitting alone at the kitchen table, she heard the question differently. Not *what works for everyone* — just *what do you want.*

She sat with it. She kept reaching for the answer and finding it wasn't there. Not because she was incapable of preference — but because preference required something she hadn't practiced. It required knowing who was doing the preferring.

---

She walked that afternoon without a destination, which she also hadn't done in years.

She bought a coffee she didn't need, just to have something to hold. She walked past a bookstore and went in and spent forty minutes in the section on natural history, which she hadn't thought about since she was twelve and obsessed with deep ocean creatures. She bought a book about bioluminescence. Nobody knew she'd bought it. Nobody would see her read it. It would just be hers.

The weekend passed slowly, and then less slowly.

By Sunday she'd started to feel the shape of herself. Not the social version — the real one, which was quieter and stranger and more specific than the one she deployed for other people. It turned out she liked mornings more than evenings. It turned out she didn't actually like the music she'd been listening to for two years — she'd adopted it because someone she liked listened to it. It turned out she had opinions about things she'd never said out loud, because she'd never needed to.

She hadn't said them out loud now. But she knew they were there.

---

The self you build from mirrors is borrowed.

It's not fake, exactly. It's assembled from other people's expectations of you. You figure out what makes you likeable and you perform it, and after long enough the performance gets comfortable enough that you stop thinking of it as a performance. You think: this is who I am.

Until you're alone and the mirrors are gone and you notice there's noise inside you trying to fill the space that other people usually fill. And the question underneath the noise is one you've been avoiding: who are you when nobody's watching?

Solitude isn't loneliness. Loneliness is wanting company and not having it. Solitude is different — it's presence with yourself. It's not comfortable at first, because you haven't practiced it, and most people haven't.

The cost of never practicing: you borrow a self from everyone around you. You become very good at being whoever the room needs. You lose the ability to tell the difference between what you chose and what you adopted. And borrowed things, eventually, have to go back.

---

Jordan finished the book about bioluminescence. It took her most of the semester, between everything else.

She didn't tell anyone she was reading it. She didn't post it anywhere.

It was just hers. And there's something about that — having a thing that's just yours. It changes the ratio slightly. You go from being entirely a reflection to being something that also produces its own light.

She started eating alone sometimes. Not always. Just when the schedule happened to open up that way, and she let it.

She stopped minding *just one* so much. She'd started to think of herself as a person whose company she was okay with.

That turned out to matter more than she'd expected.
