Maya knew the word for why the sky turned red at sunset.

She just didn't know what it meant.

She'd known the word since ninth grade — *Rayleigh scattering* — and if someone at a party brought up the sky or the physics of light, she could deploy it. She could even say something about shorter wavelengths. She'd gotten comfortable with the fact that this was knowing something. She'd accumulated a lot of things like this: the word for the smell of rain on dry earth (*petrichor*), the name of the fallacy where you assume something is true because a lot of people believe it (*argumentum ad populum*), why planes don't fall. She could hold her own in almost any conversation because she had the label for most things.

She was twenty-two years old and had never once asked *but why does that happen?* and actually waited for the answer.

---

Her brother Theo was nine.

He asked *why* constantly. Not as a conversational move — he actually wanted to know. He'd ask one thing and then ask why about the answer and then ask why about *that*, until you arrived somewhere you couldn't follow him. She'd found this endearing when he was five. By the time he was eight it had started to feel exhausting. She caught herself giving him answers designed to be satisfying enough that he'd stop.

She took him to the science museum on a Saturday in March because her mother asked her to and she had nothing else going on.

---

They stood in front of a plasma ball — one of those glass globes with lightning inside that tracks your fingers. Theo pressed both palms against it and watched the light bend toward him.

"Why does it follow my hands?" he said.

Maya opened her mouth. She had a shape of an answer — electricity, conductivity — but she realized, mid-breath, that she didn't actually know. She knew that it had to do with the electrical path seeking ground through the touch. But *why* the light, why the plasma, what was actually happening in there? She didn't know.

"I think it's because your body conducts electricity," she said.

"But why does it *want* to go to my hand?"

She looked at him. "It doesn't want to," she said. "It doesn't have wants."

"But it goes to my hand."

"Yeah."

"So why does it go?"

She didn't know. She said she didn't know, which came out of her mouth stiffly, like something she hadn't done in a while.

Theo said "let's find out" and walked toward the information panel on the wall like this was completely normal.

---

They spent four hours in the museum.

Not because Maya planned to. Because Theo kept stopping. He stopped at everything. He didn't glide through exhibits accumulating impressions the way she'd expected — the way she did in museums, reading the first two lines of every placard and nodding. He stopped and stared and asked questions and waited for answers. When the answers led somewhere he didn't understand, he said so.

He had no investment in seeming like he already knew.

Maya stood next to him in front of a display about the color of stars and realized she'd been in museums her whole adult life without actually being in them. She'd been performing museum-going — the slow walk, the tilted chin, the thoughtful expression. She wasn't there to learn. She was there to confirm that she was the kind of person who went to museums.

When had she decided that performing knowing was the same as knowing?

---

There's a theory in psychology that curiosity is triggered by a gap.

Not by ignorance — by *awareness* of ignorance. The moment you realize there's a specific thing you don't know, and that it's in principle knowable, something turns on. Curiosity is the itch. Wonder is what happens when the gap is large enough to feel vertiginous — when you realize not just that you don't know *this*, but that you don't know *how much you don't know*.

Maya had managed, for years, to avoid both sensations.

The trick was labels. When you have a label for something, the gap closes. The question feels answered. *Rayleigh scattering* — got it. *Photosynthesis* — got it. *Cognitive dissonance* — got it. The label is satisfying. It doesn't feel like there's a gap because you have a word for the space.

But the word isn't the thing. The label isn't the understanding. And there's a version of education — especially the kind optimized for performance — that teaches you to confuse the two.

---

They stopped at a window that overlooked an atrium, and Theo pointed at the sunset going pink through the glass.

"Why does the sky turn that color?" he said.

She almost said *Rayleigh scattering*. The word came up immediately, automatic, a reflex.

She didn't say it.

Instead she said: "I don't actually know why it happens, just the name for it. Do you want to figure it out?"

He looked at her like she'd done something surprising.

They looked it up. Sunlight, white, all wavelengths combined. When it travels through a short length of atmosphere, it looks white. When it travels through a long length — at dawn or sunset, at a low angle — the short wavelengths scatter away in all directions and the longer ones come through. Red travels. Blue scatters. That's why the sky is blue overhead and red at the horizon. That's Rayleigh scattering: light sorted by distance.

She'd known the word for eight years. She understood it for the first time at twenty-two, standing next to her nine-year-old brother in a science museum.

It felt different from the word. It felt like something she actually had.

---

The cost of losing wonder is specific.

You stop being surprised. And a person who can't be surprised can only update their opinions — they can't form new understanding. Understanding requires a moment of not-knowing, of genuine openness, of letting the thing be more complicated than your label for it. You can't get there from performance. You can only get there from actually asking the question.

Wonder isn't ignorance. It's the willingness to let the gap stay open long enough to feel it.

It requires a certain kind of bravery that looks, from the outside, like not being very impressive. You have to admit you don't know. You have to ask the question a nine-year-old would ask. You have to be willing to sit with *I don't understand this yet*, which in a culture that rewards confident answers feels like weakness.

But it's not weakness. It's the only way in.

The people who keep their capacity for wonder intact are the people who keep growing. Not because they know less — because they keep noticing what they don't know and letting it matter.

---

Maya started asking *why* more after that.

Not constantly. Not performatively. She didn't become her brother — she was still a person who liked having opinions. But she started noticing the labels in her head and checking, occasionally, whether there was anything behind them. Sometimes there was. Sometimes she'd understood more than she realized. And sometimes there was just the word, hanging there in front of a gap she'd been papering over for years.

Those were the good moments. Those were the ones where something actually happened.

She'd been in so many museums. She'd seen so many exhibits. And she'd spent most of that time looking at placards instead of things.

She started looking at the things.
