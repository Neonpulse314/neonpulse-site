Jake had been on his phone for forty minutes before he noticed he wasn't enjoying it.

He'd scrolled through everything — news, feeds, videos, messages. Nothing was bad exactly. Nothing was interesting. He kept refreshing things that hadn't changed, watching things he'd already half-watched, waiting for something to land. It didn't.

He put the phone down. Picked it up again. Put it down.

He was bored. Which was strange, because he'd spent forty minutes trying not to be.

---

## What Boredom Actually Is

Boredom is not the absence of stimulation. You can be bored in a loud room, at a party, halfway through a movie you chose. What boredom signals is something more specific: a mismatch between the engagement you want and what your current situation is offering.

Psychologist Sandi Mann at the University of Central Lancashire defines it as the state of wanting to engage with the world but being unable to — a kind of frustrated attention. You're not checked out. You're looking for something worth checking into, and not finding it.

This distinction matters because it changes what boredom is pointing at. If it were just the absence of input, you could fix it by adding more input. The fact that Jake's forty minutes of scrolling didn't touch his boredom at all is evidence that input wasn't what he needed.

Researcher John Eastwood's work at York University found something consistent: bored people aren't understimulated in a raw sense. They're failing to connect their attention to something that feels meaningful. The stimulation is there. The connection isn't.

---

## What It's For

Boredom is aversive. Most people will do almost anything to escape it — including things that are objectively worse than sitting still. In a now-famous 2014 study, Timothy Wilson and colleagues found that a significant number of people, when left alone in a room with nothing to do, chose to give themselves mild electric shocks rather than sit quietly with their thoughts. People who had said beforehand that they'd pay money not to be shocked chose the shock over boredom.

This suggests that boredom, at some level, functions as a threat signal. The brain interprets "I have nothing to do" not as rest but as danger — as something being wrong that needs to be fixed.

But from an evolutionary standpoint, boredom probably served a purpose. It's the signal that says: *this situation isn't offering you what you need. Look elsewhere. Find something better.* In environments where food, safety, and connection were scarce and finding them required effort, a state that pushed you to go search for something more meaningful would have been genuinely useful.

The problem is that modern environments have stacked that signal with infinite low-value options. Every time boredom fires, there's an app waiting. The boredom gets temporarily suppressed. But the thing it was pointing at — the need for genuine engagement, challenge, connection, or purpose — doesn't get addressed. It just waits.

## The Creativity Connection

Boredom research has a counterintuitive finding: it makes you more creative.

In a series of experiments, Sandi Mann and Rebekah Cadman had participants complete a boring task (copying numbers from a phone book) before a creative task (coming up with uses for plastic cups). Bored participants significantly outperformed controls on creative output.

The proposed mechanism is daydreaming. When attention has nothing to engage with, the default mode network activates — the brain's resting state, which is associated with mind-wandering, self-referential thought, imagining the future, and making unexpected connections. Boredom is one of the cleaner ways to trigger it.

This explains something most people notice and never quite account for: that ideas arrive in the shower, on long drives, on walks — in places where the mind has nothing specific to do. Not in the ten-second gaps between phone checks.

Constant low-level stimulation keeps the default mode network suppressed. The mind is always tracking something, always reacting. The connections that need space and time to form don't get either.

## The Discomfort Is the Point

There's a version of engaging with boredom that just means picking a better distraction. Go for a run instead of scrolling. Read a book instead of watching TV. These aren't bad ideas, but they're not quite the same as tolerating the boredom itself.

What's harder — and what the research suggests is more valuable — is sitting with the discomfort long enough to hear what it's saying.

Psychologists like Todd Kashdan who study psychological flexibility note that the capacity to tolerate uncomfortable internal states without immediately trying to eliminate them is a central feature of wellbeing. Not because suffering is good, but because most meaningful things require holding discomfort rather than escaping it — starting a project before you know if it will work, staying in a conversation that's going nowhere obvious, sitting with a question you can't answer yet.

Boredom is a low-stakes version of this. It's uncomfortable but safe. It's a practice round for the harder skill of not running from states that feel bad.

---

## What Chronic Avoidance Does

The evidence on what happens when boredom is never tolerated is not good.

Extensive research by Rahel Wolman, Mark Seery, and others connects high boredom-proneness — the tendency to feel bored easily and escape it compulsively — with depression, anxiety, substance use, and impulsive behavior. This isn't because boredom itself causes these things. It's because chronic escape from boredom correlates with chronic escape from any uncomfortable internal state — and internal states, including the ones that matter most, are mostly uncomfortable.

There's also a meaning dimension. Viktor Frankl, writing from his experience in Nazi concentration camps, argued that the most psychologically devastating state wasn't suffering — it was meaninglessness. He described boredom and apathy as symptoms of a life in which the connection between action and meaning has been severed. Boredom, at the extreme, isn't just a bad afternoon. It's the signal that something in the larger orientation is off.

Most boredom isn't that severe. But the mechanism is similar: boredom is the feeling of your attention looking for something worth caring about and not finding it. The chronic version isn't fixed by better distractions. It's fixed by finding things that actually warrant the attention you have.

---

Jake put the phone in another room.

He sat with the discomfort for maybe ten minutes. It wasn't pleasant. His mind kept proposing things he could be doing, ways to be useful, reasons this was wasteful.

Then, gradually, it quieted. He found himself thinking about a project he'd put aside months ago — something he'd been excited about and then lost the thread of. He didn't do anything about it that afternoon. But the thought was there.

He'd forgotten he'd wanted it until boredom ran out of other things to offer.
