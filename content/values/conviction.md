Priya had been at the firm for seven months when they started working on the Meridian pitch.

It was the biggest project her team had touched all year — a market entry strategy for a mid-sized client looking to expand into a new vertical. Twelve weeks of research, four rounds of internal review, a final presentation scheduled for Thursday. Her senior lead, Marcus, had been driving it. He was experienced, well-regarded, and visibly proud of what they'd built.

On Wednesday afternoon, the team ran through the deck one last time.

Priya sat in the back row and looked at slide fourteen.

---

The customer acquisition cost assumption was wrong.

Not slightly wrong — wrong in a way that would matter. The model assumed they could acquire customers at $80 each. Priya had spent the previous weekend reading competitor case studies for a different project, and she knew from that reading that the real number in this vertical ran closer to $220. If the CAC was $220, the payback period more than doubled. The unit economics didn't work.

She looked at the slide. She looked at Marcus, who was walking through the projections with confidence. She looked at her two other teammates, who were nodding.

She thought: maybe I'm wrong. Maybe I missed something. Maybe this is why I'm not the senior person.

She thought about that for about thirty seconds.

---

"Marcus," she said. "I have a question about the CAC assumption on slide fourteen."

The room shifted. You could feel it. Marcus paused.

"What about it?"

She walked through what she'd found. The competitor data. The range she'd seen. The way the payback period changed if the real number was closer to $220.

Marcus listened. His expression didn't change much, but something behind it did.

"Where are you getting $220?"

She explained.

There was a pause. Then Marcus said, "Let me pull up the sourcing."

---

He was quiet for a while, looking at his laptop.

The sourcing was thinner than he'd thought. The $80 figure had come from a benchmark report that was four years old and drawn from a different adjacent market. It wasn't wrong exactly — it was optimistic. The kind of optimistic that looks like rigor until someone pushes on it.

The presentation got pushed to the following Thursday. They spent two days rebuilding the financial model with a more conservative CAC range, adding a sensitivity analysis, reworking the recommendations. The final presentation was stronger for it — less confident in the wrong places, more honest about the risk.

The client appreciated the nuance. They said so.

---

Nobody said, *thank you, Priya, for catching that.*

That's not how these things go. There's no ceremony for the person who slowed things down at the last minute. Marcus didn't pull her aside and tell her she'd saved the pitch. Her teammates didn't congratulate her. What she got instead was the subtle social cost of having been the one to cause a delay — the junior person who'd created extra work for everyone the day before a big deadline.

She was, for a week or two, the one who asked too many questions.

---

Here's what conviction actually feels like from the inside: it doesn't feel like strength. It feels like standing at the edge of something and trying to decide if you're about to be a hero or an idiot.

The room is moving in one direction. The senior person is confident. Everyone else has bought in. You have a piece of information that contradicts the consensus, but you're not certain — you're never fully certain — and you're aware that if you're wrong you'll have wasted everyone's time and made yourself look bad.

You have to decide: is this social discomfort, or is this a real argument?

That's the question that matters. Not *do I feel confident* — because confidence is social. The question is whether you have a reason. A specific, traceable reason that doesn't dissolve when someone pushes back.

Priya had one. The data was real. The math was real. The discomfort of speaking up was real too, but it was a different kind of real.

---

Stubbornness is holding a position because you don't want to be wrong. Conviction is holding it because it's right — and being genuinely willing to let it go if someone shows you why it isn't.

Priya wasn't attached to being right. She would have been relieved if Marcus had pulled up better sourcing and shown her where she'd misread the data. She was hoping for that. She just needed him to show her.

He couldn't, because it wasn't there.

---

The version of her that stayed quiet would have sat in the Thursday presentation listening to the client ask about CAC assumptions. She would have watched Marcus handle the question without good data behind him. She would have known, quietly, what she had not said.

That's the cost of staying quiet when you're the only one who sees it: you have to carry it. The room moves forward. The plan goes out. And you know what you held back.

Conviction isn't the absence of doubt. It's doing the thing anyway, because you checked your reasons and they held.

The room was wrong. She said so. It mattered.

That's enough.
