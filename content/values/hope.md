Nadia had a list on her phone.

Not a bucket list. Not goals. Just a column of names — seven companies she'd applied to in three months. Next to six of them: a small x.

The seventh was still blank.

She'd been staring at her email for forty minutes. Not because the answer was slow. The answer had come at 11:14 AM. She just hadn't moved on from it.

*We've decided to move forward with another candidate.*

---

The thing about hope is that it costs something.

Not in the dramatic way — not in the way people mean when they say hope is risky or hope is fragile. In a smaller, more annoying way: to hope for something, you have to actually want it. And to want something is to be available to not getting it.

Nadia had wanted that job. Not desperately, not in a way she'd told anyone. But quietly — the way you let yourself imagine something before it happens. The route to the office. The desk. The first week where you still know nothing and everything feels new. She'd let herself imagine it. That was the investment. The rejection was just the statement that came back.

---

She opened a new tab.

Job listings. Same kind of page she'd opened and closed a hundred times. Same format, different names.

She told herself she was just looking.

She wasn't just looking. She already knew she was going to apply somewhere. She knew it the same way she'd known it after the first rejection, and the third, and the fifth.

This was the part she hadn't figured out how to explain to people: it wasn't optimism. She wasn't telling herself *this one will be different* or *you only need one yes*. She wasn't doing affirmations. She was doing something smaller and harder than that.

She was deciding to care again. Knowing what caring cost.

---

There's a version of hope that's passive — the kind where you wait for things to improve on their own and call it positive thinking. That's not this.

What Nadia was doing is the other kind: the active version, where you look at the number of times you've been disappointed and put your name in again anyway. Not because you're sure it'll work. Because the alternative is to stop putting your name in, and she wasn't ready to do that.

She started a new application. Left the list open on her phone.

One blank line. Still space on it.

---

Hope isn't the feeling that things will go well.

That's confidence, and sometimes confidence is warranted and sometimes it's delusion.

Hope is simpler. It's the decision to keep showing up to something you care about even when you have good reasons not to. Not believing you'll win. Believing the trying is still worth it.

The cost is real: every time you hope, you're choosing to be the kind of person who can be let down again.

A lot of people decide, at some point, that this is too expensive. They stop applying. Stop asking. Stop putting their name in. They call it being realistic.

What they're actually doing is stopping the clock on something that hurt them.

Nadia hit submit.
