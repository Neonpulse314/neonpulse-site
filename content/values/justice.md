Tariq had been at the job for four months when it happened.

The project had been rough — three weeks of late iterations, two rounds of feedback that contradicted each other, and a deadline that kept moving. The team had pushed through it. The client was happy. The manager called a debrief.

She went around the room, naming what had worked. When she got to the framework that pulled the whole thing together — the one that had saved the pitch — she looked at Tariq.

"That came from you. That's the kind of thinking that makes the difference."

The room looked at him. It felt good. He could feel how good it felt.

He glanced at Reena, who was sitting two seats down, her expression careful and still.

---

The framework had come from Reena.

It wasn't complicated. She'd sent it in a Slack message at 11pm on a Tuesday, during one of the bad weeks. Tariq had seen it, recognized immediately that it was the right move, and brought it into the meeting the next morning. He'd presented it the way you present things in meetings — with confidence, moving fast, not stopping to say *this came from someone else's late-night message.*

He hadn't stolen it. He'd just... moved with it.

But Reena had been there at 11pm. Reena had sent the message. And now Reena was sitting two seats away while the manager thanked someone else for her idea.

---

Tariq could see the path where he nodded.

It would have been easy. Nobody would have said anything. The manager didn't know. His other teammates probably didn't remember the Slack message. Reena hadn't spoken up, which meant she wasn't going to make it uncomfortable. He could just accept it, be grateful, use the goodwill.

He sat with it for three seconds, which was long enough.

"Actually," he said, "that one came from Reena. The Slack message on the Tuesday night — that was hers. I just brought it into the room."

The manager looked at Reena. "That was you?"

Reena said it was.

The manager thanked her. Moved on. The debrief continued.

---

Nobody gave Tariq credit for the correction.

That's not how it works. You don't get points for not taking something that wasn't yours. There was no applause, no moment of recognition. His manager just moved on to the next item. It wasn't even awkward. It was nothing.

Reena caught his eye afterward, briefly. She didn't say anything. She didn't need to.

He sat at his desk after the meeting and thought about what he had and hadn't done.

---

Here's the honest thing about justice: most of it doesn't happen in courtrooms.

It happens in debrief meetings when someone is praised for the wrong reason. It happens when you find a wallet and nobody's watching. It happens when the billing error is in your favor and the vendor will never notice. It happens in the small moments when you're holding something that doesn't belong to you and keeping it would be easy and costless.

Nobody is forcing you. The stakes aren't high. You'll never be caught if you don't say anything.

That's exactly when justice is a choice.

---

The cost of choosing it is real.

Tariq gave back something useful — goodwill he'd already mentally banked, a reputation boost that would have been hard to trace back to a mistake. He didn't get that. What he got instead was smaller and harder to explain: he knew what had actually happened in that room. He'd looked at a thing he could have kept and handed it back.

Justice doesn't feel heroic when you're doing it. There's no swell of music. You just say the true thing and let the moment settle.

But the version of yourself that doesn't say it — the one who nods and accepts and walks away knowing what you held onto — you'd have to carry that version forward. He'd have to sit in the next meeting next to Reena knowing what had happened.

He didn't want to carry that.

---

The question isn't whether you're a just person in the abstract. Most people are, in the abstract.

The question is what you do with the wallet in your hand. When there's no one watching. When keeping it is easy and returning it costs you something real.

That's when it counts.

That's when you find out what you actually believe.
