Alex could do five things at once.

He'd gotten good at it over four years of university — laptop open to the lecture slides, phone face-up on the desk, AirPods in with something low in one ear, browser tab switched to Reddit between professor pauses. He'd gotten a B+ average doing this. He thought he'd discovered something: that you could absorb what you needed by being loosely everywhere instead of precisely anywhere.

He was twenty-one and he had never, not once, given anything his full attention for longer than about twelve minutes.

He didn't know this. That was the thing about it — you can't feel what you're not noticing.

---

The summer internship was at a small structural engineering firm, twelve people, a converted house on the edge of the city. No open-plan office noise, no ambient busyness. It was quieter than anything Alex had worked in before. Disconcertingly quiet.

The senior engineer was named David. Late fifties. One monitor. No phone on his desk — it stayed in his bag. When he read something, he read it. When he worked on a calculation, he closed his email. When someone came to his office to talk, he turned away from the screen completely and looked at them.

Alex found this slightly uncomfortable. It felt like being examined.

---

Three weeks in, Alex was given a set of load calculations to review — cross-checking another engineer's numbers before the report went to the client. It was detail work. He did it the way he did most things: spread across an hour, glancing at the file between a Slack conversation about weekend plans and a video someone had sent. He marked it approved. The numbers looked right. Nothing jumped out.

David caught a sign error in his review. A negative where there should have been a positive. Small, consequential, the kind of mistake that compounds.

He didn't make it into a lesson. He just fixed it and sent Alex a one-line note: *Caught a sign error on p. 4 — worth another pass.*

Alex went back to the document. He read it again, this time with the phone face-down and nothing else open.

He found two more things he'd missed.

---

Later he asked David how he caught it. It wasn't a method question — he meant: how do you find the thing that looks fine until it isn't?

David said: "I read it once."

Alex waited for more. That was the whole answer.

"Most mistakes are visible," David said. "People just aren't there when they read. They're somewhere else. The page gets processed but nothing touches it."

---

There's research on this.

When you switch between tasks — even briefly, even voluntarily — you don't immediately bring your full attention to the new one. A portion of it stays with the previous task, tied up in whatever you left unresolved. Psychologists call this *attention residue*. The more you switch, the more of yourself you leave behind, until the thing in front of you gets only the part of you that's left over.

This isn't laziness. It's architecture. The brain doesn't actually multitask — it switches, rapidly, cycling between threads. What feels like doing two things at once is doing two things in fast alternation, and each switch costs something. Not much, each time. Enough, over a thousand switches a day.

The other thing: deep thinking requires a sustained state. It doesn't switch on immediately when you need it. You have to settle into it, which takes time — often twenty minutes or more of unbroken focus before you're doing your best work. If you never stay anywhere for twenty minutes, you never get there.

Alex had built a life of constant switching. He was very fast at the surface of things and had never developed any facility with the depth of them.

---

He started experimenting.

One task at a time, for stretches. Phone in his bag. He didn't announce it or make it into a personality thing — he just tried it and noticed what happened. At first it felt slow. He kept feeling the pull toward something else — the urge to check, to vary the input, to confirm that nothing was happening in the places he wasn't looking. He'd reach for his phone and then stop himself, not out of willpower but out of curiosity about what would happen if he didn't.

What happened: the work got better. Not incrementally — noticeably. He started catching his own errors before anyone else saw them. He started understanding problems more completely before he tried to solve them. He started producing things that didn't require three revisions.

He also noticed something else. The switching urge faded when he was actually inside something. When he got deep enough into a problem that it had its own gravity, the pull toward distraction disappeared. The problem was interesting in a way that didn't feel like entertainment. He was in it.

He'd heard the word *flow* before. He hadn't known what it meant until he'd felt it. It turned out you couldn't get there by intention. You got there by staying.

---

Attention isn't concentration.

Concentration is something you do by force. You grit your way through a task, holding yourself to it by willpower, and you're exhausted at the end. That's not what David did. What David did was simpler: he stopped going other places. He gave the thing in front of him permission to be the only thing. And when it had his attention completely, it gave him something back — he saw it more clearly, thought about it better, caught what he would have missed.

Attention is a resource, but it's not like money. You don't accumulate it by spending less. You build the capacity for it by practicing it, and you erode it by fragmenting it constantly. A person who has spent years giving fractional attention to everything hasn't built up a reserve — they've degraded the ability.

The cost of divided attention is invisible until it isn't. You think you're absorbing things. You're skimming. You think you're connecting with people. You're processing them. You think you caught everything. You missed the sign on page four.

---

David retired that October. On his last day he cleaned out his desk — it didn't take long, he didn't accumulate much — and stopped by Alex's desk on the way out.

Alex put his phone face-down. He looked up and actually looked at him.

David noticed. He didn't say anything about it. He just said goodbye.

But he said it to someone who was there.
