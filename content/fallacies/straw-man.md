Marcus had spent twenty minutes explaining why the city should invest in more bike lanes. He talked about reduced traffic, lower emissions, cheaper infrastructure costs per commuter, and comparable outcomes in European cities.

The councilman's response: "So you want to tear up every road and make car owners feel like criminals?"

Marcus hadn't said anything like that. But the room was already responding to the councilman's version.

---

## What It Is

The straw man fallacy happens when someone misrepresents your position — simplifying it, exaggerating it, or replacing it with a weaker or more extreme version — and then argues against that version instead of what you actually said.

The name comes from the metaphor: instead of fighting a real opponent, you build a stuffed dummy (a straw man), defeat it easily, and declare victory. It looks like a refutation. It isn't.

The structure is always the same:
1. Person A makes an argument.
2. Person B misrepresents Person A's argument.
3. Person B attacks the misrepresented version.
4. Person B declares that Person A's argument has been defeated.

What didn't happen: Person B engaged with what Person A actually said.

---

## Why It's So Effective

The straw man works because most audiences aren't tracking the original argument carefully enough to notice the substitution. Once the distorted version is in the room, it becomes the thing being discussed. Defending yourself requires stopping to say "that's not what I said" — which sounds defensive, which looks like you're on the back foot, which makes the straw man feel like it landed.

It's also effective because extreme versions of positions are genuinely easier to criticize. "Some investment in bike infrastructure" is a reasonable position that requires engagement. "Tear up all the roads" is an obviously bad policy that can be dismissed instantly. The straw man upgrades the difficulty of the attack by downgrading the strength of the position.

Psychologist Jonathan Haidt's research on motivated reasoning helps explain why it's so common: people start from a conclusion they want to reach and work backward to construct justifications. If you want to defeat a position, the easiest path is to fight the weakest version of it. The straw man is what motivated reasoning looks like in rhetoric.

---

## How to Spot It

The markers of a straw man:

**Exaggeration.** Your position gets intensified. "Regulate social media" becomes "ban free speech." "Reform the police" becomes "eliminate all law enforcement." The upgrade is the tell.

**Oversimplification.** A nuanced position gets reduced to its most blunt form. "We should weigh long-term environmental costs" becomes "you want to destroy the economy."

**Missing the qualifications.** You said *some*, *under certain conditions*, *as one part of a broader approach*. The straw man drops every qualifier and argues against the absolute version.

**The "so you're saying" move.** This is often where it happens explicitly — "so what you're really saying is..." followed by something you didn't say. If you hear those words, pay attention to what follows.

---

## What It Costs

The obvious victim is accuracy — debates conducted through straw men don't produce good decisions because no one is engaging with the real arguments.

But the subtler cost is to the person who uses it. Straw-manning is a way of avoiding the actual challenge. If Marcus's bike lane argument had real weaknesses — if the infrastructure costs were actually higher than he claimed, or if the European comparisons didn't apply locally — those weaknesses would never get examined through a straw man response. The real argument never got tested.

This is why people who habitually use the straw man tend to have fragile positions. They've never had to defend what they actually believe.

---

## What To Do About It

When you're on the receiving end, the response is direct: name the substitution without getting defensive.

"That's not what I argued. My actual claim was [restate clearly]. Can you engage with that?"

This is uncomfortable because it interrupts the momentum of the debate. Do it anyway. If you let the misrepresentation stand and argue against it, you're implicitly accepting that it's your position.

When you notice yourself doing it — simplifying someone's view before criticizing it, adding extremity they didn't claim — stop. Ask yourself: is this what they actually said? Could they object that you're misrepresenting them? If yes, go back to the original argument and engage with it.

The straw man is a shortcut to winning. Winning isn't the point. Getting it right is.

---

Marcus interrupted: "I didn't say anything about tearing up roads. I said the city should add protected bike lanes on four specific corridors. Can we talk about that?"

The councilman pivoted to a different objection. A weaker one.

That was progress.
